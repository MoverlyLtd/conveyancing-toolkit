# Building Regulations — Conveyancing Guidance

Help conveyancers assess and resolve building regulations issues found
during the transaction. The most common issue is missing completion
certificates for historical building work.

## Critical Rule: The "Before Any Approach" Principle

**⚠️ This is the single most important fact in building regulations
conveyancing — and the one most frequently missed.**

- Once anyone contacts the local authority about unauthorised building
  work, indemnity insurance becomes **unavailable** for that work
- This includes informal enquiries, phone calls, or "just checking"
- The seller, buyer, agent, or conveyancer making the contact — any
  contact invalidates cover
- **Always decide on strategy (regularisation vs indemnity) BEFORE any
  approach to building control**
- If the client has already contacted the local authority, indemnity is
  off the table — regularisation is the only route

## Missing Completion Certificate — Decision Process

Follow the decision tree in `references/decision-tree.md` for the full
flowchart. Summary:

### Step 1: Identify the work

Establish from TA6 replies, survey, or title what building work has been
done. Common triggers:
- Loft conversion (most common issue)
- Extension or conservatory
- Structural wall removal
- Replacement windows/doors
- Rewiring or new circuits
- Boiler replacement
- Chimney removal or structural alterations

### Step 2: When was the work done?

- **Pre-1985**: Building regulations approval was not required for most
  domestic work before the Building Act 1984 came into force (Nov 1985).
  Generally not an issue unless structural concerns exist.
- **1985–1999**: Regulations applied but enforcement was less rigorous.
  Many lenders accept indemnity insurance for work of this age.
- **2000 onwards**: Full compliance expected. Lenders increasingly require
  completion certificates. Part P (electrical) introduced 2005.

### Step 3: Was a competent person scheme used?

If the work was self-certified under a competent person scheme, a
certificate should exist — search at **competentperson.co.uk**. Schemes:

| Scheme | Covers |
|--------|--------|
| FENSA | Replacement windows and doors |
| CERTASS | Replacement windows and doors |
| NAPIT | Electrical installations |
| NICEIC | Electrical installations |
| ELECSA | Electrical installations |
| Gas Safe | Gas appliance installation |
| HETAS | Solid fuel appliances |

If a certificate exists, it is equivalent to a completion certificate — no
further action needed.

### Step 4: Has anyone approached the local authority?

- **Yes** → Indemnity insurance is unavailable. Proceed to regularisation.
- **No** → Both options remain open. Choose strategy based on
  circumstances.

### Step 5: Choose resolution route

#### Option A: Regularisation

Apply to the local authority for retrospective building regulations
approval.

- **No time limit** on applications — can regularise work of any age
- **Cost**: typically £400–800 depending on scope of work
- **Process**: physical inspection by building control surveyor
- **Timescale**: 4–8 weeks typically
- **Outcome**: completion certificate issued if work passes
- **Preferred by lenders** — provides actual confirmation of compliance
- **Risk**: if work fails inspection, remedial work required at seller's
  cost

#### Option B: Indemnity insurance

One-off insurance policy covering enforcement action risk.

- **MUST be obtained BEFORE any contact with local authority** — any
  approach invalidates cover
- **Cost**: typically £50–200 (one-off premium, no renewal)
- **Covers**: enforcement action risk and diminution in value
- **Does NOT cover**: actual defects in the work itself
- **Not all lenders accept this** — check UK Finance Lender's Handbook
  Part 2 for the specific lender
- **More acceptable for**: older work (pre-2000), minor work, work that
  has been in place for many years without issue
- **Less acceptable for**: recent work, fire safety non-compliance (Part
  B), structural work

## Building Regulations Parts — Key References

Only include Parts relevant to the work identified:

| Part | Subject | Key conveyancing context |
|------|---------|------------------------|
| **A** | Structure | Structural wall removal, extensions, loft conversions. Structural engineer's calculations typically required. |
| **B** | Fire safety | **Critical for loft conversions**: fire doors to habitable rooms, escape windows, interconnected smoke alarms, 30-min fire resistance to new floor, protected stairway. Part B non-compliance is taken most seriously by lenders. |
| **E** | Sound insulation | Party wall sound insulation in conversions. Relevant for loft conversions in semi-detached/terraced properties. |
| **K** | Protection from falling | Stairs, balustrades, guarding in loft conversions. Minimum balustrade height 900mm (stairs) / 1100mm (landings). |
| **L** | Conservation of fuel & power | Insulation requirements for extensions, loft conversions, replacement windows. U-values must meet current standards at time of work. |
| **M** | Access | Ground floor access requirements. Relevant for extensions and new-build. |
| **P** | Electrical safety | Notifiable work: new circuits, consumer unit replacement, bathroom/outdoor electrical work. Introduced 2005. Self-certification via NAPIT/NICEIC/ELECSA or building notice required. |

## Common Scenarios

### Loft conversion without completion certificate

The most common building regulations issue. Almost always requires
multiple Parts: A (structure), B (fire safety), K (stairs/guarding), L
(insulation), and possibly P (electrics).

- **Fire safety is the critical concern** — lenders treat Part B
  non-compliance most seriously
- Check for: fire doors, protected stairway, escape window to loft room,
  smoke detection, 30-min fire resistance
- If pre-2000 and no approach made: indemnity often acceptable
- If post-2000 or fire safety concerns: regularisation strongly preferred

### Extension without sign-off

Parts A (structure), L (insulation), possibly B (fire safety if inner
room created), P (electrics).

- Foundations, damp-proofing, structural integrity are key inspection
  points
- If visible and longstanding (10+ years), indemnity usually available

### Replacement windows not FENSA/CERTASS registered

Part L (thermal performance), Part N (glazing safety — now incorporated
into Part K).

- Search competentperson.co.uk first
- Very common issue — indemnity readily available and widely accepted
- Low risk — most lenders accept indemnity for this

### Boiler replacement without Gas Safe certificate

- Gas Safe certificate is a legal requirement (Gas Safety Regulations
  1998)
- Search Gas Safe register at gassaferegister.co.uk
- If no certificate: obtain retrospective inspection from Gas Safe
  registered engineer
- Indemnity insurance is NOT appropriate for gas safety — always seek
  retrospective certification or replacement

### Bathroom/kitchen rewiring

Part P (electrical safety) — applies to new circuits, not like-for-like
replacement of accessories.

- Notifiable if: new circuit, consumer unit change, work in
  bathroom/outdoor
- Not notifiable if: replacing switches, sockets, light fittings on
  existing circuits
- Self-certification via registered electrician (NAPIT/NICEIC/ELECSA)

### Removal of internal wall

Part A (structure) — may be load-bearing.

- Structural engineer's report should confirm whether wall was
  load-bearing
- If load-bearing: steel beam (RSJ) should be evident; building regs
  approval required
- If non-load-bearing: building regulations not required (but confirmation
  needed)

## Lender Requirements

- Check **UK Finance Lender's Handbook Part 2** for the specific lender
- Most mainstream lenders require completion certificate for work done
  **during or after** the current sale
- Many accept indemnity insurance for **older work** — but definitions of
  "older" vary
- **Part B (fire safety) non-compliance** is the area lenders are most
  strict about — some will not accept indemnity for fire safety issues
  regardless of age
- A detailed survey or specialist report may be required
- If regularisation is in progress, some lenders will complete subject to
  retention or undertaking

## Advising the Client

1. **Never advise the client to contact building control** before deciding
   on strategy — explain the indemnity insurance implications clearly
2. If the seller has already approached the local authority, note this
   changes available options
3. Cost of regularisation vs indemnity should be presented as a commercial
   decision — but always flag where the lender's position constrains the
   choice
4. For fire safety issues (especially loft conversions), strongly
   recommend regularisation over indemnity regardless of age
5. Where indemnity is the chosen route, policy should be in the name of
   the buyer and noted against the title for successors in title

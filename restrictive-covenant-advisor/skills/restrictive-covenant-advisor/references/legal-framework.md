# Legal Framework — Restrictive Covenants

Key statutory provisions and case law for restrictive covenants in England and Wales.

## Statutory Provisions

### s84 Law of Property Act 1925 — Modification and Discharge

The Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) has power to discharge or modify restrictive covenants on application by any person interested in the land.

**Grounds for discharge/modification:**

**(a) Obsolete** — The restriction ought to be deemed obsolete by reason of changes in the character of the property or the neighbourhood or other circumstances.

**(aa) Impedes reasonable use** — The continued existence of the restriction would impede some reasonable use of the land for public or private purposes AND either:
- The restriction does not secure any practical benefits of substantial value or advantage to the beneficiary; OR
- The restriction is contrary to the public interest;
AND in either case money would be adequate compensation for the loss or disadvantage (if any).

**(b) Agreement** — The persons of full age and capacity entitled to the benefit of the restriction have agreed, expressly or by implication, by their acts or omissions, to the restriction being discharged or modified.

**(c) No injury** — The proposed discharge or modification would not injure the persons entitled to the benefit of the restriction.

**Tribunal powers:**
- Full discharge
- Modification (partial relaxation)
- Award compensation to persons entitled to the benefit
- Impose conditions on discharge/modification

**Practice points:**
- Application to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber), not the county court
- Applicant must serve notice on all persons entitled to the benefit
- If no objection → often dealt with on paper
- If objection → hearing (costs risk)
- Tribunal fee: currently £880 (standard track)

### s78 Law of Property Act 1925 — Annexation of Benefit

"A covenant relating to any land of the covenantee shall be deemed to be made with the covenantee and his successors in title and the persons deriving title under him or them, and shall have effect as if such successors and other persons were expressed."

**Effect after *Federated Homes*:** s78 automatically annexes the benefit of a restrictive covenant to the land of the covenantee, unless a contrary intention is expressed. This means:
- No express words of annexation needed in the covenant
- The benefit runs with the land automatically
- Each successive owner of the benefiting land can enforce
- Only excluded if the covenant expressly negates statutory annexation

### s56 Law of Property Act 1925 — Third Party Rights

"A person may take an immediate or other interest in land or other property, or the benefit of any condition, right of entry, covenant or agreement over or respecting land or other property, although he may not be named as a party to the conveyance or other instrument."

**Effect:**
- Allows enforcement by persons not party to the original deed
- Limited scope: the person must be identifiable and in existence at the date of the covenant
- Less important since the Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999, but still relevant for pre-2000 covenants

## Key Case Law

### Tulk v Moxhay [1848] 2 Ph 774

**The foundation of restrictive covenant enforcement in equity.**

- Facts: Covenant to maintain Leicester Square as a garden; land sold to Moxhay who proposed to build
- Held: Equity would enforce the covenant against a subsequent purchaser who took with notice
- Principle: A restrictive covenant is enforceable against successors in title who take the land with notice of the covenant
- In registered land: entry on the charges register provides notice

### Federated Homes Ltd v Mill Lodge Properties Ltd [1980] 1 WLR 594

**The leading case on statutory annexation under s78 LPA 1925.**

- Facts: Covenant restricting number of dwellings; benefit not expressly annexed
- Held: s78 LPA 1925 automatically annexes the benefit of a covenant to the land of the covenantee
- Effect: Overturned the previous requirement for express annexation
- Limitation: Does not apply if a contrary intention is expressed in the covenant
- Practical impact: Most post-1925 covenants have the benefit automatically annexed

### Elliston v Reacher [1908] 2 Ch 374

**Building scheme requirements.**

Conditions for a building scheme (allowing mutual enforcement between plot owners):
1. Both plaintiff and defendant derive title from a common vendor
2. The common vendor laid out the estate in plots before selling
3. The restrictions were intended to benefit all plots in the scheme
4. The purchasers bought their plots on the understanding the restrictions benefited all plots

If a building scheme exists → every owner within the scheme can enforce against every other owner.

### Halsall v Brizell [1957] Ch 169

**The benefit and burden principle (relevant to positive covenants).**

- Principle: A person who takes the benefit of a deed must also accept its burdens
- Example: If an estate road is maintained under mutual covenant, an owner who uses the road must contribute to maintenance
- Limited application: only applies where the benefit and burden are directly related

### Wrotham Park Estate Co v Parkside Homes [1974] 1 WLR 798

**Damages in lieu of injunction for covenant breach.**

- Held: Where an injunction would be oppressive (e.g., demolishing completed houses), the court may award damages instead
- Measure of damages: a reasonable sum for the "release" of the covenant — what the parties would have negotiated
- Practical impact: breach of covenant doesn't always mean demolition; damages may be the remedy

## Land Registration

### Registered land (most properties in England and Wales)

- Restrictive covenants appear on the **charges register** (Section C) of the burdened title
- The covenant text or a reference to the instrument is included
- The benefiting title may have a notice on its **property register** noting the benefit
- Registration constitutes notice — no "bona fide purchaser without notice" defence

### Checking the register

1. Read the charges register entries carefully — they may summarise or reference the full deed
2. If the entry says "contained in a Transfer/Conveyance dated [date]" → obtain a copy of that document from Land Registry for the full wording
3. Check the property register of neighbouring titles to identify who claims the benefit

# Enforceability Assessment

Systematic checklist for determining whether a restrictive covenant is still enforceable.

## Core Principle

A restrictive covenant runs with the land in equity (*Tulk v Moxhay* [1848]) if:
1. It is negative in substance (restricts rather than requires)
2. It was made for the benefit of the covenantee's land
3. It touches and concerns that land
4. The burden was intended to bind successors
5. The current owner had notice (registered land: entry on charges register satisfies this)

## Checklist

### 1. Does the beneficiary still own benefiting land?

- A covenant is only enforceable by someone who owns land capable of benefiting
- If the original covenantee sold all their nearby land, enforcement right may be lost
- Check: who owns the land that was intended to benefit? Is it still in a position to benefit?
- **If no identifiable beneficiary with benefiting land exists → covenant likely unenforceable**

### 2. Has the benefit been properly transmitted?

The benefit must reach the current owner of the benefiting land by one of:

| Method | How it works | Key authority |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| **Annexation** | Benefit attached to the land automatically | s78 LPA 1925; *Federated Homes v Mill Lodge* [1980] |
| **Assignment** | Expressly assigned with each transfer | Must be contemporaneous with transfer |
| **Building scheme** | Mutual covenants in a development | *Elliston v Reacher* [1908] conditions |

**s78 LPA 1925 effect:** Since *Federated Homes*, s78 is deemed to annex the benefit of a covenant to the land unless a contrary intention is expressed. This means most post-1925 covenants are automatically annexed.

**Contrary intention:** If the covenant is expressed as personal ("the vendor and his successors in title" only), s78 may not apply. Look for express exclusion of annexation.

### 3. Has there been a historic breach?

- A history of breach without enforcement **does not extinguish** the covenant legally
- But it significantly weakens it practically:
  - Insurers treat it as lower risk
  - A Tribunal is more likely to modify/discharge under ground (a) or (aa)
  - A beneficiary who tolerated past breaches has weaker equitable standing
- **Key question:** Have neighbouring properties breached the same covenant without consequence?
- Check: aerial photos, planning history, local knowledge

### 4. Has the character of the neighbourhood changed?

- If the area has fundamentally changed since the covenant was imposed (e.g., from rural to urban, residential to mixed), the covenant may be obsolete
- This is ground (a) for a s84 application — "deemed obsolete by reason of changes in character of the property or neighbourhood"
- Change alone doesn't extinguish — but it makes discharge/modification achievable

### 5. Is the covenant personal or proprietary?

- A **personal** covenant binds only the original parties — it doesn't run with the land
- Look for language like "the purchaser hereby covenants with [named individual]" without reference to successors or the land
- If the covenant is purely personal, it died with the original parties
- Most well-drafted covenants include "successors in title" and reference to the benefiting land

### 6. Is the covenant positive in substance?

- Only negative (restrictive) covenants run with the land in equity
- A covenant to "maintain a fence" or "contribute to road repairs" is **positive** — it cannot bind successors in equity
- Test: does compliance require spending money or taking action? If yes → likely positive → not enforceable against successors
- Exception: conditions in long leases can enforce positive covenants

## Enforceability Summary Matrix

| Factor | Strong enforcement | Weak enforcement |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Beneficiary with nearby land | Yes, identifiable | No identifiable beneficiary |
| Benefit annexed/assigned | Properly annexed per s78 | Personal only, no annexation |
| Breach history | No prior breach | Widespread breach, no enforcement |
| Neighbourhood character | Unchanged | Fundamentally different |
| Covenant nature | Clearly restrictive | Arguably positive |
| Age | Recent (post-2000) | Very old (pre-1925) but age alone ≠ unenforceable |

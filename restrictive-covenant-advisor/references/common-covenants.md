# Common Covenant Types — Practical Guidance

## 1. No Building or Alteration Without Consent

**The most common restrictive covenant in residential property.**

Typical wording: "Not to erect or permit to be erected any building or structure on the property nor to make any alteration to the external appearance of the property without the prior written consent of [beneficiary]."

**Practical issues:**
- Does "alteration" include internal works? Usually no, unless the wording is exceptionally broad
- Does "building" include garden structures (sheds, greenhouses)? Depends on wording — "building or structure" likely yes
- Consent clause: if consent exists but has not been sought, the breach is the failure to obtain consent, not the building itself
- If consent was sought and refused unreasonably → possible argument that covenant should not be enforced in equity, but not reliable
- Historic extensions built without consent: common — check whether insurance is available

**Typical advice:** If works are proposed, obtain indemnity insurance before starting. If works are already done, insure retrospectively (provided no approach has been made).

## 2. Single Dwelling Restriction

Typical wording: "Not to erect or permit on the property more than one private dwellinghouse" or "The property shall not be used other than as a single private dwellinghouse."

**Practical issues:**
- Conversion to flats → clear breach
- HMO (house in multiple occupation) → likely breach depending on wording
- Granny annex / garden studio with bedroom → grey area; self-contained = likely breach, ancillary room = possibly not
- Airbnb / short lets → may breach "single dwelling" or "private" element depending on scale
- This covenant type is commonly enforced by neighbours in residential areas

**Typical advice:** Material breaches (flat conversion, HMO) require insurance or discharge. Ancillary structures may be lower risk but should still be assessed.

## 3. No Business or Trade Use

Typical wording: "Not to use or permit the property to be used for any trade, business, or profession" or "for residential purposes only."

**Practical issues:**
- Working from home (desk job, no clients visiting) → usually not a breach; no impact on character
- Running a business with clients visiting → likely breach if regular/commercial in nature
- Childminding / home tutoring → grey area
- Online business with no physical impact → very low risk of enforcement
- Professional practice (solicitor, accountant) from home → breach if wording says "profession"

**Key test:** Does the use change the residential character of the property? If it would be indistinguishable from normal residential use, enforcement risk is minimal.

**Typical advice:** Invisible home-working is very low risk. Visible commercial use (signage, client traffic, deliveries) should be insured or covenant addressed.

## 4. No Further Building on the Land

Typical wording: "Not to erect any further buildings on the land hereby conveyed" or "Not to build on any part of the garden or open land."

**Practical issues:**
- Garden extensions → breach
- Conservatories → likely breach (permanent structure)
- Pergolas, gazebos → depends on wording; permanent structures more likely caught
- Garden offices → likely breach if "building" or "structure"
- Driveways, patios, hard landscaping → usually not "building"

**Typical advice:** Any significant structure requires assessment. If the beneficiary's land overlooks the garden, risk of enforcement is higher for visible structures.

## 5. Maintenance Obligations

Typical wording: "To keep the boundary fences and walls in good repair" or "To maintain the front garden in a neat and tidy condition."

**⚠️ These are usually POSITIVE covenants.** They require the owner to do something (spend money, take action), not refrain from something.

**Legal effect:**
- Positive covenants do NOT run with freehold land in equity
- They bind only the original covenantor, not successors
- Exception: can be enforced in long leasehold estates
- Exception: may be enforceable via an indemnity chain (common but often broken)
- Exception: mutual benefit and burden (*Halsall v Brizell* [1957]) — if you take the benefit, you accept the burden

**Typical advice:** If the client is a successor in title to the original covenantor and the property is freehold, a positive covenant is generally not enforceable against them. But check for indemnity chains and the benefit/burden doctrine.

## 6. Height Restrictions

Typical wording: "Not to erect any building exceeding [X] metres in height" or "No structure on the property shall exceed the ridge height of the existing dwellinghouse."

**Practical issues:**
- Loft conversions that raise the ridge line → may breach
- Two-storey extensions on a bungalow plot → likely breach
- Roof-mounted solar panels → usually not a "building" but check wording
- Measurement point: ground level, eaves, ridge? Ambiguity in wording can be exploited

**Typical advice:** Get precise measurements. If a proposed development exceeds the limit, insure or seek discharge. If it's marginal, professional measurement and a pragmatic view may suffice.

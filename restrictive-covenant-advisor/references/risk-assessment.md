# Risk Assessment Framework

Structured approach to assessing the risk of enforcement of a restrictive covenant.

## Risk Factors

Score each factor to build an overall risk picture.

### 1. Age of Covenant

| Age | Risk indication | Notes |
|-----|----------------|-------|
| Pre-1926 | Lower (but not zero) | May predate statutory annexation; check if benefit properly passed |
| 1926–1970 | Moderate | s78 LPA 1925 applies; annexation likely automatic per *Federated Homes* |
| 1970–2000 | Moderate–higher | More recent, more likely actively monitored |
| Post-2000 | Higher | Recent covenant, likely still relevant, beneficiary probably engaged |

**⚠️ Age alone does NOT extinguish a covenant.** A 200-year-old covenant is still legally enforceable if the conditions are met. Age is a risk factor, not a legal defence.

### 2. History of Enforcement

| History | Risk level |
|---------|-----------|
| Beneficiary has actively enforced against others | High |
| No known enforcement history | Lower |
| Widespread breach by neighbours without consequence | Significantly lower |
| Beneficiary obtained injunction in past | Very high |

Check: planning records, local searches, title documents of neighbouring properties.

### 3. Nature of Proposed Breach

| Breach type | Risk level | Examples |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| Visible physical change | High | Extension, new building, demolition, significant external alteration |
| Change of use (visible) | High | Residential to commercial, single to multiple occupation |
| Change of use (invisible) | Lower | Home office use, occasional business calls |
| Minor internal alteration | Low | Internal reconfiguration, updated bathroom, new kitchen |
| Trivial/technical | Very low | Minor variation from original specification |

**Key principle:** The more visible and material the breach, the higher the risk. A neighbour who can see the breach from their property is more likely to object.

### 4. Proximity of Beneficiary's Land

| Proximity | Risk level |
|-----------|-----------|
| Immediately adjacent (shares boundary) | High |
| Same street or development | Moderate–high |
| Same neighbourhood but not adjacent | Moderate |
| Different area entirely | Lower |
| Beneficiary's land cannot be identified | Lowest |

**Rationale:** Proximate beneficiaries are more likely to notice a breach and have greater practical interest in enforcement.

### 5. Identifiability of Beneficiary

| Status | Risk level |
|--------|-----------|
| Named individual or company, still owns benefiting land | Higher |
| Original developer, still active in the area | Higher |
| Original developer, dissolved/defunct | Lower |
| Management company or residents' association | Higher (organised enforcement) |
| Cannot identify any current beneficiary | Lowest |

### 6. Nature of Benefiting Land

| Situation | Risk level |
|-----------|-----------|
| Beneficiary's land directly overlooks or adjoins | High |
| Beneficiary's land would be materially affected (noise, traffic, visual impact) | High |
| Beneficiary's land is distant/would not be affected | Lower |
| No land capable of benefiting can be identified | Lowest |

### 7. Building Scheme / Development Covenants

If the property is part of a planned development where mutual covenants were imposed:
- Every plot owner can enforce against every other → **multiple potential enforcers**
- Higher risk because any neighbour in the scheme can object
- Check: is there a building scheme? (*Elliston v Reacher* [1908] conditions)
- Common in: housing estates, planned developments, converted buildings

## Overall Risk Matrix

Combine the factors above:

| Overall risk | Profile | Recommended approach |
|-------------|---------|---------------------|
| **Very low** | Old covenant, no identifiable beneficiary, neighbourhood changed, widespread breach | Do nothing (or insurance for belt-and-braces) |
| **Low** | Old covenant, identifiable but distant beneficiary, no enforcement history, minor breach | Do nothing or low-cost insurance |
| **Moderate** | Identifiable beneficiary with nearby land, but no enforcement history, moderate breach | Indemnity insurance (if no approach made) |
| **High** | Proximate beneficiary, recent covenant, material/visible breach | Insurance if available; otherwise Tribunal or release |
| **Very high** | Active beneficiary, recent covenant, major development, or complaint received | Tribunal application or negotiate release; insurance likely unavailable or won't cover |

## Reporting to Client

When reporting a restrictive covenant assessment:

1. State the covenant in plain English
2. Identify who benefits and whether they can still enforce
3. Classify the risk level with reasoning
4. Present the available options (with costs and timelines)
5. **Explicitly warn about the no-approach rule before the client takes any action**
6. Recommend a course of action
7. If a lender is involved, state what the lender is likely to require

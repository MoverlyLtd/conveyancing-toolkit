# Options for Dealing with Restrictive Covenants

Four options, in order of cost and complexity.

## Option A: Do Nothing

**When appropriate:**
- Breach is minor or technical (e.g., small internal alteration where covenant restricts "structural alteration")
- Covenant is clearly obsolete — no identifiable beneficiary, neighbourhood completely changed
- No lender involved, or lender does not require action
- Client accepts the residual risk

**Risk:** If a beneficiary later enforces, the buyer has no protection. Appropriate only where risk is genuinely minimal.

**Lender impact:** Most lenders will not accept "do nothing" for a known material breach. May need to combine with insurance or Tribunal route.

## Option B: Restrictive Covenant Indemnity Insurance

The most common practical solution. A one-off policy that protects against enforcement action.

### Eligibility — CRITICAL PREREQUISITES

**The policy is VOID or UNAVAILABLE if:**

1. ❌ The covenant beneficiary has been approached (by anyone — client, agent, solicitor, planning consultant)
2. ❌ A complaint or notice about the breach has been received
3. ❌ The beneficiary has indicated they may enforce
4. ❌ There is current or threatened litigation

**"Approached" includes:**
- Letters to the beneficiary (even friendly/informal)
- Phone calls
- Conversations over the garden fence
- Emails
- Planning application consultations where the beneficiary is a statutory consultee
- Instructing an agent who then contacts the beneficiary
- Social media messages

**The "no approach" rule is absolute.** Even a well-intentioned enquiry kills the insurance route.

### What the policy covers

- Legal costs of defending enforcement action (injunction proceedings, damages claims)
- Damages or compensation ordered by a court
- Diminution in property value caused by enforcement
- Typically covers the current owner AND successors in title

### What the policy does NOT cover

- ❌ Cost of remediation (undoing the breach — e.g., demolishing an extension)
- ❌ Cost of complying with the covenant going forward
- ❌ Losses arising from voluntary compliance
- ❌ Anything that happens after the beneficiary has been approached

### Practical details

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Premium** | One-off, typically £50–300 depending on risk and property value |
| **Cover level** | Usually matches property value; can be increased |
| **Duration** | Perpetual — no renewal needed |
| **Availability** | Specialist legal indemnity insurers via broker; some conveyancers have block policies with pre-agreed rates |
| **Speed** | Can be arranged in 1–3 working days |
| **Lender acceptance** | Most lenders accept as alternative to discharge; policy must note lender's interest |

### When insurance is the right choice

- Breach is known or proposed but beneficiary has NOT been approached
- No complaint or enforcement history
- Risk is moderate — breach is real but enforcement unlikely
- Client wants certainty at low cost
- Lender requires protection but doesn't insist on discharge

### When insurance is NOT available or suitable

- Beneficiary has been contacted → insurance unavailable
- Complaint received → insurance unavailable
- Very high risk breach (e.g., large commercial development on land with clear residential-only covenant and active beneficiary) → insurer may decline or price prohibitively
- Lender specifically requires discharge, not insurance

## Option C: Upper Tribunal Application (s84 LPA 1925)

Apply to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) for modification or discharge of the covenant.

### Statutory grounds

| Ground | Description | When to use |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **(a) Obsolete** | Covenant deemed obsolete by reason of changes in character of the property or neighbourhood | Area has fundamentally changed; covenant serves no practical purpose |
| **(aa) Impedes reasonable use** | Covenant impedes some reasonable use of the land AND either provides no practical benefit of substantial value, OR is contrary to public interest, AND money would adequately compensate | Most common ground; proposed development/use is reasonable and covenant benefit is marginal |
| **(b) Agreement** | Persons entitled to the benefit have agreed (expressly or impliedly) | Beneficiaries consent but want formal discharge |
| **(c) No injury** | Proposed discharge/modification would not injure the persons entitled to the benefit | Breach causes no actual harm to benefiting land |

### Process

1. File application with Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)
2. Serve notice on all persons with benefit of the covenant
3. If objections → hearing (oral or written representations)
4. Tribunal may discharge, modify, or refuse
5. If discharged/modified → apply to Land Registry to update register

### Cost and timeline

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Legal fees** | £1,000–5,000+ depending on complexity and whether contested |
| **Tribunal fee** | Currently £880 (standard track) |
| **Timeline** | 6–18 months (faster if uncontested) |
| **Compensation** | Tribunal may order applicant to pay compensation to beneficiary |

### When the Tribunal route is best

- Covenant is clearly obsolete — strong case on ground (a)
- Insurance is unavailable (beneficiary has been approached)
- Insurer declines to cover the risk
- Lender requires formal discharge
- Client intends significant development and wants clean title

### Risks

- Application may fail → costs wasted
- Contested hearings are expensive
- Compensation may be ordered
- The application itself alerts the beneficiary (which also kills future insurance)

## Option D: Negotiate Release with Beneficiary

Direct negotiation to obtain a formal release deed.

### Process

1. Identify the covenant beneficiary (Land Registry search)
2. Approach them to negotiate a release
3. Agree terms (often a payment/premium is required)
4. Execute a deed of release
5. Apply to Land Registry to remove/modify the entry

### ⚠️ Critical warning

**Approaching the beneficiary permanently closes the insurance option.** This is irreversible. The decision to negotiate must be made with full understanding that if negotiation fails, the only remaining options are the Tribunal or living with the covenant.

### When negotiation is appropriate

- Beneficiary is clearly identified and accessible
- Covenant is relatively minor and release likely to be agreed
- Client is willing to pay a premium for the release
- Insurance is already unavailable (beneficiary previously approached)
- Client prefers a clean title over an insurance policy

### Cost

- Variable — some beneficiaries release for free, others demand significant sums
- Legal costs for both sides (the beneficiary may expect their legal costs to be paid)
- Typical range: £500–10,000+ depending on negotiation

### Risks

- Beneficiary refuses to release → stuck with Tribunal or doing nothing
- Beneficiary demands unreasonable premium
- Negotiation can be slow and uncertain
- Alerting the beneficiary may prompt them to actively enforce

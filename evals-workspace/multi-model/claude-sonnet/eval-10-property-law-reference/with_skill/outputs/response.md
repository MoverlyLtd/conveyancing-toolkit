# Restrictive covenants

A restrictive covenant is a promise by one party not to do something on their land for the benefit of adjoining land. It is a legal obligation that "runs with the land" – meaning it applies to current and future owners.

## What are restrictive covenants?

Restrictive covenants are negative promises that restrict how land can be used. They might prevent certain activities such as:

- building on part of the property
- using the property for business purposes
- keeping certain animals
- parking vehicles in certain areas
- making structural alterations

Restrictive covenants are different from positive covenants, which require someone to do something (like maintain a fence or contribute to shared costs).

## How do restrictive covenants work?

For a restrictive covenant to be enforceable:

- it must have been created properly (usually in a deed)
- it must benefit adjoining land (not just be a personal promise)
- the person enforcing it must own land that benefits from the restriction
- the covenant must "touch and concern" the land (relate to its use or value)

## Common examples

Typical restrictive covenants include restrictions on:

- **Use** - for example, residential use only, no business use
- **Building** - restrictions on extensions, outbuildings, or development
- **Appearance** - requirements to maintain the character of an area
- **Activities** - no keeping of certain animals, vehicles, or materials

## How to find out about covenants

Restrictive covenants should be registered at HM Land Registry and appear on:

- the charges register of the title
- filed plans and documents

When buying property, your conveyancer should:
- check the title for any restrictive covenants
- explain what they mean for your intended use
- advise on enforceability and insurance options

## Enforcement

A restrictive covenant can be enforced by:

- the person or organisation that has the benefit of the covenant
- successors in title to that benefited land
- sometimes by residents' associations or management companies

Remedies for breach can include:
- injunctions to stop the breach
- damages (though these may be limited)
- sometimes an account of profits

## Removing or modifying covenants

If a restrictive covenant is problematic, you may be able to:

- **Negotiate a release** - pay the beneficiary to remove or modify it
- **Apply to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)** - if the covenant is obsolete, doesn't benefit anyone, or would impede reasonable development
- **Take out insurance** - if the risk of enforcement is low

## Insurance for restrictive covenants

Restrictive covenant insurance can protect against:
- enforcement action by unknown beneficiaries
- discovery of breaches by previous owners
- costs of defending enforcement action

This is often more cost-effective than trying to remove old covenants.

## Professional advice

Given the complexity of covenant law and the potential consequences of breach, it's essential to:

- have all covenants properly explained by your conveyancer
- understand how they affect your intended use of the property
- consider insurance or removal options where appropriate
- take legal advice before making any changes that might breach a covenant

The law on restrictive covenants is complex and depends heavily on the specific wording and circumstances of each case.

## Introduction

This guide covers how third party interests can be protected on the register through the entry of notices and restrictions.

The system of registered land provides for interests that affect registered land to be protected by entry on the register. The main methods of protection are:

- registration with its own title
- entry of a notice
- entry of a restriction
- an overriding interest that takes effect without registration

### Notices

A notice is an entry in the charges register that gives notice of the fact that a person claims to be entitled to an interest affecting the estate.

The entry of a notice does not guarantee that the interest is valid, but it ensures that a person who acquires the estate will take it subject to the interest if it is valid.

### Restrictive covenants

Restrictive covenants are commonly protected by the entry of a notice. The notice will usually refer to a deed or document that contains the full terms of the covenant.

A restrictive covenant notice might appear as:

"(Date) Restrictive covenant contained in a Transfer dated [date] made between (parties) as set out in the schedule of restrictive covenants hereto."

or

"Restrictive covenants contained in a Conveyance dated [date] and made between [parties]."

The Land Registry will file a copy of the deed containing the covenants, which can be inspected to see the full terms.

### Enforcement

The ability to enforce a restrictive covenant depends on whether the person seeking to enforce it has the benefit of the covenant. This usually requires:

- ownership of land that benefits from the covenant
- that the covenant touches and concerns that land
- that there was an intention for the covenant to run with the land

### Practical considerations

When buying property, your conveyancer should:

- identify all restrictive covenants affecting the property
- explain their practical effect on your intended use
- advise on the risk of enforcement
- consider whether insurance is advisable

The age and nature of covenants, and whether there are obvious beneficiaries, will affect the enforcement risk.

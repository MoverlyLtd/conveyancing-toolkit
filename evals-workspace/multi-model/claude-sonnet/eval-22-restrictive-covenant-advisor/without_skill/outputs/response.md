Based on the facts you've described, your client may have several viable options to proceed with the rear extension despite the restrictive covenant. Here's my analysis and recommendations:

## Primary Options:

### 1. **Acquiescence/Waiver Argument**
The neighbouring property's own extension in 2015 may constitute acquiescence to breaches of the covenant or waiver of their right to enforce it. Key considerations:
- Did they know about the covenant when building their extension?
- Does their extension breach the same covenant?
- Have they previously enforced this covenant against others?

### 2. **Application to Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) under s.84 Law of Property Act 1925**
Strong grounds appear available:
- **s.84(1)(aa)** - Covenant impedes reasonable use of land with no practical benefit to beneficiary
- **s.84(1)(c)** - Discharge/modification would not injure the beneficiary

The beneficiary's own breach significantly strengthens this application.

### 3. **Insurance Route**
Consider restrictive covenant indemnity insurance if:
- Risk of enforcement is low
- Cost-effective compared to formal discharge
- Lender accepts this approach

**Phase 1**: Immediate steps
- Obtain detailed plans of both properties showing respective extensions
- Research planning history to confirm dates and details
- Check Land Registry for any notices of discharge applications

**Phase 2**: Negotiation
- Approach the beneficiary directly requesting consent/deed of variation

- Negotiate reasonable terms (often modest payment)

**Phase 3**: Formal application (if negotiation fails)
- Upper Tribunal application with strong prospects given the circumstances
- Typical timescale: 6-9 months
- Costs: £3,000-£10,000 plus Tribunal fees

## Key Risks:
- Injunction risk if proceeding without consent (though lower given beneficiary's own breach)
- Costs exposure if Upper Tribunal application fails
- Delay to building timeline

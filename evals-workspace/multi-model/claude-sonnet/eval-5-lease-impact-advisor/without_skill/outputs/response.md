Yes, you should definitely be concerned about a 95-year lease, and you must advise your client about the significant implications. Here are the key issues:

## Mortgage Concerns
- Most lenders are reluctant to lend on leases under 85-90 years
- Some lenders won't lend at all on leases under 80 years
- Your client may face difficulties with future mortgages or remortgaging
- This affects both immediate purchase and future saleability

## Lease Extension Rights & Costs
- Under the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993, leaseholders can extend after owning for 2 years
- Marriage value becomes payable when the lease drops below 80 years
- At 95 years, marriage value isn't yet payable, but will be in 15 years
- Extension costs increase significantly as the lease shortens

## Diminishing Asset Value
- Property values typically start declining noticeably around 80-85 years
- The shorter the lease, the steeper the decline in value
- This affects your client's investment and equity

## Practical Advice
You should:
1. **Strongly recommend immediate lease extension** - it's much cheaper now than waiting
2. Obtain a specialist leasehold valuation to assess extension costs
3. Check the lease terms for ground rent escalation clauses
4. Ensure your client understands the urgency - every year of delay increases costs
5. Consider whether the purchase price reflects the lease length appropriately

## Professional Duty
Make sure you clearly explain these risks in writing and ensure your client makes an informed decision. Many buyers underestimate the impact of shorter leases.

This is a common scenario in residential conveyancing. Before discussing your options, I must address the most critical rule regarding restrictive covenants and insurance.

### ⚠️ The "No Approach" Rule
**Has anyone contacted or approached the person or entity who benefits from this covenant (the "beneficiary")?** This includes informal chats, letters, or phone calls by the client, an agent, or a previous owner.

If the beneficiary has been approached, **indemnity insurance is no longer available**. You must maintain total silence regarding the covenant if you wish to keep the insurance option open.

---

### 1. Assessment of the Breach
The covenant from 1965 prohibits "business use." In modern conveyancing, we distinguish between different types of business use:

*   **Invisible/Low Risk:** A home office with no clients visiting, no signage, and no increased traffic (like a desk job) is technically a breach, but it does not change the "residential character" of the property. The risk of enforcement is extremely low because it is not visible to neighbors or the beneficiary.
*   **Visible/High Risk:** Running a business with deliveries, clients, or signage would be a material breach and significantly higher risk.

Since your client’s use is "not visible," it falls into the lowest risk category.

### 2. Enforceability
Under the **s84 Law of Property Act 1925**, several factors might suggest this covenant is weakening:
*   **Acquiescence/Waiver:** If other neighbors have been working from home for years without the beneficiary taking action, this strengthens your client's position.
*   **Obsolete Character:** If the neighborhood has changed significantly since 1965, the covenant may be deemed obsolete under Ground (a) of s84.

### 3. Options for the Client

| Option | When to use | Estimated Cost | Timeline |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Do nothing** | Since the breach is invisible and technical, many clients choose this, especially if the beneficiary is no longer identifiable. | Nil | Immediate |
| **Indemnity insurance** | **Provided no approach has been made.** This provides peace of mind for the buyer and satisfies mortgage lenders. | **£50 – £300** (one-off premium) | Days |
| **Upper Tribunal (s84 LPA 1925)** | If the client wants the covenant legally removed. You would apply to the **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)**. | **£1,000 – £5,000+** | 6–18 months |
| **Negotiate release** | Only if the beneficiary is known and the insurance route is closed. | Variable (Premium + Legal fees) | Weeks–months |

### 4. Statutory Remedy: s84 Law of Property Act 1925
If your client needs to formally discharge or modify the covenant (for example, if they wanted to open a visible business later), they must apply to the **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)**. 

Under **s84 LPA 1925**, the Tribunal can discharge a covenant if:
*   **(a) Obsolete:** The character of the neighborhood has changed.
*   **(aa) Impedes reasonable use:** The restriction secures no practical benefit of substantial value.
*   **(b) Agreement:** The beneficiary agrees.
*   **(c) No injury:** The discharge will not injure the beneficiary.

### 5. Lender Requirements
If the property is being purchased with a mortgage, the lender will typically require **restrictive covenant indemnity insurance** to protect their security, even for a "low risk" invisible breach. Most lenders accept insurance as a standard solution as long as the policy limit covers the full value of the property and notes their interest.

### Summary Recommendation
If no approach has been made, the most practical route is **indemnity insurance**. It is fast, relatively inexpensive (£50–300), and resolves the issue for both the client and their lender. However, if the client is comfortable with the risk and the lender permits, they may choose to do nothing given the "invisible" nature of the home office.

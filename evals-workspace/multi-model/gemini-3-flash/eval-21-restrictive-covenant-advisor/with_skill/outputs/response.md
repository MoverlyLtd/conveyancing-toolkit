This includes the original developer, the person who sold the land in 1965, or any current neighbors who might have the right to enforce it. If any contact has been made (even an informal chat or a letter), **covenant indemnity insurance will likely be unavailable.** 

Assuming no approach has been made, here is an assessment based on the "Restrictive Covenant Advisor" framework.

### 1. Assessment of the Breach
The proposed use—a small, non-visible home office with no visiting clients—is a **technical breach** at most. 

In modern conveyancing practice, a covenant against "business or trade use" is primarily intended to protect the residential character of the neighborhood (preventing noise, traffic, and signage). Since your client’s activity is "invisible," the risk of enforcement is extremely low. Most courts and the Upper Tribunal do not consider a desk-based home office to be a breach that changes the residential character of a property.

### 2. Enforceability
*   **Date:** Since the covenant was created in 1965, it is subject to **s78 of the Law of Property Act 1925**. This means the benefit was likely automatically "annexed" to the neighboring land.
*   **Enforcement History:** The fact that it hasn't been enforced since 1965 (if other similar uses exist in the street) weakens the beneficiary's position, but does not legally extinguish the covenant.

### 3. Your Options

### 4. Indemnity Insurance Specifics
Indemnity insurance for a restrictive covenant of this age and nature is standard.
*   **Cost:** Typically a one-off premium between **£50 and £300**, depending on the property value and the specific insurer.
*   **Coverage:** It covers legal defense costs and any loss in property value if a beneficiary successfully enforces the covenant.
*   **Lender requirements:** Most lenders accept this as a solution for a covenant breach, provided the policy is for a "limit of indemnity" equal to the full market value of the house.

### Summary Advice
If the client wants the "safest" route for a future sale, they should take out a **restrictive covenant indemnity policy** now. They must ensure they **do not** contact any neighbors or the original vendor about the covenant before the policy is in place. If they are comfortable with a very low-risk profile, "doing nothing" is also a common and practical choice for this specific type of "invisible" breach.

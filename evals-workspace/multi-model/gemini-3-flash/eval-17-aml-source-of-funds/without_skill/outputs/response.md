To comply with the **Money Laundering Regulations 2017**, the **Legal Sector Affinity Group (LSAG) Guidance**, and **SRA Warning Notices**, you must adopt a risk-based approach. You are looking for two things: **Source of Funds (SoF)** (where the money for this specific transaction comes from) and **Source of Wealth (SoW)** (how the client accumulated their total net worth).

Here is the specific evidence required for each element of the £750,000 purchase.

---

### 1. Savings (£200,000)
For a sum of this size, simply seeing a current balance is insufficient. You must evidence the **accumulation** of these funds.

*   **Bank Statements:** Request the last **6 months of original bank statements** (or certified/PDF downloads) for the account where the money is held. You are looking for a gradual build-up of funds consistent with the client's known income.
*   **Evidence of Salary:** If the savings came from employment, you need the last **3 months of payslips** and the most recent **P60**. This correlates the deposits seen in the bank statements with their employer.
*   **Self-Employed/Business Owners:** If the client is self-employed, request the last **two years of SA302s** (Tax Calculations) and the corresponding **Tax Year Overviews** from HMRC to prove the income exists to support £200k in savings.
*   **Large One-off Deposits:** If the £200,000 was boosted by a specific event (e.g., a bonus, redundancy payment, or sale of shares), you need the specific paper trail for that event (e.g., a bonus letter or a dividend voucher).

### 2. Gift from Parents (£100,000)
Third-party funds are high-risk. You must perform due diligence on the **donors** (the parents) as if they were the clients themselves regarding the source of that specific £100,000.

*   **Identification (KYC):** Full AML ID checks on both parents (Passport/Driving Licence and Proof of Address).
*   **Signed Gift Letter:** A formal letter signed by the parents confirming:
    *   The amount of the gift (£100,000).
    *   That it is an unconditional gift with **no repayment required**.
    *   That they will acquire **no legal or beneficial interest** in the property.
    *   Their relationship to the buyer.
*   **Parents’ Source of Funds:** You must see the parents' bank statements showing the £100,000 sitting in their account.
*   **Parents’ Source of Wealth:** You must ask (and evidence) how the parents acquired that £100,000.
    *   *If from savings:* 6 months of their bank statements showing accumulation.
    *   *If from a house sale:* A copy of the Completion Statement from their solicitor.
    *   *If from a pension lump sum:* A pension statement or letter from the provider showing the drawdown.
    *   *If from an inheritance:* A letter from the executors or a Grant of Probate.
*   **Bankruptcy Search:** Most lenders require you to carry out a K16 bankruptcy search against the donors to ensure the gift isn't an attempt to defraud creditors.

### 3. Mortgage (£450,000)
While mortgage funds from a UK-regulated lender are considered lower risk, you still need to verify the legitimacy of the loan.

*   **The Mortgage Offer:** A full copy of the formal offer received directly from the lender (usually via the lender's portal or via the client).
*   **Check Consistency:** Ensure the mortgage amount on the offer matches the figures provided in the client’s initial instruction.
*   **Lender Identity:** Confirm the lender is a regulated financial institution (e.g., high street bank or specialist lender registered with the FCA). If it is a "private mortgage," you must treat the lender as a third-party donor and perform full SoW/SoF checks as you did for the parents.

### 4. Overarching Source of Wealth (SoW)
The SRA expects you to step back and ask: *"Does it make sense that this person has £750,000 to spend?"*

*   **The "Story":** Document a brief narrative of the client's career and financial history. (e.g., "The client is a Senior Manager at X Ltd earning £90k p.a. for the last 10 years, which explains the £200k savings.")
*   **Consistency Check:** If the client is 21 years old and has £200,000 in savings but no clear employment history, your risk level increases, and you must dig significantly deeper into the "Source of Wealth" (e.g., inheritance or trust funds).

### 5. Practical Compliance Steps
*   **Electronic Verification (eIDV):** Use an AML platform (like Thirdfort, Credas, or InfoTrack) to verify ID and, where possible, use Open Banking to analyze the £200k savings.
*   **The "Red Flag" Check:** Look for unusual activity, such as the £100k gift arriving from a business account instead of a personal account (this is a major red flag for tax evasion/money laundering).
*   **Audit Trail:** Ensure your file contains a "Source of Funds Verification" memo that summarizes your findings and explicitly states why you are satisfied that the funds are "clean."

**Warning:** Do not proceed to exchange until the Gift Letter and the Parents' SoW evidence are in your possession, as these are the most common areas for SRA audits to find "inadequate due diligence."

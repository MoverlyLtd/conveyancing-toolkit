As a Licensed Conveyancer governed by the **Council for Licensed Conveyancers (CLC)**, your obligations regarding client money are primarily set out in the **CLC Accounts Code** and the **CLC Code of Conduct**.

The overarching principle is that client money must be kept safe, held separately from the firm’s own money, and used only for the purpose for which it was intended.

Below is a detailed breakdown of your specific obligations:

---

### 1. The Definition of Client Money
You must first correctly identify **Client Money**. This is money held or received by you on behalf of a client, including:
*   Purchase deposits and completion funds.
*   Mortgage advances.
*   Money held for Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) or Land Transaction Tax (LTT).
*   Land Registry fees.
*   Money held as a stakeholder.

### 2. Separation of Funds (The "Golden Rule")
The most fundamental obligation is the strict separation of client and office money.
*   **The Client Account:** You must maintain at least one specific bank account used solely for client money. It must be held at a bank or building society in England or Wales and the account name must include the word **"Client"**.
*   **No Commingling:** You must not use the client account to hold your own funds (office money), except for a small amount to open the account or to cover bank charges.
*   **Immediate Deposit:** Any client money received must be paid into the Client Account **immediately** (usually defined as the same day or the next working day).

### 3. Withdrawals and Payments
You have a fiduciary duty to ensure client money is only moved for valid reasons.
*   **Authorization:** Money can only be withdrawn from the client account if it is to be paid to the client, or to a third party on the client’s instructions (e.g., an estate agent or the seller’s solicitor).
*   **Professional Fees:** You may only withdraw client money to pay your own firm’s fees if you have performed the work and have **already issued a bill of costs** or a notification of costs to the client.
*   **No "Debit Balances":** You must never pay out more money on behalf of a client than you actually hold for that specific client. This would effectively mean using "Client B's" money to fund "Client A's" transaction, which is a severe regulatory breach.

### 4. Accounting Records and Reconciliations
The CLC requires meticulous bookkeeping to ensure transparency and auditability.
*   **Double-Entry Bookkeeping:** You must maintain a system that shows every transaction for every client individually.
*   **Timeliness:** Transactions must be recorded in the accounting records within **two to three working days**.
*   **Monthly Reconciliations:** You must perform a formal reconciliation at least **once every calendar month**. This involves comparing:
    1.  The cash book balance.
    2.  The individual client ledger balances.
    3.  The bank statement balances.
*   Any discrepancies found during reconciliation must be investigated and corrected immediately.

### 5. Duty to Pay Interest
Under the CLC Accounts Code, you have a duty to account to the client for interest when it is **fair and reasonable** to do so.
*   **Interest Policy:** Your firm must have a written policy on the payment of interest, which must be shared with the client in the Terms of Engagement.
*   While you can set a "de minimis" limit (a minimum amount below which interest is not paid), this limit must be reasonable and transparent.

### 6. Dealing with Residual Balances
One of the most common CLC audit findings involves "residual balances"—small amounts of money left on a ledger after a matter has concluded.
*   **Timely Return:** You must return all client money as soon as there is no longer any reason to retain it.
*   **Reasonable Steps:** If you cannot find the client, you must take documented, "reasonable steps" to trace them.
*   **Unclaimed Money:** If balances remain for more than 12 months and you cannot locate the client, you must apply to the CLC for permission to pay the money to a registered charity. (For amounts under £50, there are simplified procedures, but you must still maintain a record).

### 7. The Annual Accountant’s Report (AR)
As a CLC-regulated professional, your firm must appoint an **Reporting Accountant** to examine your accounts annually.
*   The accountant checks for compliance with the CLC Accounts Code.
*   The **Accountant's Report** must be submitted to the CLC within **six months** of the end of your accounting period.
*   If the report is "qualified" (indicating breaches), you must provide an explanation and a plan for rectification to the CLC.

### 8. Transparency and the Code of Conduct
Beyond the technical accounting, the **CLC Code of Conduct** requires:
*   **Integrity:** Acting with honesty in all financial dealings.
*   **Disclosure:** Ensuring clients are fully aware of how their money is held and any commissions or referral fees you may be receiving or paying (which must not be taken from client money).
*   **Reporting Breaches:** If you discover a significant breach of the Accounts Code (e.g., a shortfall in the client account), you have a mandatory obligation to **self-report** to the CLC immediately.

### Summary Checklist for a Licensed Conveyancer:
1.  **Never** mix office and client funds.
2.  **Never** let a client ledger go into a deficit.
3.  **Always** issue a bill before transferring your fees.
4.  **Always** reconcile your bank accounts at least once a month.
5.  **Always** clear residual balances promptly after completion.

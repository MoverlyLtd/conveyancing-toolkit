In UK conveyancing, a loft conversion completed in 2019 without a Building Regulations Completion Certificate presents a significant issue. Because the work was not done under a Competent Person Scheme (which is rare for loft conversions anyway, as they usually require multi-disciplinary inspections), the lack of a certificate means there is no legal proof that the structure is safe, fire-compliant, or thermally efficient.

Here is a detailed breakdown of the implications and the available remedies.

---

### 1. The Implications

#### A. Enforcement Action by the Local Authority
Under the Building Act 1984, Local Authorities have two main routes for enforcement:
*   **Section 36 Notice:** The council can order the homeowner to remove or rectify work that contravenes regulations. Historically, this had a **12-month limit** from the date of completion. Since the work was done in 2019, the seller may argue they are "past the enforcement period."
*   **Injunctions:** Under Section 35, the council can apply for an injunction to stop the use of a dangerous structure. There is **no time limit** for this. While rare for residential lofts, it is a theoretical risk if the conversion is structurally unsound.

#### B. Mortgage and Lending Issues
This is the most common "real-world" problem.
*   **CML/BSA Handbook Compliance:** Most lenders (via the UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook) require the solicitor to ensure that all necessary permissions and certificates are in place for significant alterations. 
*   **Valuation:** A surveyor will likely value the property as a "two-bedroom house with storage in the loft" rather than a "three-bedroom house." If the buyer is paying a price based on the extra bedroom, the lender may down-value the property or refuse the mortgage entirely until a certificate is produced.

#### C. Safety and Insurance
*   **Fire Safety:** Loft conversions must have fire doors, interlinked smoke alarms, and a protected escape route. Without a certificate, there is no guarantee these exist.
*   **Structural Integrity:** Lofts require specific floor joist reinforcements. Standard ceiling joists are rarely strong enough to support a "habitable" room.
*   **Insurance:** If a fire occurs and starts in the loft, or if the roof collapses due to structural failure, the insurer may refuse to pay out because the work was unauthorized and did not meet safety standards.

#### D. Future Saleability
The problem does not go away. When the current buyer eventually sells, they will face the same scrutiny from the next buyer’s solicitor.

---

### 2. Remedies

There are three primary ways to resolve this during the conveyancing process:

#### Option 1: Building Regulations Indemnity Insurance (The "Quick Fix")
This is the most common solution used by solicitors to keep a chain moving.
*   **How it works:** The seller pays a one-off premium (usually £200–£500) for a policy that protects the buyer and lender against the costs of any future enforcement action by the council.
*   **Pros:** It is fast and relatively cheap.
*   **Cons:** **It does not prove the loft is safe.** It only covers the cost of legal defense or loss in value if the council enforces. Crucially, the policy becomes **void** if the buyer contacts the council to ask about the conversion.
*   **Lender view:** Most lenders accept this for work over 12 months old, but it does not solve the "valuation" issue if the surveyor refuses to count it as a bedroom.

#### Option 2: Regularisation Certificate (The "Correct Fix")
The seller applies retrospectively to the Local Authority for a "Regularisation Certificate."
*   **How it works:** A building control officer visits the property. They will likely require "opening up" works (e.g., cutting holes in plasterboard to check joists, insulation, and fire-stopping).
*   **Pros:** If granted, the loft is legally recognized as a habitable room, maximizing the property value.
*   **Cons:** It is time-consuming (weeks or months). If the work is substandard, the council will issue a list of expensive remedial works. If the seller refuses to do them, the council is now "on notice" of the breach, making indemnity insurance (Option 1) impossible to obtain.

#### Option 3: Surveyor’s Structural Report
If the buyer is cash-rich or the lender is flexible, the buyer can commission a structural engineer or a specialist surveyor to inspect the loft specifically.
*   **How it works:** The engineer checks joist spans, head height, and stairs. 
*   **Pros:** Provides peace of mind regarding safety without involving the council.
*   **Cons:** It doesn't provide legal "completion," and it doesn't satisfy a strict lender.

#### Option 4: Price Reduction
The buyer negotiates a reduction in the purchase price to reflect:
1.  The cost of obtaining a Regularisation Certificate.
2.  The cost of any potential remedial works.
3.  The "diminution in value" (the difference in price between a house with a legal bedroom and a house with a "storage loft").

---

### Summary Checklist for a Buyer
1.  **Check the Survey:** Did the RICS surveyor flag the loft as "non-compliant"?
2.  **Ask the Lender:** Will the lender accept Indemnity Insurance, or do they require a Completion Certificate?
3.  **Check Fire Safety:** At a minimum, ensure there are mains-powered smoke alarms and that the loft door is a fire door.
4.  **Confirm Planning Permission:** Building Regs and Planning Permission are different. Even if it doesn't have Building Regs, did it need Planning Permission (e.g., if it’s in a Conservation Area or has a large dormer)? If it lacks Planning Permission too, that is a separate, additional risk.

**Recommendation:** For a conversion as recent as 2019, a buyer should be very cautious. If the mortgage lender allows, **Indemnity Insurance** is the path of least resistance, but a **price reduction** is often justified because the property is technically being sold with a defect.

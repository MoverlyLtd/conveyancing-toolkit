As an estate agent listing a property with **68 years remaining**, you are dealing with a "short lease" property. This carries significant legal and financial implications that will affect the property's marketability, its value, and the pool of potential buyers.

In UK conveyancing, 80 years is the "red line." Since this lease is well below that, here is a detailed breakdown of what you need to know.

---

### 1. The "Marriage Value" Trap
The most critical issue with a 68-year lease is **Marriage Value**. Under the *Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993*, when a lease drops below 80 years, the cost to extend it increases significantly.

*   **What it is:** Marriage value is the increase in the value of the property following the lease extension.
*   **The Cost:** By law, the landlord is entitled to **50% of this "uplift"** in value. 
*   **Impact:** Extending a lease at 81 years might cost £5,000–£10,000. Extending the same lease at 68 years could cost £25,000–£40,000 (plus legal and surveyor fees for both sides). This must be factored into your £425,000 valuation.

### 2. Mortgageability Issues
At 68 years, the property is approaching the "unmortgageable" threshold for many mainstream lenders.
*   **Lender Criteria:** Most lenders require the lease to have a minimum term remaining at the *end* of the mortgage (usually 25–30 years) or a minimum term at the *start* (often 70–85 years).
*   **High-Street Rejection:** Major lenders like Santander, HSBC, and Nationwide have strict thresholds. At 68 years, many will refuse to lend entirely, or will demand a significantly higher deposit (lower Loan-to-Value).
*   **The Result:** Your buyer pool will likely be limited to **cash buyers** or those using specialist niche lenders with higher interest rates.

### 3. The Statutory Right to Extend (Section 42)
Under current law, a leaseholder must own the property for **two years** before they have the legal right to a statutory lease extension. 
*   **The Risk for Buyers:** A buyer may be reluctant to wait two years because the lease will drop to 66 years in that time, making the extension even more expensive.
*   **The Solution (Formal Assignment):** To make the flat sellable, the **seller** should serve a **Section 42 Notice** between exchange and completion. This notice can be "assigned" to the buyer, allowing the buyer to take over the extension process immediately upon completion without waiting two years.

### 4. Valuation and Price Sensitivity
The asking price of £425,000 needs to be scrutinized. 
*   **"Short Lease" Discount:** A flat with a 68-year lease is typically worth **10% to 20% less** than an identical flat with a 100+ year lease. 
*   **The Math:** If £425k is the "long lease" value, the property should likely be listed significantly lower. If £425k is already the discounted price, you must be prepared to explain the cost of the extension to the buyer.

### 5. The Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024
The law is currently in a state of transition. This Act was passed in May 2024 but **is not yet fully in force** (awaiting "commencement orders"). 
*   **Abolition of Marriage Value:** The Act intends to abolish marriage value, which would make extending a 68-year lease significantly cheaper.
*   **990-Year Extensions:** It will also increase the standard extension length from 90 years to 990 years.
*   **The Catch:** We do not yet know *when* these parts of the Act will become law (likely 2025/2026). You cannot guarantee a buyer that they will benefit from this soon.

### 6. Consumer Protection Regulations (CPRs)
As an agent, you have a legal duty to disclose "material information."
*   **Disclosure:** You **must** prominently disclose the lease length (68 years) in the marketing particulars on portals like Rightmove or Zoopla. Failing to do so is a breach of CPRs because it is a factor that would heavily influence a consumer's decision to view or purchase.

---

### Summary Advice for the Listing
1.  **Check the Ground Rent:** If the ground rent is high (e.g., doubling every 10–25 years) or exceeds £250/year (£1,000 in London), this creates an "AST trap" which further complicates mortgageability.
2.  **Ask the Seller for a Valuation:** Suggest the seller pays for a specialist leasehold surveyor to provide a "Lease Extension Valuation." This gives you a concrete figure to discuss with buyers.
3.  **Propose the "Section 42" Route:** Advise the seller that they should be prepared to start the extension process and assign it to the buyer. This is often the only way to secure a buyer who needs a mortgage.
4.  **Target the Right Audience:** Market the property as an opportunity for cash buyers or investors, or specifically mention that a "lease extension is available by negotiation/assignment."

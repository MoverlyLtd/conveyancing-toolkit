### **Risk Band: CRITICAL**
At 68 years, this property falls into the **Critical** category. This is a significant threshold that will fundamentally change how you market the property and who can buy it.

---

### **1. Mortgage Availability & Lender Eligibility**
The buyer pool is severely restricted because many high-street lenders have a "hard floor" of 70 years at the time of completion.

*   **INELIGIBLE:** Barclays, Halifax, Lloyds, TSB, Coventry Building Society, and Virgin Money (who requires 85 years) will likely reject this lease length outright.
*   **MARGINAL/REFERRAL:** Santander (refers everything under 82 years) and HSBC (refers everything under 85 years) are unlikely to lend without a lease extension in progress.
*   **POTENTIAL OPTIONS:** Nationwide and NatWest are currently among the most flexible. For a standard 25-year mortgage, they generally require 55–60 years to remain at the end of the mortgage term. However, even these lenders may down-value the property significantly during the survey.

**Impact:** You are largely looking at cash buyers or specialist/niche lenders, which often leads to lower offers.

---

### **2. Saleability & Pricing Impact**
*   **Price Discount:** Properties with leases under 70 years typically see a **15% to 30% discount** compared to a property with a long (90+) lease.
*   **Buyer Perception:** Most buyers (and their solicitors) will see "68 years" as a major red flag. If you market at £425,000 without addressing the lease, you may see high initial interest that falls away once the legal paperwork starts.

---

### **3. Lease Extension & Marriage Value**
Because the lease is under 80 years, **"Marriage Value"** applies. This is a legal requirement to pay the freeholder 50% of the "profit" gained by extending the lease.

*   **Cost Estimate:** For a £425,000 flat with 68 years left, the cost to extend the lease via the statutory route is likely to be significant—often in the region of **£20,000 - £30,000+** plus legal and valuation fees.
*   **The 2024 Reform Act:** The *Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024* is now law and intends to abolish marriage value, which would make this extension much cheaper. However, **this part of the law is not yet in force** and may not be for some time. You must advise based on current costs.

---

### **4. Recommended Strategy for the Listing**

To protect the sale, you should discuss these two options with your vendor:

**Option A: The "Assign the Benefit" Route (Highly Recommended)**
The vendor serves a "Section 42" Tenant’s Notice to the freeholder to start the formal extension process *before* the sale. They then "assign the benefit" of that notice to the buyer on completion.
*   **Why?** This allows the buyer to bypass the standard 2-year ownership rule and finish the extension immediately. It makes the property mortgageable for many more lenders.

**Option B: Informal Negotiation**
The vendor asks the freeholder for an informal price to extend.
*   **Risk:** Freeholders often include "toxic" terms in informal deals (like doubling ground rents) which can make the property even harder to sell or mortgage. Your vendor's solicitor must review any informal offer carefully.

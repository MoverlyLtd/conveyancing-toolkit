The Conveyancing Association (CA) Protocol, which forms part of their **Best Practice Guide**, is designed to streamline the conveyancing process by reducing unnecessary enquiries and focusing on material risks.

When dealing with a property extension built recently (2021) without planning permission records, the CA Protocol provides a specific framework for how both the seller’s and buyer’s legal representatives should act.

Here is a detailed breakdown of how the CA Protocol handles this scenario:

---

### 1. The Principle of "Material Information"
The CA Protocol emphasizes that the seller’s lawyer should proactively disclose any known lack of planning permission at the outset.
*   **The TA6 Form:** Under the Protocol, the seller must be honest in the Law Society Property Information Form (TA6). If the extension was built in 2021 and no permission is held, this must be disclosed.
*   **Early Disclosure:** The Protocol encourages the seller's lawyer to identify this "defect" in title/compliance before a buyer is even found or at the very start of the transaction to prevent delays.

### 2. Verification of "Permitted Development" (PD)
Before treating the lack of records as a "breach," the CA Protocol suggests a pragmatic assessment:
*   **Does it actually need permission?** The lawyer should check if the extension falls under **Permitted Development Rights**. For a rear extension (2021), this generally means it must not exceed 3m (semi-detached/terraced) or 4m (detached) in depth, or up to 8m under the "Prior Approval" scheme for larger extensions.
*   **Action:** If it falls under PD, the lack of "Planning Permission" records is not a defect, though a **Certificate of Lawful Development** would have been ideal. The Protocol discourages buyers' lawyers from making "frivolous enquiries" if it is clear the work was PD.

### 3. Handling the Enforcement Window (The 4/10 Year Rule)
This is the most critical area for a 2021 build.
*   **Old Rule:** Previously, operational development (building works) became immune from enforcement after **4 years**.
*   **New Rule (Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023):** As of **April 25, 2024**, the enforcement period for all breaches of planning control in England has been extended to **10 years**.
*   **Protocol Impact:** Because the extension was built in 2021, it is **well within the 10-year enforcement window**. The CA Protocol states that where a breach is within the enforcement period, the matter **must** be resolved because it poses a genuine risk to the buyer and the lender.

### 4. The CA Protocol Approach to Enquiries
The CA Protocol aims to stop the "back and forth" of unnecessary questions.
*   **Buyer’s Lawyer:** Should not ask "Why is there no planning permission?" but rather "What steps have been taken to ensure the lender’s interest is protected?"
*   **Seller’s Lawyer:** Should not wait for the question. They should proactively offer a solution (usually Indemnity Insurance) rather than trying to argue with the local authority or seeking retrospective permission (which takes too long and alerts the council, voiding insurance options).

### 5. Specific Solution: Lack of Planning Indemnity Insurance
For a 2021 build, the CA Protocol identifies **Indemnity Insurance** as the standard industry solution to keep the "chain" moving, provided certain conditions are met:
*   **The "No Contact" Rule:** The Protocol strictly forbids anyone (buyer, seller, or lawyer) from contacting the Local Planning Authority (LPA) to ask about the extension. If the LPA is contacted, insurance becomes unavailable.
*   **Policy Coverage:** The seller usually pays for the policy. It protects the buyer and lender against the costs of demolition or alteration should the LPA take enforcement action.
*   **Lender Compliance:** The CA Protocol reminds lawyers that they must ensure any insurance policy complies with the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook** (specifically Clause 5.4.5), which requires the policy to be for the benefit of the lender and any subsequent owners.

### 6. Distinction: Planning vs. Building Regulations
The CA Protocol highlights that "Planning Permission" and "Building Regulations" are separate.
*   Even if the buyer accepts an indemnity policy for *Planning*, the Protocol notes that a 2021 build should ideally have a **Building Regulations Completion Certificate**.
*   If the Completion Certificate is also missing, the risk is higher (safety/structural). The CA Protocol suggests that for very recent works (like 2021), a buyer should be advised to have a structural survey, as indemnity insurance does not cover the extension falling down—it only covers the cost of legal enforcement.

### Summary of the CA Protocol Action Plan for this case:
1.  **Identify:** Seller’s lawyer notes the 2021 extension has no paperwork.
2.  **Evaluate:** Is it PD? If yes, provide evidence (e.g., measurements). If no, it is a breach.
3.  **Disclose:** Inform the buyer immediately.
4.  **Resolve:** Seller’s lawyer proactively offers a **Lack of Planning Permission Indemnity Policy**.
5.  **Direct:** Buyer’s lawyer checks the policy against the UK Finance Handbook.
6.  **Avoid:** No one contacts the Local Authority.
7.  **Survey:** Buyer is advised to rely on a physical survey for the quality of the 2021 build, rather than just the legal paperwork.

**Crucial Note:** If the extension is "High Risk" (e.g., it's in a Conservation Area, a Listed Building, or is clearly dangerous), the CA Protocol suggests that indemnity insurance may not be enough, and the parties may need to consider a **Retrospective Planning Application**, despite the delays this causes.

In this scenario, you are dealing with a restrictive covenant that is relatively modern (1992) and prima facie enforceable. However, the fact that the beneficiary (the neighbor) has already built a large extension significantly weakens their ability to enforce the covenant against your client.

Here is a detailed breakdown of the advice you should provide, structured by legal principles, strategic options, and risk assessment.

---

### 1. Analysis of Enforceability
First, you must confirm the covenant "runs with the land." Since it was created in 1992, it must be noted in the Charges Register of your client’s title and the Benefit must be noted (or implied) in the neighbor's Property Register.

*   **The Benefit/Burden:** Under the rule in *Tulk v Moxhay*, restrictive covenants bind successors in title.
*   **The "Change of Character" Argument:** Under **Section 84(1) of the Law of Property Act 1925**, a covenant can be discharged or modified if the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) finds that by reason of changes in the character of the property or the neighborhood, the restriction ought to be deemed obsolete. The neighbor's 2015 extension is evidence that the "external appearance" of the original scheme has already been permanently altered.

### 2. Equitable Defences: The "Clean Hands" Doctrine
The neighbor’s extension provides your client with strong equitable defences should the neighbor attempt to seek an injunction:

*   **Acquiescence and Waiver:** If the original covenantee (or the current neighbor) allowed extensions to be built in the area without protest, the covenant may be deemed to have been waived.
*   **Equitable Bar to Enforcement:** A court is unlikely to grant an injunction to a neighbor who has themselves breached the same or similar restrictive schemes. This is based on the principle that "he who seeks equity must come with clean hands." If the neighbor’s extension was also a breach of the 1992 covenant, their standing to sue is severely compromised.
*   **The Rule in *Duke of Bedford v Trustees of the British Museum*:** If the party entitled to the benefit has altered the situation so that the original purpose of the covenant can no longer be achieved, the covenant becomes unenforceable in equity.

### 3. Strategic Options for the Client

You should present the following three paths to your client, ranked from least to most aggressive:

#### Option A: Restrictive Covenant Indemnity Insurance (Recommended)
This is the standard conveyancing solution.
*   **How it works:** A policy is taken out to cover legal costs and loss of property value if the neighbor tries to enforce the covenant.
*   **The "Golden Rule":** You **must not** contact the neighbor or the original developer if you intend to seek insurance. Once a potential beneficiary is alerted to the breach, insurers will refuse to provide cover.
*   **Cost:** A one-off premium (likely £300–£700 depending on property value).
*   **Pros:** Fast, allows the sale to proceed, protects the lender.

#### Option B: Negotiated Release/Modification
*   **How it works:** Approach the neighbor and ask them to enter into a **Deed of Release or Variation**.
*   **Leverage:** Point out that their 2015 extension likely violates the same covenant (or a reciprocal one) and that their "breach" makes it impossible for them to reasonably object.
*   **Cons:** The neighbor may demand a "buy-out" fee (a percentage of the uplift in value the extension brings). If they refuse, you can no longer get indemnity insurance (see Option A).

#### Option C: Application to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)
*   **How it works:** Apply under **Section 84 of the LPA 1925** to have the covenant formally discharged or modified.
*   **Grounds:**
    *   **Ground (a):** It is obsolete.
    *   **Ground (aa):** It impedes reasonable user of the land without providing a "thinly substantial" benefit to the neighbor.
    *   **Ground (c):** The modification will not injure the persons entitled to the benefit.
*   **Pros:** Clears the title permanently.
*   **Cons:** Extremely expensive (£10k-£20k+), takes 12–18 months, and is overkill for a simple rear extension.

### 4. Risk Assessment: Injunction vs. Damages
If the client proceeds without insurance and the neighbor sues:
*   **Injunction:** The court orders the extension to be torn down. (Low risk here, as courts prefer damages if the neighbor has already extended their own home).
*   **Damages:** Following *Shelfer v City of London Electric Lighting Co*, if the injury to the neighbor's rights is small and can be estimated in money, the court may award damages instead of an injunction. Given the neighbor's own extension, any "injury" to them is negligible.

### Final Recommendation
1.  **Do not contact the neighbor yet.**
2.  **Review the neighbor’s title:** Confirm if their 2015 extension was granted a "Deed of Release" or if they simply ignored the covenant.
3.  **Obtain a quote for Indemnity Insurance:** This is the most cost-effective way to satisfy a future buyer and a mortgage lender.
4.  **If insurance is unavailable:** Draft a robust letter to the neighbor (only if the client is prepared to lose the insurance option) pointing out the 2015 extension and suggesting a mutual Deed of Release to "tidy up" both titles.

**Warning:** Advise the client that even if the covenant is resolved, they still require **Planning Permission** and **Building Regulations** approval, which are entirely separate from the private legal matter of the covenant.

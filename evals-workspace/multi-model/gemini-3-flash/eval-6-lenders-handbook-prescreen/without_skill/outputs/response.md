This is a complex scenario involving two distinct issues that are heavily regulated by the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook (the Handbook)**. Because you are acting for both the lender and the buyer, you have a duty of care to ensure the security is "good and marketable."

Here is a detailed breakdown of what the Handbook says regarding these specific issues.

---

### 1. The Lease Term (72 Years Remaining)
The Handbook’s requirements for lease length are found in **Section 5.14 (Leasehold Property)**.

*   **Minimum Term (Section 5.14.1):** The Handbook states that the lease must have a minimum unexpired term. However, the exact length is **lender-specific** and is found in **Part 2** of the Handbook for that specific lender.
    *   **Common Requirements:** Most major lenders (e.g., Barclays, Santander, HSBC) typically require at least **85–90 years** at the start of the mortgage, or **30–50 years remaining at the end of the mortgage term**.
    *   **The 72-Year Problem:** At 72 years, the property has already fallen below the **80-year threshold**, meaning "Marriage Value" is payable to the freeholder for an extension. This significantly increases the cost of extending the lease.
*   **The Valuer’s Role:** Section 5.14.1 also states that you must ensure the valuer was aware of the lease length when they conducted the valuation. If the valuation was based on a 99-year lease but it only has 72 years, the valuation is void, and you must report this to the lender.
*   **Action Required:** You must check Part 2 for your specific lender. If the 72-year term is below their minimum, the property is unmortgageable unless the seller initiates a lease extension (under the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993) as a condition of completion.

---

### 2. Loft Conversion without Building Regulations
This is covered under **Section 5.5 (Planning and Building Regulations)**.

*   **Evidence of Compliance (Section 5.5.1):** The Handbook states that you must "take all reasonable steps to ensure that the property has the benefit of any necessary planning permissions... and building regulations approval."
*   **The Breach (Section 5.5.2):** If there is no evidence of Building Regulations sign-off, there is a breach. The Handbook requires you to report this to the lender **unless** you can provide a "clear" certificate or if the works are long-standing.
*   **Enforcement Period:** While the local authority usually only has 1 year to issue an enforcement notice for a Building Regs breach (under Section 36 of the Building Act 1984), they have unlimited time to apply for an injunction if the structure is **unsafe**.
*   **Lender Notification:** If you cannot obtain sign-off, you **must** report this to the lender. Many lenders will require a structural engineer’s report to ensure the loft is safe before they will proceed, as Building Regs are primarily concerned with fire safety and structural integrity.

---

### 3. Title Indemnity Insurance (Section 9)
In practice, solicitors often try to resolve missing Building Regs with **Indemnity Insurance**.

*   **Standard Requirement (Section 9.1):** You can use indemnity insurance to mitigate the risk of local authority enforcement. However, the Handbook (Section 9.2) states that the policy must be for the benefit of the lender and the borrower and must be a one-off premium.
*   **The "Lack of Consent" Trap:** If the loft conversion is "recent" (usually less than 12 months old), most insurers will not provide cover, and the Handbook suggests lenders will not accept it.
*   **Safety Warning:** Most lenders will **not** accept indemnity insurance as a substitute for structural integrity. If the valuer identifies the loft conversion as "living space" but there are no regs, they may value the property as a lower-bedroom house (ignoring the loft), which could lead to a down-valuation.

---

### 4. Leasehold-Specific Alterations (Section 5.14.9)
Because the property is leasehold, there is a secondary layer of risk:

*   **Landlord’s Consent:** Most leases contain a covenant prohibiting structural alterations without the **Landlord's License for Alterations**.
*   **Section 5.14.9 states:** You must ensure that all conditions in the lease have been complied with. If the loft was converted without the Landlord’s written consent, this is a **breach of lease**.
*   **Forfeiture Risk:** A breach of lease gives the Landlord a right of re-entry (forfeiture). Lenders are extremely sensitive to this. Even if you get Building Regs indemnity, you would *also* need "Absence of Landlord’s Consent" indemnity or (preferably) retrospective consent from the Freeholder.

---

### Summary of What You Must Do:

1.  **Check Part 2 of the Handbook:** See if 72 years meets the lender's specific minimum term requirement.
2.  **Contact the Valuer:** Ask the valuer if they were aware of (a) the 72-year lease and (b) the lack of Building Regs. If their valuation assumes both are in order, the lender must be notified.
3.  **Request a "License for Alterations":** Ask the seller’s solicitor for the Freeholder’s consent for the loft conversion.
4.  **Report to Lender:** If no Building Regs exist, you must submit a formal disclosure to the lender. You should state whether you recommend Indemnity Insurance or if a structural survey is required.
5.  **Caution on Insurance:** Do not approach the Local Authority to ask about the loft. If you do, you "tip them off," which makes Title Indemnity Insurance impossible to obtain (as most policies are voided if the authority is notified).

**Warning:** A 72-year lease with an unauthorized loft conversion is a "high-risk" title. Most lenders will require the lease to be extended and a retrospective Building Regulations Regularisation Certificate to be obtained before they will release funds.

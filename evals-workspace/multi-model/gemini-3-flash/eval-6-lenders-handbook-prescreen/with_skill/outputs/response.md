To determine how a lender will view these issues, we must look at the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders' Handbook Part 1** (general requirements) and **Part 2** (lender-specific requirements).

Here is the assessment based on the two issues identified:

### 1. Leasehold Term: 72 Years Remaining
*   **Handbook Reference:** Part 1, Section 5.14.1 (Check 9.1 in the pre-screen).
*   **Requirement:** The unexpired lease term must meet the lender's minimum requirement specified in **Part 2, Section 5.14.1**.
*   **Assessment:** A 72-year term is considered "short" by many modern lenders.
    *   **Common Tier 1 Requirements:** Many lenders (like Nationwide or NatWest) require at least 30–40 years remaining *at the end of the mortgage term*. If the borrower is taking a 30-year mortgage, a 72-year lease leaves 42 years at the end, which may be acceptable. 
    *   **Stricter Lenders:** Some lenders (like Santander) require a absolute minimum (e.g., 55 or 70 years) regardless of the mortgage term.
*   **Action:** You must check the specific lender's Part 2 response for section 5.14.1. If the term is below their limit, the lease must be extended before completion, or the lender must grant a specific waiver.

### 2. Loft Conversion: No Building Regulations Sign-Off
*   **Handbook Reference:** Part 1, Sections 5.5.1 & 5.5.2 (Checks 13.6 and 13.7).
*   **Requirement:** You must take "all reasonable steps" to ensure the property has necessary building regulation approval. 
*   **Assessment:**
    *   **The "Good and Marketable" Test:** Under HB 5.5.2, if there is a breach but you can satisfy yourself that there is **no reasonable prospect** of the local authority taking enforcement action, and you can still certify the title as **good and marketable**, you may proceed without reporting.
    *   **Enforcement Reality:** Local authorities are generally time-barred from enforcement action under Section 36 of the Building Act 1984 after **12 months** (though they can seek an injunction via the High Court for longer).
*   **Action:** 
    *   If the conversion was done recently (less than 12 months/under 4 years ago), you **must report** this to the lender.
    *   If it is older, you can often resolve this via **Indemnity Insurance**, provided the lender's Part 2 (Section 5.4.5) allows for insurance in lieu of missing documents.
    *   **Warning:** Even if you use insurance, you must be sure the lack of sign-off doesn't affect the **valuation**. If the loft is described as a "bedroom" in the mortgage offer/valuation but doesn't meet regs, this is a **discrepancy** that must be reported under HB 4.2.

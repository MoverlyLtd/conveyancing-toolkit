From a UK conveyancing perspective, a leasehold term of **95 years** is generally considered a "safe" length in the immediate term, but it sits in a middle ground that requires careful strategic planning.

While it is not an immediate cause for alarm, there are several specific factors you must consider regarding mortgageability, future resale value, and the evolving legislative landscape.

Below is a detailed breakdown of the considerations for a 95-year lease.

---

### 1. The "80-Year Rule" and Marriage Value
The most critical threshold in UK leasehold law is **80 years**. 

*   **The Cliff Edge:** Once a lease falls below 80 years, the cost of extending it increases significantly because of **"Marriage Value."** This is a calculation where the surveyor determines the increase in the value of the property created by the new lease and requires the leaseholder to pay 50% of that "profit" to the landlord.
*   **The 95-Year Buffer:** With 95 years remaining, the buyer has a **15-year buffer** before hitting that expensive 80-year mark. 
*   **Recommendation:** If the buyer intends to live in the property for more than 5–10 years, they should be aware that when they come to sell, the next buyer will be looking at a lease closer to the 80-year limit, which may affect the sale price or the speed of the transaction.

### 2. Mortgage Lender Requirements
Every lender has a specific "minimum unexpired lease term" requirement, which can be found in the **UK Finance Lenders’ Handbook**.

*   **Current Standing:** Most lenders are perfectly happy with 95 years. Standard requirements are usually:
    *   A minimum of 70–85 years at the time of completion.
    *   Or, at least 30–50 years remaining *at the end* of the mortgage term.
*   **Future Impact:** If the buyer takes a 30-year mortgage, the lease will have 65 years left at the end of the term. While the initial loan is safe, the buyer would find it very difficult to remortgage or sell the property 15 years into that term without extending the lease first.

### 3. Statutory Rights to Extend
Under the **Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993**, a leaseholder currently has a statutory right to extend their lease by **90 years** and reduce the ground rent to a **"peppercorn" (zero)**.

*   **The Two-Year Rule:** Under current (old) rules, the buyer must own the property for **two years** before they can formally serve a Section 42 Notice to extend the lease. 
*   **Strategy:** If the buyer is concerned, they can ask the *seller* to initiate the lease extension process and "assign the benefit" of the Section 42 Notice to the buyer upon completion. This allows the buyer to bypass the two-year waiting period.

### 4. The Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024
This is a vital new development. This Act received Royal Assent in May 2024, though many of its provisions are awaiting secondary legislation to come into full effect (likely 2025/2026).

*   **Marriage Value:** The new law aims to **abolish marriage value**. If this part of the Act is fully implemented, the "80-year cliff" becomes less relevant, making it cheaper to extend leases even if they are short.
*   **990-Year Extensions:** The statutory extension length will increase from 90 years to **990 years**.
*   **Removal of the Two-Year Rule:** The Act intends to remove the requirement to own the property for two years before extending.
*   **Risk:** Until these provisions are fully "commenced" by the government, the buyer must rely on the existing (more expensive) system.

### 5. Ground Rent and "Onerous" Clauses
The length of the lease (95 years) is often less of a problem than the **Ground Rent** terms contained within it. You must check the lease for:

*   **Doubling Ground Rent:** Clauses where ground rent doubles every 10, 15, or 20 years. Lenders often refuse to mortgage properties where ground rent exceeds 0.1% of the property value or has aggressive doubling terms.
*   **Section 8 Issues:** If ground rent exceeds **£250 (£1,000 in London)**, the lease can technically be classified as an Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST). This creates a risk where a landlord could theoretically evict a tenant for rent arrears (forfeiture) without the lender being able to intervene. Most solicitors now require a "Lender Protection Clause" or an indemnity policy for this.

### Summary Checklist for the Buyer:

1.  **Is 95 years "short"?** No, but it is "declining." It is a marketable length today, but will require action within 10 years.
2.  **What is the Ground Rent?** If it is high or doubles frequently, this is a much bigger concern than the 95-year term.
3.  **Future Plans?** If the buyer plans to move in 3–5 years, 95 years is fine. If they plan to stay for 20 years, they should budget for a lease extension cost.
4.  **The 2024 Act:** Advise the buyer that the law is changing in their favor, likely making extensions cheaper and longer in the near future.

**Conclusion:** There is no need to withdraw from the purchase based on a 95-year term alone. However, the buyer should be advised to investigate the cost of a statutory extension now so they can factor that future cost into their financial planning.

In UK conveyancing (specifically England and Wales), restrictive covenants are private agreements between landowners where one party promises not to do certain things on their land for the benefit of another’s land.

To understand the rules and identify covenants affecting a specific freehold property, you must look in the following places and apply the following legal principles.

---

### 1. Where to Look (The Sources)

#### A. The Land Registry: Official Copies (Register of Title)
For registered land, the primary source is the **Official Copies of the Register**.
*   **The Charges Register (Part C):** This is where restrictive covenants are listed. You will see an entry stating that the land is subject to "restrictive covenants contained in a Conveyance dated [Date]."
*   **Filed Deeds:** Often, the Register only provides a summary or a reference. You must look for the words **"Copy Filed"** or **"Original Filed."** If this appears, you must obtain a copy of the original **Transfer (TR1/TP1)** or **Conveyance** from the Land Registry to read the full, precise wording of the covenant.

#### B. The Title Deeds (The "Bundle")
If the property is **unregistered**, you must look at the physical "root of title" (usually a deed at least 15 years old) and subsequent conveyances.
*   Check the **Land Charges Department** search results (Form K15) against previous owners’ names to see if any restrictive covenants (Class D(ii)) were registered.

#### C. The Seller’s Property Information Form (TA6)
In a standard transaction, the seller completes a **TA6 form**. Section 4 specifically asks the seller about any breaches of covenants or any notices received regarding them.

---

### 2. The Legal Rules for Validity
Not all covenants listed on a title are legally enforceable. For a restrictive covenant to "run with the land" (bind future owners), it must meet the criteria established in the landmark case ***Tulk v Moxhay (1848)***:

1.  **It must be "Negative" in nature:** It must be a "thou shalt not." If it requires the owner to spend money (e.g., "maintain a fence"), it is a *positive* covenant. Positive covenants generally do not run with freehold land (subject to some exceptions like "chains of indemnity").
2.  **It must benefit "Dominant Land":** The person seeking to enforce the covenant must own land that is capable of being benefited by the restriction.
3.  **It must "Touch and Concern" the land:** It cannot be a personal whim; it must affect the mode of occupation or the value of the land.
4.  **Intention:** The original parties must have intended for the burden to pass to successors (usually implied by Section 79 of the Law of Property Act 1925).
5.  **Notice:** The covenant must be correctly registered (as a D(ii) Land Charge for unregistered land or in the Charges Register for registered land) to bind a "purchaser for value."

---

### 3. Common Types of Restrictive Covenants
When looking at the deeds, you will typically find restrictions against:
*   **Alterations/Extensions:** "Not to make any external alterations without the prior written consent of the Vendor."
*   **Trade or Business:** "Not to carry on any trade, business or profession upon the property."
*   **Nuisance:** "Not to do anything which may be a nuisance or annoyance to the neighbours."
*   **Animals:** "Not to keep any livestock or poultry" (often found in 1950s/60s deeds).
*   **Fencing:** "Not to erect any fence or wall forward of the building line."

---

### 4. What to Do if a Covenant is Found
If you find a covenant that interferes with your plans (e.g., you want to build an extension but there is a "No Alterations" covenant), a conveyancer will take the following steps:

#### A. Determine Enforceability
Is the "Dominant Owner" (the person who benefits) still identifiable? If the covenant was created by a developer 100 years ago and that company no longer exists, the covenant may be "spent" or practically unenforceable.

#### B. Indemnity Insurance
This is the most common solution in UK conveyancing. If a covenant has been breached for more than 12 months without objection, or if you plan a future breach, you can buy a **Restrictive Covenant Indemnity Policy**. This protects against the cost of legal fees and loss of property value if someone tries to enforce the covenant.
*   *Warning:* You cannot get insurance if you have already contacted the person with the benefit of the covenant.

#### C. Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)
Under **Section 84 of the Law of Property Act 1925**, you can apply to have a covenant discharged or modified if:
*   It is obsolete (due to changes in the neighborhood).
*   It prevents a reasonable use of the land.
*   The person entitled to the benefit has agreed (expressly or impliedly) to it being lifted.
*   *Note:* This is expensive and time-consuming.

#### D. Express Release
Negotiate a "Deed of Release" with the person who owns the benefiting land. This usually involves paying them a sum of money.

---

### 5. Summary Checklist for your Search:
1.  **Download Official Copies** from the Land Registry.
2.  **Inspect the Charges Register** for mentions of Conveyances/Transfers.
3.  **Order the "Filed Deed"** specifically to read the full text.
4.  **Identify the "Dominant Land"** to see who can enforce it.
5.  **Check for "Building Schemes"** (where a whole estate is bound by the same rules—see *Elliston v Reacher*).
6.  **Confirm if Planning Permission has been granted** (Note: Planning permission does *not* override a restrictive covenant).

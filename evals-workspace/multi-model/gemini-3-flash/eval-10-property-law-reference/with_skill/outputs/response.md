According to the **Law Society**, a restrictive covenant is a private agreement between landowners where one party agrees to restrict the use of their land in some way for the benefit of another's land. These are common in freehold property transactions and are designed to uphold the character or value of a neighborhood. (Source: [https://www.lawsociety.org.uk/topics/property/restrictive-covenants](https://www.lawsociety.org.uk/topics/property/restrictive-covenants))

For technical details on how these are protected on the land register, **HM Land Registry (HMLR) Practice Guide 19** explains that restrictive covenants are generally protected by an entry in the Charges Register of the affected title. (Source: [https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/restrictions/practice-guide-19-notices-restrictions-and-the-protection-of-third-party-interests-in-the-register](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/restrictions/practice-guide-19-notices-restrictions-and-the-protection-of-third-party-interests-in-the-register))

### Key Rules and Considerations:

*   **Binding Nature:** Restrictive covenants "run with the land." This means that even if you didn't sign the original agreement, the restrictions bind subsequent owners of the property.
*   **Common Examples:** Restrictions often include prohibitions on building extensions without consent, running a business from the property, or keeping livestock.
*   **Enforcement:** The owner of the land that benefits from the covenant (the "dominant" land) has the right to enforce it. If a covenant is breached, the beneficiary could seek an injunction or claim damages.
*   **Challenging a Covenant:** If a covenant is obsolete or prevents reasonable use of the land, an application can be made to the **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)** to have it discharged or modified. This is often a complex and costly process.
*   **Indemnity Insurance:** In many conveyancing transactions where an old restrictive covenant has been breached (or where the beneficiary is unknown), solicitors may suggest "Restrictive Covenant Indemnity Insurance" to protect the buyer against potential enforcement costs.

### Where to look in your documents:
You should check the **Charges Register** of your Official Copy of Register of Title. It will typically refer to a "Conveyance" or "Transfer" deed which contains the specific wording of the covenants.

**Consider discussing the specific implications of any covenants found on your title with your conveyancer, as they can significantly impact how you use or develop your property.**

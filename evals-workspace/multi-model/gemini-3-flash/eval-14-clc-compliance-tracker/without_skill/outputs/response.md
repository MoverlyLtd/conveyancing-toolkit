As a firm regulated by the **Council for Licensed Conveyancers (CLC)**, your handling of complaints is governed primarily by the **CLC Code of Conduct** and the **CLC Complaints Code**.

Because the complaint specifically concerns "delays," you must address both the procedural requirements of the Code and the substantive issue of service standards. Here is a detailed breakdown of what the CLC requires you to do.

---

### 1. Initial Acknowledgment and Information
The moment a complaint is identified (whether made orally or in writing), the CLC requires you to:
*   **Acknowledge receipt:** While the CLC Handbook doesn't specify a set number of days for acknowledgment, best practice—and the standard usually expected by the CLC—is to acknowledge the complaint within **2 to 5 working days**.
*   **Provide the Procedure:** You must provide the client with a written copy of your firm’s **Complaints Procedure**.
*   **Identify the Complaint Handler:** Inform the client who will be dealing with the complaint (usually a senior manager or the Head of Legal Practice/COLP).

### 2. The Internal Investigation Process
The CLC Complaints Code dictates that your process must be:
*   **Free of charge:** You cannot charge the client for the time spent investigating the complaint.
*   **Fair and Thorough:** You must investigate the facts objectively. Since the complaint is about **delays**, you must review the file timeline to determine if the delay was within your control (e.g., staffing issues, oversight) or outside of it (e.g., slow mortgage offer, chain delays, or unresponsive third-party solicitors).
*   **Timely:** You have a maximum of **8 weeks** to resolve the complaint.

### 3. Communicating the "Final Response"
At the conclusion of your investigation, you must send a formal **Final Response Letter**. This letter must:
*   **Address all points raised:** Specifically, explain why the delays occurred.
*   **Offer Redress (if applicable):** If the delay was your firm's fault, the CLC expects you to consider an apology, a reduction in fees, or financial compensation for any quantifiable loss or significant distress/inconvenience caused.
*   **Signpost to the Legal Ombudsman (LeO):** This is a mandatory requirement. You must inform the client that if they remain dissatisfied, they have the right to take the matter to the Legal Ombudsman. You must provide the LeO’s contact details and the time limits for doing so (usually within 6 months of your final response).

### 4. Mandatory Signposting for ADR
Even if your firm does not intend to use an Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) provider, the CLC requires you to provide the client with the name and website address of an approved ADR entity (e.g., Ombudsman Services or ProMediate) that could handle the complaint. You must state whether or not you agree to use that ADR scheme.

### 5. Internal Governance and Reporting
The CLC doesn't just care about the individual client; they care about your firm’s systemic health.
*   **The Complaints Register:** You must record the complaint in your firm’s central Complaints Register.
*   **Root Cause Analysis:** The CLC expects firms to analyze complaints to identify patterns. If "delays" are a recurring theme, the CLC expects you to take remedial action (e.g., hiring more staff, updating case management software, or reducing individual caseloads).
*   **Regulatory Notification:** You do not typically need to report an individual complaint to the CLC immediately *unless* it reveals a serious breach of the CLC Code of Conduct or indicates the firm is in financial distress. However, you must provide this data to the CLC during your annual regulatory return.

### 6. Dealing specifically with "Delays"
Since the complaint is about delays, you should be aware that the **CLC Customer Service Code** requires you to "keep each client informed of progress" and "advise clients of any cause for, and the implications of, any delay." 

If your investigation finds that:
*   **You did not inform the client of the delay:** This is a breach of the Service Code.
*   **The delay was avoidable:** You should consider a proactive "goodwill" gesture to prevent the matter from escalating to the Legal Ombudsman, as the LeO frequently awards compensation for "distress and inconvenience" caused by conveyancing delays.

### Summary Checklist for your Firm:
1.  **Stop the clock:** Ensure you hit the 8-week deadline for a final response.
2.  **Verify the file:** Did you manage expectations? If you quoted 8-12 weeks and it took 20 without explanation, the complaint is likely valid.
3.  **Draft the response:** Include the specific "signposting" language for the Legal Ombudsman.
4.  **Update the Register:** Log the outcome for your next CLC inspection.

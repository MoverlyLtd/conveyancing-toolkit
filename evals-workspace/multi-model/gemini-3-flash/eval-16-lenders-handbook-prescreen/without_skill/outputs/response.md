In the UK, mortgage lending criteria for leasehold properties are primarily governed by the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook** (specifically Section 5.14.1). Each lender sets its own minimum requirements regarding the "unexpired term" of a lease.

For a property with **72 years remaining**, here is how the three lenders you mentioned generally view the application:

### 1. Halifax
*   **The Rule:** Halifax (part of Lloyds Banking Group) typically requires a minimum of **70 years remaining** on the lease at the time of the mortgage application.
*   **Will they lend on 72 years?** **Yes.** 
*   **Specifics:** Halifax is one of the more flexible major lenders regarding lease length. They do not usually require a "buffer" at the end of the mortgage term, provided the lease is at least 70 years at the start.

### 2. Nationwide Building Society
*   **The Rule:** Nationwide requires the lease to have at least **55 years remaining at the end of the mortgage term**. 
*   **Will they lend on 72 years?** **Likely No (subject to the mortgage term).**
*   **The Calculation:** 
    *   If you take a standard **25-year mortgage**: $72 - 25 = 47$ years remaining at the end. (Fails the 55-year rule).
    *   If you take a **30-year mortgage**: $72 - 30 = 42$ years remaining at the end. (Fails).
    *   To satisfy Nationwide, you would need to take a mortgage term of no more than **17 years** ($72 - 17 = 55$).
*   **Specifics:** For most modern buyers seeking a 25+ year term, Nationwide would require the lease to be extended before completion or require the seller to have started the extension process.

### 3. Barclays
*   **The Rule:** Barclays generally requires a minimum of **70 years remaining** on the lease at the point of completion.
*   **Will they lend on 72 years?** **Yes.**
*   **Specifics:** Like Halifax, Barclays has historically adhered to a flat minimum of 70 years. However, they are rigorous regarding "onerous ground rent" clauses (e.g., doubling ground rent every 10–15 years), which often coincide with older 70-80 year leases.

---

### Crucial Conveyancing Considerations (The "80-Year Rule")

While Halifax and Barclays may lend on a 72-year lease, you must consider the **statutory implications** of the lease dropping below 80 years:

1.  **Marriage Value:** Because the lease is already below 80 years, it has attracted "Marriage Value." This means it is significantly more expensive to extend the lease than it would have been at 81 years. The cost of extension increases every year the lease gets shorter.
2.  **Future Saleability:** Even if Halifax lends to you now, when you come to sell in 5 years, the lease will be **67 years**. At that point, Halifax and Barclays will likely **refuse** to lend to your buyer. You may find yourself "mortgage trapped" unless you extend the lease during your ownership.
3.  **Valuation Hit:** A surveyor for any of these lenders may note the short lease in their valuation report. If the surveyor decides the short lease affects the "marketability" of the property, they may down-value the property or recommend a retention of funds until the lease is extended.

### Summary Table

| Lender | Minimum Requirement | Verdict on 72-Year Lease |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |

**Recommendation:** If you are the buyer, you should ask the seller to commence the lease extension process (serving a Section 42 Notice) between exchange and completion, so you can inherit the right to extend without waiting the statutory two years of ownership.

*Disclaimer: Lender criteria can change monthly. Your solicitor or mortgage broker should check the most recent updates in the UK Finance Handbook (Section 5.14.1) for each specific lender before proceeding.*

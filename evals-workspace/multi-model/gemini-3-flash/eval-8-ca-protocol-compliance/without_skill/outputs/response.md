In the context of residential conveyancing in England and Wales, the **Law Society’s Conveyancing Protocol (2019 Edition)** is the primary framework governing the conduct of solicitors. If the other side is failing to respond to enquiries, they are likely in breach of the Protocol’s core obligations.

Here is a detailed breakdown of what the Protocol says and the specific steps you should take.

### 1. The General Obligation (Standard Requirement 1)
The Protocol’s overarching principle is to facilitate the exchange of contracts and completion as soon as is reasonably possible.
*   **Cooperation:** Standard Requirement 1 mandates that solicitors must act "professionally and cooperatively" to ensure the transaction proceeds efficiently. 
*   **Avoidance of Delay:** Solicitors are specifically instructed to avoid "unnecessary delays" and to provide information in a clear and concise manner.

### 2. Specific Protocol Steps Regarding Enquiries
The Protocol provides specific instructions for both the Buyer’s and Seller’s solicitors regarding the management of enquiries:

*   **Step 31 (Seller’s Obligations):** This step explicitly states that the Seller’s solicitor must **"deal with any enquiries as soon as possible"**. They are expected to seek instructions from their client promptly and provide full and complete responses.
*   **Step 30 (Managing Delays):** If there is a delay in obtaining information (e.g., waiting for a management company or a third party), the Seller’s solicitor is under an obligation to **"keep the other side informed of any delays and the reasons for them."** Silence is a direct breach of this step.
*   **Step 24 (Buyer’s Obligations):** The Protocol also places a burden on you. It states that the Buyer’s solicitor should only raise "necessary" additional enquiries. If your enquiries are deemed "standard" or "repetitive" (asking things already covered by the TA6 or TA10), the other side may argue they are not obliged to prioritize them.

### 3. The CQS (Conveyancing Quality Scheme) Context
Most firms following the Protocol are CQS-accredited. Under the CQS requirements:
*   Firms must adhere to the Protocol.
*   Failure to respond to enquiries is a performance issue that can be escalated to the firm’s **Senior Responsible Officer (SRO)**.

### Recommended Action Plan Based on the Protocol

If you are facing a wall of silence, you should move through the following stages of escalation:

#### Stage 1: The "Protocol Compliance" Letter
Write a formal letter/email specifically referencing the Protocol. 
*   **Drafting Tip:** "We refer to Step 31 of the Law Society Conveyancing Protocol. Our enquiries dated [Date] remain outstanding. Under Standard Requirement 1, you are obliged to act cooperatively and avoid unnecessary delay. Please provide responses within [e.g., 48 hours] or explain the reason for the delay as per Step 30."

#### Stage 2: Telephone Escalation
The Protocol encourages communication. A formal "chaser" system often gets stuck in a firm's administrative pile. Call the fee earner directly. If they are unavailable, speak to their Head of Department or the SRO. Remind them that their firm’s CQS accreditation requires adherence to the Protocol.

#### Stage 3: Client Reporting
Under the **SRA Code of Conduct**, you have a duty to keep your client informed.
*   Advise your client that the other side is in breach of the Conveyancing Protocol.
*   Suggest that your client contacts the Estate Agent. Agents often have a more direct line to the Seller and can apply "commercial pressure" that solicitors cannot.

#### Stage 4: Formal Warning of Non-Compliance
If the delay continues without explanation, you may formally notify them that you are reporting the matter to your client as a breakdown in the Protocol. You can threaten to advise your client to reconsider the transaction due to the other side's "failure to show a willingness to proceed" (a common trigger for clients to pull out).

### 4. A Note on "Excessive Enquiries"
Before escalating, ensure your house is in order. The Law Society has recently taken a hard line against "excessive" enquiries. If you have raised 50+ enquiries on a straightforward registered title, the other side may feel justified in their delay. 
*   **Protocol Step 24** warns against asking questions that have already been answered in the Small Print, the Title, or the TA forms. If your enquiries are bespoke and necessary, you are on firm ground.

### Summary for your Client
"The Law Society Conveyancing Protocol, which both firms are committed to following, dictates that the other solicitor must respond to enquiries as soon as possible and keep us informed of any reasons for delay. They are currently in breach of these professional standards. I have formally reminded them of their obligations under the Protocol and am awaiting an urgent response before we discuss further options, such as escalating to their Senior Management."

This is a very common scenario in residential conveyancing. Because the work was carried out in 2018, it is subject to modern Building Regulations, specifically **Part L** (Conservation of fuel and power/thermal insulation) and **Part K** (Protection from falling, collision and impact—formerly Part N regarding safety glazing).

Here is the recommended advice for your client:

### 1. The "No Contact" Rule (Critical)
**Do not contact the local authority building control department yet.** Under no circumstances should the client, the seller, or the estate agent make an enquiry about this specific property. 
*   **Why:** Once the local authority is notified of unauthorised work, **indemnity insurance becomes unavailable**. This would remove the cheapest and fastest way to resolve the issue.

### 2. Double-Check the Registers
Before proceeding with more complex solutions, verify if the "local company" actually registered the work but the certificate was simply lost. 
*   Search the property address at [competentperson.co.uk](https://www.competentperson.co.uk) or directly on the [FENSA](https://www.fensa.org.uk) or [CERTASS](https://www.certass.co.uk) websites.
*   If a record exists, a duplicate certificate can be ordered for a small fee, which solves the problem entirely.

### 3. Resolution Route: Indemnity Insurance vs. Regularisation
Assuming no certificate is found, you have two main options:

#### Option A: Indemnity Insurance (Recommended)
This is the standard industry solution for missing window certificates.
*   **Process:** The seller pays a one-off premium (typically £50–£200) for a policy that protects the buyer and lender against local authority enforcement action and any resulting loss in property value.
*   **Pros:** It is instant and allows the transaction to proceed without delay. Most lenders (per the UK Finance Handbook) accept indemnity insurance for replacement windows as the enforcement risk is very low.
*   **Cons:** It does **not** cover the cost of fixing the windows if they are actually defective or poorly insulated; it only covers legal enforcement risk.

#### Option B: Regularisation (Retrospective Approval)
The seller applies to the local authority for a "Regularisation Certificate."
*   **Process:** A building control surveyor will inspect the windows. They may require proof of the glass type (to check "U-values" for insulation) and ensure safety glass is used in critical locations (e.g., floor-to-ceiling windows or doors).
*   **Pros:** Provides absolute certainty that the windows meet legal standards.
*   **Cons:** It costs significantly more (£400+), takes 4–8 weeks, and if the windows fail the inspection, the seller would be forced to replace them at their own cost.

### 4. Lender Requirements
You should check **Part 2 of the UK Finance Mortgage Lenders' Handbook** for the client's specific lender. Most mainstream lenders are comfortable with indemnity insurance for replacement windows because the work is considered "low risk" compared to structural changes or loft conversions.

### Summary of Advice to Client
1.  **Instruct the seller** to provide a FENSA/CERTASS certificate or a Building Regulations completion certificate.
2.  **If they cannot,** request that the seller pays for a **Building Regulations Indemnity Policy**.
3.  **Warn the client** that the insurance covers legal risk, not the quality of the windows. If they are concerned about the windows' performance or safety, they should ask their surveyor to take a closer look at the glazing and seals.
4.  **Confirm** that neither they nor the seller should speak to the council until the indemnity policy is in place.

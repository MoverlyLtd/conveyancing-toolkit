Replacement windows installed since April 2002 must comply with **Part L** of the Building Regulations, which covers thermal performance and energy efficiency.

Here is the guidance for resolving this issue:

### 1. The "Before Any Approach" Rule (Critical)
**Do not contact the local authority building control department** until a strategy is agreed upon. If anyone (seller, buyer, or agent) makes even an informal enquiry about the lack of certification for these windows, **indemnity insurance will become unavailable**. This is because insurance only covers the risk of the council "discovering" the breach; if they are notified, the risk is no longer insurable.

### 2. Check for Self-Certification
Before assuming there is no certificate, search the **competentperson.co.uk** website. Even if the seller does not have the paperwork, the installer (if they were a "local company" registered with a scheme) may have registered the work electronically. You should search for:
*   **FENSA** or **CERTASS** (the most common schemes for windows).
If a certificate is found here, it is equivalent to a building regulations completion certificate, and no further action is required.

### 3. Resolution Options

If no self-certification is found, you have two main routes:

#### Option A: Indemnity Insurance
This is the most common and quickest resolution for replacement windows, which are generally considered low-risk.
*   **Cost:** Typically **£50–£200** (one-off premium).
*   **Process:** A policy is put in place to cover the cost of potential enforcement action by the local authority.
*   **Lender Position:** Most mainstream lenders will accept indemnity insurance for replacement windows, as the risk of enforcement is very low.
*   **Limitation:** It does not guarantee that the windows actually meet Part L standards; it only protects against the cost of legal action or a loss in property value.

#### Option B: Regularisation
This involves applying for retrospective building regulations approval from the local authority.
*   **Cost:** Typically **£400–£800**.
*   **Process:** A building control surveyor will visit the property. They may require proof of the glass type or even the removal of a small section of trim to check for insulation around the frame.
*   **Timescale:** 4–8 weeks.
*   **Lender Position:** Lenders prefer this route as it provides a physical **Regularisation Certificate** confirming the windows are compliant.
*   **Risk:** If the windows do not meet Part L standards (e.g., they aren't energy efficient enough), the council will refuse the certificate, and the seller will have to replace or upgrade the glass at their own cost.

### Summary Advice
Since the work was done in 2018, it is relatively recent, but windows are generally low-enforcement risks (unless they are in a listed building or conservation area). 

1.  **Search the competent person register** first to see if a FENSA/CERTASS record exists.
2.  If not, and **provided no contact has been made with the council**, ask the seller to provide a **Building Regulations Indemnity Policy**. This is usually the fastest and cheapest way to satisfy a lender and progress the transaction.
3.  Ensure the client understands that an indemnity policy covers **legal enforcement risk**, not the **physical quality** or energy efficiency of the windows.

In UK conveyancing, replacement windows are considered "controlled fittings" under the **Building Regulations 2010**. Since the installation took place in 2018, it is subject to these regulations. The absence of a FENSA (or CERTASS) certificate or a Building Regulations Completion Certificate represents a legal defect in the title's compliance.

Here is a detailed breakdown of the advice you should provide to your client, categorized by risk, lender requirements, and practical solutions.

---

### 1. The Legal Problem
Replacement windows must comply with **Part L (Thermal Efficiency)**, **Part K (Protection from falling, collision and impact - i.e., safety glass)**, and often **Part B (Fire Safety/Means of Escape)**. 
*   **FENSA/CERTASS:** These are "Competent Person Schemes." Registered installers can self-certify that their work meets the regulations.
*   **Building Control:** If the installer was not registered, they were legally required to notify Local Authority Building Control (LABC) before installation and have it inspected.

The lack of either means there is no legal evidence that the windows are safe, thermally efficient, or offer adequate means of escape in a fire.

### 2. Risk of Local Authority Enforcement
Under **Section 36 of the Building Act 1984**, a local authority can take enforcement action for a breach of Building Regulations.
*   **12-month limit:** The council can issue a notice to remove or alter non-compliant work within 12 months of completion. As the windows were installed in 2018, this window has passed.
*   **Injunctions (Section 36(6)):** There is no time limit for an injunction; however, local authorities rarely pursue this for domestic windows unless they pose a severe safety risk to the public.
*   **Advice:** The risk of the council forcing the client to rip the windows out is **low**, but the risk to the property's **marketability** and **mortgageability** is high.

### 3. Impact on Mortgages (UK Finance Handbook)
If the client is using a mortgage, you must comply with the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook** (Section 5.10). Most lenders require that any replacement windows have the necessary completion certificates.
*   If you proceed without them, you may be in breach of your duty to the lender.
*   You must report the lack of certification to the lender or resolve it via one of the methods below.

### 4. Specific Recommendations for the Client

#### A. The "Search" Step
Advise the client to check the [FENSA website](https://www.fensa.org.uk/check-fensa-certificate) or the [CERTASS website](https://www.certass.co.uk/) using the property's postcode. Sellers are often mistaken, and a certificate may have been registered even if they lost the paperwork.

#### B. Building Regulations Indemnity Insurance (The Standard Solution)
This is the most common way to resolve the issue in UK conveyancing.
*   **What it does:** It protects the buyer (and lender) against the costs of any enforcement action taken by the local authority.
*   **Who pays:** Usually the seller pays the one-off premium (typically £20–£50).
*   **The "Golden Rule":** You must warn the client **not to contact the Local Authority** to ask about the windows. If the council is made aware of the breach, the indemnity insurance becomes void or unavailable.
*   **The Limitation:** Stress to the client that this **does not cover the quality of the windows.** If the windows leak or are poorly fitted, the insurance will not pay out.

#### C. Specialist Surveyor Inspection
Since the "local company" did not provide certification, there is a risk the windows were not fitted to a high standard. You should advise the client to ask their surveyor to specifically inspect:
*   **Safety Glass:** Look for the BS EN 12150 kitemark (required in doors and low-level windows).
*   **Means of Escape:** Ensure at least one window in each habitable room on the first floor is large enough to serve as a fire escape.
*   **Thermal Integrity:** Checking for "misting" between panes or drafts.

#### D. Regularisation (Retrospective Approval)
The seller could apply to the Local Authority for a **Regularisation Certificate**. 
*   **Downside:** This is slow, expensive, and involves an inspector potentially taking the window apart or drilling holes to check for lintels/insulation.
*   **Risk:** If the inspector fails the windows, the "enforcement risk" becomes live, and indemnity insurance is no longer an option. This is usually a last resort.

### 5. Final Advice Checklist
1.  **Request a FENSA/Building Control search** via the seller’s solicitor to double-check.
2.  **Ask for the original invoice** from the "local company." This might provide a warranty (though warranties are often non-transferable) and proves the date of installation.
3.  **Insist on an Indemnity Policy** at the seller’s expense to satisfy the lender.
4.  **Qualify your advice:** Explicitly state that you are not a surveyor and cannot comment on the structural or thermal quality of the windows; they are proceeding at their own risk regarding the physical condition of the units.

When dealing with funds originating from Dubai (United Arab Emirates), a UK conveyancer must move beyond standard Customer Due Diligence (CDD) and implement **Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)**. 

While the UAE was removed from the FATF "Grey List" in February 2024, many UK law firms and the SRA still categorize the region as "High Risk" due to the high volume of cash-based transactions, the prevalence of Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs), and the opacity of some corporate structures.

Here are the specific additional AML checks and actions you should carry out:

---

### 1. Mandatory Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)
Under **Regulation 33 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017**, EDD is required if you determine there is a high risk of money laundering. Dubai often triggers this due to the complexity of the "Source of Wealth."
*   **Action:** You must obtain more information on the customer and the beneficial owner, and more information on the intended nature of the business relationship.

### 2. Source of Wealth (SoW) vs. Source of Funds (SoF)
For standard UK transactions, you might only look at the bank statement for the deposit (SoF). For Dubai funds, you **must** establish the SoW.
*   **The Question:** Not just "where is the money now?" but "how did the client accumulate their total net worth?"
*   **Evidence required:** 
    *   If from employment: 3–6 months of UAE pay slips and a copy of the Dubai employment contract.
    *   If from business: Audited accounts or a letter from a reputable UAE accounting firm.
    *   If from property sale in Dubai: A copy of the "Title Deed" from the Dubai Land Department (DLD) and the "Form F" (Contract) or "Memorandum of Understanding."
    *   If from gift: Evidence of the donor’s wealth and a signed declaration.

### 3. Verification of UAE Corporate Entities (Free Zones)
If the funds are coming from a company or the client owns a business in Dubai, you must navigate the "Free Zone" system. Dubai has over 30 Free Zones (e.g., DIFC, DMCC, JAFZA), each with its own registry.
*   **Action:** Do not rely solely on a trade license. Obtain a **Certificate of Incumbency** or a **Memorandum of Association** to identify the Ultimate Beneficial Owners (UBOs).
*   **Verification:** Use the specific Free Zone’s online portal to verify the company status (e.g., the DIFC Public Register).

### 4. Scrutiny of the Banking Pathway
The "pathway" of the money is as important as the source. 
*   **Direct Transfer:** Ensure funds are transferred directly from a regulated UAE bank (e.g., Emirates NBD, HSBC UAE, Abu Dhabi Commercial Bank). 
*   **Currency Exchanges:** Be extremely cautious of funds arriving via "Currency Exchange Houses" (common in the UAE). These can sometimes bypass standard banking transparency. If a client uses one, you must verify that the exchange house is regulated by the **UAE Central Bank** and request the "MT103" document to track the full payment trail.
*   **Prohibited:** Strictly avoid any funds arriving via **Hawala** or informal value transfer systems.

### 5. Sanctions and PEP Screening
Dubai is a global hub for high-net-worth individuals from jurisdictions currently under UK/International sanctions (e.g., Russia, Iran).
*   **Sanctions Check:** Perform an up-to-the-minute check on the client and any corporate officers against the **UK Sanctions List** (OFSI).
*   **PEP Status:** Check if the client or their family members hold positions in UAE government departments, the judiciary, or state-owned enterprises (e.g., DEWA, ADNOC). Dubai’s governance structure means many business leaders also hold "PEPy" status.

### 6. Geographical Risk Assessment (The "Red Flags")
Check for these Dubai-specific red flags:
*   **The "Golden Visa":** If the client is in the UK on a UAE Golden Visa, verify the basis on which it was granted (investment, talent, etc.), as this speaks to their wealth profile.
*   **Tax Residency:** The UAE has no personal income tax. If a client claims wealth from "tax-free savings," you must see the bank statements showing the accumulation of those savings over a period of years, not a sudden lump sum.
*   **Third-Party Payments:** If the money is coming from a third party in Dubai (e.g., a business associate or a different company), this is a high-risk indicator. You should generally refuse to accept funds from a third party unless they are a close family member and have undergone full ID&V and SoW checks.

### 7. Independent Verification (Technology)
Do not rely on photocopies.
*   **Action:** Use an electronic verification platform (e.g., Thirdfort, Amiqus, or Credas) that can perform "NFC" chips reads on passports.
*   **Dubai specific:** You can ask the client to provide a **"Police Clearance Certificate"** from the Dubai Police, which is often used for official purposes to prove "good conduct."

### 8. Legal Sector Affinity Group (LSAG) Guidance
Ensure your file note explicitly references the **LSAG Guidance**. You should document your "Risk Appetite" and provide a written rationale as to why you are satisfied with the Dubai funds.

### Summary Checklist for your file:
1.  **Risk Assessment:** Signed off by the Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO).
2.  **SoW:** Documented history of how the money was made (e.g., 5 years of history).
3.  **SoF:** Bank statements showing the money sitting in a UAE account for at least 6 months.
4.  **Transaction Trail:** MT103 or equivalent showing the movement from the UAE bank to your firm’s client account.
5.  **Sanctions/PEP:** Clean search results dated within 30 days of exchange.

**Warning:** If the client is evasive about providing Dubai Land Department documents or corporate registry details, or if the funds arrive from an account not in the client’s name, you must consider filing a **Suspicious Activity Report (SAR)** to the National Crime Agency (NCA) and seeking "Required Terms" (formerly Defence Against Money Laundering) before proceeding to exchange.

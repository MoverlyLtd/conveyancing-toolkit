When a client is using overseas funds from Dubai (UAE) for a UK residential property purchase, you must apply **Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)**. Overseas funds, particularly from major international financial hubs, are a significant trigger for increased scrutiny.

Beyond the standard evidence for the underlying source (e.g., 12 months of statements if the funds are savings), you must carry out the following additional checks:

### 1. Source Country Risk Assessment
You must document a risk assessment of the jurisdiction.
*   **FATF/HMT Lists:** Check the current status of the UAE against the FATF "High-Risk" (Black) and "Increased Monitoring" (Grey) lists, as well as the HM Treasury sanctions list. (Note: While the UAE was removed from the FATF grey list in early 2024, many UK firms still treat it as a higher-risk jurisdiction for EDD purposes).
*   **Transparency International:** Check the Corruption Perceptions Index for the UAE to inform your risk weighting.

### 2. Specific Overseas Funding Evidence
Request the following documents to bridge the gap between the Dubai account and your UK client account:
*   **SWIFT/Wire Transfer Confirmation:** Obtain the full MT103 or equivalent confirmation. This must show the sending bank, the account holder's name (matching your client), the receiving bank, the date, and the exact amount.
*   **Currency Conversion Evidence:** If the funds were held in AED (Dirhams) and converted to GBP, request the FX contract note or bank conversion receipt showing the original currency, the rate applied, and the final GBP amount.
*   **Intermediary Tracing:** If the funds have passed through any intermediary accounts or "correspondent" banks between Dubai and the UK, you must trace each "hop" in the chain.

### 3. Source of Wealth (SOW) Check
Because this transaction triggers EDD, you are required to verify the client's **Source of Wealth**, not just the source of these specific funds.
*   You need to understand and document how the client accumulated their total financial position (e.g., a high-level history of their career, business ownership in Dubai, or investments).
*   Request evidence such as employment contracts, company accounts, or investment portfolio summaries from the last several years.

### 4. Cross-Border Red Flags to Watch For
Review the `red-flags-checklist.md` for specific indicators, including:
*   **Routing:** Are the funds coming directly from the client’s Dubai bank, or are they being routed through a third country (e.g., a holding company in the BVI) with no clear connection to the client?
*   **Third-Party Transfers:** If the funds are being sent by a business or another person in Dubai on the client's behalf, this is a **High Risk** flag. You must perform full CDD on that third party and establish the commercial/family rationale.
*   **Shell Companies:** If the client is using a corporate vehicle based in Dubai to fund the purchase, you must identify the Ultimate Beneficial Owner (UBO) and verify the company's legitimate business activity.

### 5. Regulatory & Reporting Actions
*   **MLRO Sign-off:** As this is an EDD case, you should obtain senior sign-off (from your MLRO or Compliance Officer) before accepting the funds.
*   **Ongoing Monitoring:** Under Regulation 28(12), if the funding structure changes (e.g., the amount from Dubai increases or the sending account changes), you must re-perform these checks.
*   **Record Keeping:** Retain all Dubai-related bank statements, SWIFT messages, and your documented risk assessment for at least 5 years after completion.

Under the **Protocol for Residential Conveyancing**, the seller’s solicitor is expected to **answer pre-contract enquiries promptly and fully**, and not to let matters drift. In practice, the Protocol says:

- **Enquiries should be raised as early as possible**
- The seller’s solicitor should **respond promptly**
- If an enquiry can’t be answered straight away, the seller’s solicitor should **chase the seller or relevant third party** and keep the buyer’s solicitor updated
- **Delays should be explained**
- Parties should act **reasonably and co-operatively** to progress the transaction

A few important points:

1. **There is no hard sanctions regime in the Protocol itself**  
   If the other side simply fails to respond, the Protocol gives you a basis to complain about lack of progress, but it does not usually provide a direct penalty or automatic remedy.

2. **The seller’s solicitor should not ignore enquiries**  
   If an enquiry is genuinely outstanding because they need information from the client, managing agent, lender, local authority pack, etc., they should at least acknowledge it and say when they expect to answer.

3. **You should chase and escalate in writing**  
   It is standard to send a polite but firm chaser, referring to the Protocol and asking for:
   - a response by a specific date/time
   - confirmation whether the delay is due to the client or a third party
   - an indication of whether the transaction can still proceed to exchange

4. **If the delay is unreasonable, you can put them on notice**  
   You can say that if replies are not received by a certain deadline, your client will reconsider whether they are able to continue with the transaction on the current timetable.

5. **The Protocol encourages transparency rather than silence**  
   If a point cannot yet be answered, the proper response is usually:
   - “enquiry noted”
   - “awaiting reply from seller / managing agent / landlord / search provider”
   - “expected by [date]”
   rather than no response at all.

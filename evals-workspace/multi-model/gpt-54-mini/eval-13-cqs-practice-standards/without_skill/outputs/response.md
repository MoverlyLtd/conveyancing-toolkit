For a **new fee earner joining a CQS-accredited firm**, CQS doesn’t just expect general induction; it expects **role-appropriate conveyancing training, supervision, and recorded competence**.

The key CQS-specific onboarding points are:

## 1) They must be brought into the CQS training framework
A new fee earner should be given:
- the firm’s **CQS policies and procedures** relevant to conveyancing,
- the firm’s **client-care and file-handling standards**,
- any **checklists / precedents / workflow systems** used on residential transactions,
- the firm’s **risk and compliance procedures**, including anti-fraud, AML, source of funds, and ID checks,
- any required training on **Lender Handbook compliance**, undertakings, exchange/completion procedures, and SDLT/LR requirements.

CQS expects staff to understand and follow the firm’s conveyancing processes from the outset.

## 2) They should not work unsupervised until competent
A new fee earner should be **closely supervised** at first. In practice, this means:
- they may handle matters only within their demonstrated competence,
- higher-risk work should be reviewed by an experienced supervisor,
- the firm should record any restrictions on the work they can do,
- supervision should continue until the fee earner is considered competent for the tasks assigned.

This is especially important where they are dealing with:
- lender panel work,
- undertakings,
- completion monies,
- client identity and fraud risk,
- title investigations,
- completion and post-completion formalities.

## 3) Their competence should be assessed and recorded
CQS expects firms to be able to show that fee earners are **competent for the work they carry out**. So onboarding should include:
- an initial assessment of their experience,
- identification of any gaps in knowledge,
- a plan for training and support,
- records of completed induction/training,
- supervision notes or sign-off where appropriate.

If they are newly qualified or moving from a different practice area, the firm should be particularly careful not to assume conveyancing competence automatically.

## 4) They should receive CQS-linked mandatory training
Depending on the firm’s internal arrangements and the individual’s role, onboarding should cover the relevant CQS and conveyancing risk topics such as:
- **fraud prevention** and property fraud indicators,
- **AML/sanctions/source of funds** checks,
- **undertakings** and completion risk,
- **mortgage fraud and lender requirements**,
- **conflict checks** and confidentiality,
- **data protection** and secure communications,
- handling **third-party payments** and bank detail changes,
- reporting obligations where something goes wrong.

## 5) Supervision should be proportionate to the risk
CQS is not about a one-size-fits-all induction; it’s about the firm ensuring staff are properly supervised according to experience and risk. A new fee earner may be allowed to progress low-risk tasks earlier, but:
- they should not independently handle complex or high-value transactions until ready,
- they should have a named supervisor or supervisor team,
- escalation routes for unusual issues should be clear.

## 6) The firm should keep evidence for CQS audit/compliance purposes
A CQS-accredited firm should be able to demonstrate:
- induction and training completed,
- role-specific supervision arrangements,
- competence assessment,
- any limitations on the fee earner’s authority,
- ongoing CPD where required.

That evidence may be relevant if the firm is asked to show compliance with CQS standards.

## Practical onboarding checklist for a new fee earner
A sensible CQS-compliant onboarding pack would include:
- firm policies and procedures manual,
- conveyancing workflow/checklists,
- AML/ID/source of funds procedures,
- fraud and cybersecurity guidance,
- Lender Handbook and lender panel requirements,
- undertakings policy,
- file supervision and escalation structure,
- completion/post-completion process guide,
- training log and competence sign-off form.

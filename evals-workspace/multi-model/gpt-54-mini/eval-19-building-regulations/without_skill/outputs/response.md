A loft conversion completed in 2019 without a Building Regulations Completion Certificate, and not covered by a competent person scheme, is a common conveyancing issue. The implications depend on what documentation exists and whether the work was actually compliant.

## Main implications

### 1. Potential Building Regulations breach
A loft conversion typically requires Building Regulations approval because it involves:
- structural alterations,
- fire safety measures,
- stairs/access,
- insulation and ventilation,
- sometimes drainage and electrics.

If there is no completion certificate, it may mean:
- the work was never formally approved,
- inspections were not carried out,
- or the local authority has not signed it off.

### 2. Risk of enforcement
In England and Wales, local authority Building Control can take enforcement action for breach of Building Regulations, but in practice:
- they usually cannot require removal years later unless they can show a continuing non-compliance issue,
- the main risk is that the authority could investigate if the matter is brought to their attention,
- there is also the possibility of problems if the loft is unsafe or materially non-compliant.

Because the work was done in 2019, the risk is lower than for very recent work, but not zero.

### 3. Risk on resale and mortgageability
This can affect:
- buyer confidence,
- lender requirements,
- future saleability.

Many lenders will lend if there is acceptable insurance or evidence of compliance, but some may insist on:
- regularisation,
- an indemnity policy,
- or may decline if the conversion is central to the property’s value or use.

### 4. Use as living accommodation
If the loft is being used as a bedroom or habitable room, the absence of certification is more significant:
- it may not meet fire escape or structural standards,
- it may not legally be considered a compliant habitable room,
- and the valuation may assume less value than expected if it is not sign-off compliant.

### 5. Insurance issues
Buildings insurance may not automatically be invalid, but:
- an undisclosed defect could complicate claims if a loss is linked to the loft conversion,
- insurers may ask questions if there is a known structural or fire safety issue.

## Remedies

### 1. Obtain retrospective Building Regulations approval: “Regularisation”
If the conversion was done without approval, the usual remedy is to apply to the local authority for a **regularisation certificate**.

This involves:
- submitting an application to Building Control,
- paying a fee,
- opening up parts of the work for inspection where necessary,
- possibly carrying out remedial works to satisfy current standards.

Important points:
- This is not guaranteed.
- The council may require invasive inspection.
- If the work does not comply, you may need to upgrade it.

This is usually the most robust solution if the seller wants to resolve the issue properly.

### 2. Indemnity insurance
A **Building Regulations indemnity policy** can sometimes be used where:
- the local authority has not been contacted about the breach,
- there has been no enforcement action,
- no regularisation application has been made.

It protects against financial loss if the authority takes enforcement action, but it does **not** make the loft compliant.

Limitations:
- It does not cover the cost of remedial works if the conversion is defective.
- It usually becomes unavailable if the council is contacted about the issue.
- Some lenders accept it, some do not, especially if the loft is a material part of the property.

### 3. Seller to provide evidence of compliance by another route
Even without a completion certificate, the seller may have:
- approved plans,
- building control inspection records,
- structural calculations,
- engineer’s certificates,
- fire safety evidence,
- invoices from competent contractors,
- completion sign-off correspondence.

These can help, but they are not a substitute for a completion certificate if Building Control never signed off the work.

### 4. Further specialist inspection
A buyer may instruct:
- a chartered building surveyor,
- a structural engineer,
- or a loft conversion specialist.

This can assess:
- whether the loft appears structurally sound,
- whether stairs, fire doors, escape routes, and head height are acceptable,
- whether there are obvious defects.

This won’t replace legal approval, but it helps quantify risk.

### 5. Price renegotiation or retention
If the buyer is concerned, they may:
- renegotiate the price to reflect the risk and possible remediation costs,
- or agree a retention pending production of a certificate or completion of works.

## Key legal distinction: Building Regulations vs Planning Permission
A common point: the lack of a Building Regulations certificate is separate from planning permission.

- Some loft conversions are permitted development and do not need planning permission.
- But they still usually need Building Regulations approval.
- So even if planning was not required, the building control issue remains.

## Practical conveyancing approach
Usually the solicitor would:
1. Ask the seller for any building control paperwork, plans, or inspection records.
2. Check whether a completion certificate can still be obtained or whether regularisation is possible.
3. Consider whether indemnity insurance is acceptable to the lender.
4. If the loft is essential to the property value, recommend a surveyor’s inspection.
5. Report the issue to the buyer and lender before exchange.

## Bottom line
The absence of a 2019 completion certificate means the loft conversion may be technically non-compliant and could create:
- enforcement risk,
- mortgage/lender concerns,
- resale issues,
- and possible safety concerns.

The main remedies are:
- **regularisation** with the local authority, if feasible;
- **indemnity insurance**, if no contact has been made with Building Control and the lender accepts it;
- or **evidence and/or remedial works** if defects are identified.

For **overseas funds from Dubai**, you should do the normal source of funds checks for the **underlying source** of the money, and then add **cross-border / overseas-funds checks** because the funds are coming from abroad.

## Additional checks to carry out

### 1) Source country risk assessment
Assess Dubai/UAE as part of the wider **source country** risk review. Check:
- **HM Treasury sanctions list**
- **FATF high-risk / increased-monitoring jurisdictions**
- Any relevant corruption / country-risk indicators

Even if the UAE is not on a sanctions list, overseas funds still warrant a documented country risk assessment.

### 2) Trace the full chain of funds
You should be able to trace the money from its original source through to your client’s account:
- If the money passed through **intermediary accounts**, get statements for each step
- Check there are no unexplained third-party transfers or layering
- Confirm the funds are in an account in the client’s name, if possible

### 3) Currency conversion evidence
If funds were converted from AED into GBP, obtain:
- **FX contract note** or bank conversion confirmation
- Evidence showing:
  - original currency
  - exchange rate
  - GBP equivalent
  - date of conversion

### 4) SWIFT / wire transfer confirmation
Ask for the transfer evidence showing:
- sending bank
- receiving bank
- account holder names
- amount, currency, and date

This helps verify the funds really came from the stated overseas source.

### 5) Apply the underlying source checks as well
Overseas doesn’t replace the normal checks. Depending on how the money arose, ask for:
- **savings**: 12 months bank statements
- **gift**: giftor ID, gift letter, giftor bank statements, proof of relationship
- **sale proceeds**: sale completion statement, bank receipt, onward tracing
- **inheritance**: grant of probate/administration and distribution evidence
- **employment / bonus / business income**: payslips, employment letter, accounts, dividend vouchers, etc.

### 6) Enhanced Due Diligence
For overseas funds, **EDD is usually appropriate**, especially if:
- the amount is large
- there are multiple transfers or jurisdictions
- the client is a PEP or connected to one
- the source is a company, trust, or third party
- the client is reluctant to provide documents
- the funds arrived very recently before completion

EDD would usually mean:
- more detailed evidence
- senior sign-off / MLRO review
- source of **wealth** check as well as source of funds

### 7) Check for red flags specific to overseas funds
Look out for:
- funds routed through accounts with no obvious connection to the client
- last-minute changes in funding route
- unexplained third-party payments
- conversion through multiple currencies
- use of offshore companies or trusts
- cash being deposited overseas before transfer

## Practical document request list
For Dubai funds, I would usually ask for:
- client’s bank statements showing receipt and holding of the funds
- SWIFT confirmation for the inbound transfer
- FX conversion evidence if converted into GBP
- evidence of the original source of the funds in Dubai
- if a third party is involved, full CDD on that party too

## Key point
Because the funds are overseas, don’t just accept a closing balance or a one-line explanation. You should be able to **trace the money back to a credible, documented source** and explain why the Dubai/UAE route makes sense.

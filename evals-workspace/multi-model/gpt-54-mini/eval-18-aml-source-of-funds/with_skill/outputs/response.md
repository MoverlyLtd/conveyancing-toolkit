With **overseas funds from Dubai**, you should do the **standard source-of-funds checks for the underlying funding source** and then add **overseas-funds-specific enhanced due diligence** on a **risk-based approach**.

## Additional AML checks for Dubai / overseas funds

### 1) Source country risk assessment
Assess the country risk for the funds’ route and origin, including:
- **HM Treasury sanctions list**
- **FATF high-risk / increased-monitoring jurisdictions**
- **Transparency International corruption risk indicators**

Even if Dubai/UAE is not automatically high-risk in every case, you should document a **country risk assessment** based on the actual circumstances.

### 2) SWIFT / wire transfer evidence
Obtain evidence of the transfer into the transaction account, ideally:
- **SWIFT message / wire confirmation**
- Sending bank name
- Sending account holder name
- Receiving bank name and account holder
- Amount, currency, and date

If the money passes through **intermediary accounts**, trace each hop and explain why each account was used.

### 3) Currency conversion evidence
Because the money is coming from overseas, ask for evidence showing:
- Original currency
- Exchange rate used
- GBP equivalent received
- FX contract note or bank conversion confirmation

This is important where funds were converted from AED or another currency before or during remittance.

### 4) Trace the underlying source of funds
You still need the normal evidence for **where the money originally came from**, for example:
- Savings: 12 months statements
- Sale proceeds: sale completion statement, TR1, bank receipt
- Gift: **giftor’s ID, giftor’s bank statements, signed gift letter confirming no obligation to repay**
- Inheritance: probate/letters of administration and receipt evidence
- Business income/dividends: relevant accounts and statements, if applicable

### 5) Consider source of wealth, not just source of funds
For overseas funds, especially if the amounts are large or the client has complex finances, ask how the client built their overall wealth:
- employment
- business ownership
- property portfolio
- investment activity
- family wealth

### 6) Enhanced due diligence
For overseas funds, EDD is often appropriate, especially if there are any red flags, such as:
- third-party funding
- funds routed through multiple accounts
- last-minute changes
- no obvious connection between client and source country
- reluctance to provide documents
- cash-intensive background
- PEP status or close association with a PEP

EDD should include more detailed document review, senior approval, and a written risk assessment.

### 7) Check for third-party involvement
Confirm:
- whose account the money is coming from
- whether the sender is the client, a family member, or another third party
- whether there is any beneficial owner behind the remitting entity

If it is a gift, remember giftor checks are separate from buyer checks.

### 8) Verify consistency across all documents
Make sure names, dates, amounts, and account details are consistent across:
- client explanation
- bank statements
- SWIFT/wire evidence
- FX documents
- sale/inheritance/gift documents

### 9) Ongoing monitoring
Under **Regulation 28(12)**, keep monitoring throughout the retainer. If the funding source, route, or amount changes mid-transaction, re-run the risk assessment and request updated evidence.

### 10) Record-keeping and reporting
- Keep all CDD/EDD records for **5 years** from the end of the business relationship or transaction.
- If anything gives rise to **suspicion**, report internally to your **MLRO/nominated officer** and consider a **SAR to the NCA**.
- If you need to keep acting while suspicious, you may need a **DAML** request: **7 working days** for refusal, then a **31-day moratorium** if refused.

## Practical minimum pack for Dubai funds
I’d usually expect, at minimum:
- explanation of the source of funds
- underlying source documents
- **SWIFT/wire confirmation**
- **FX evidence**
- **country risk assessment**
- evidence tracing any intermediary accounts
- EDD and senior sign-off where risk is elevated

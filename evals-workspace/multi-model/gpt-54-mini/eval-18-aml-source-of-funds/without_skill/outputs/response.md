When the purchase money is coming from Dubai, you should treat this as a **higher AML risk** matter and go beyond the usual source of funds/source of wealth checks. In practice, I would consider the following additional steps.

## 1) Verify the client’s identity more robustly
If the client is based overseas, standard ID may not be enough on its own.

- Obtain **certified copies** of passport and proof of address, or use a reliable electronic ID verification service.
- Make sure certification is from a suitable person and that the certification is current and clear.
- If the client is not physically present, consider whether you need to **video verify** them and record the process.
- Check whether the client is a **PEP**, family member/close associate of a PEP, or otherwise high risk.

## 2) Establish the source of wealth, not just source of funds
For overseas purchasers, AML expectations are usually more demanding.

You should understand:
- **How the overall wealth was accumulated**  
  e.g. salary, business profits, inheritance, sale of a company, property sales, investment portfolio.
- **How long** the client has held that wealth.
- Whether the wealth is consistent with the client’s occupation, business activity, and lifestyle.

Typical evidence may include:
- employment contract / payslips / tax returns
- company accounts and dividend records
- sale agreement for a business or property
- inheritance documentation
- investment statements

## 3) Trace the funds all the way to the remitting account
If money is coming from Dubai, you should usually insist on a clear trail showing the route of funds into the UK client account.

Ask for:
- bank statements showing the funds in the UAE account
- evidence of the transfer from the original source account
- explanation for any intermediate transfers
- confirmation of whose name the sending account is in
- details of any third-party involvement

If the money has been moved through several accounts, you should understand **why** and whether any of those accounts are:
- in the client’s name
- a company account
- a relative’s account
- an exchange house or payment service provider

## 4) Check for third-party funding carefully
Extra caution is needed if:
- a parent, spouse, employer, or other third party is contributing
- funds are being transferred from a company or trust
- the Dubai account is not in the client’s own name

You should obtain:
- the identity and AML checks on the third-party funder
- evidence of the relationship to the client
- reason for the payment
- documentary proof of the third party’s source of wealth/funds

If the funder is a company or trust, you may need to identify:
- the beneficial owners
- controllers
- trustees/protector, where relevant

## 5) Review whether the funds arise from a high-risk sector or jurisdiction
Dubai/UAE is not automatically suspicious, but you should consider risk factors such as:
- money remitted from a **free zone company**
- cash-intensive business
- crypto-related activity
- overseas property trading or speculative investments
- jurisdictions connected to sanctions, corruption, or weak transparency

If there are links to higher-risk countries or sectors, increase your scrutiny.

## 6) Screen for sanctions, adverse media, and criminal risk
Do up-to-date checks on:
- the client
- beneficial owners
- third-party funders
- any company involved
- directors/trustees where relevant

Also consider adverse media searches for:
- fraud
- corruption
- tax evasion
- money laundering
- politically exposed connections
- insolvency or litigation suggesting hidden funding sources

## 7) Be alert to currency exchange and payment route issues
International purchases often involve:
- exchange houses
- money service businesses
- inward remittances in tranches

You should ask:
- why the funds are not coming directly from the client’s main bank account
- whether any currency conversion has occurred
- whether there are multiple smaller transfers, which can indicate structuring or layering
- whether the payment destination is genuinely the client’s own account in the UK

## 8) Confirm tax and residency coherence
Not strictly AML-only, but useful in risk assessment:
- Is the client resident in Dubai/UAE?
- Does the declared wealth match known UK tax filings or overseas filings if relevant?
- Is the transaction consistent with their stated residence, business, and family circumstances?

Inconsistencies may indicate false identity, nominee arrangements, or concealed ownership.

## 9) Consider whether enhanced due diligence is required
If any of the following apply, you should almost certainly treat the matter as **EDD**:
- client is non-face-to-face
- client is overseas
- funds are complex or layered
- there is a PEP connection
- third-party funding
- unusual ownership structures
- adverse media or sanctions concerns
- use of trust/company structures with limited transparency

EDD might include:
- senior management approval
- deeper verification of source of wealth and source of funds
- more documentary evidence
- closer ongoing monitoring
- more detailed file notes explaining the rationale

## 10) Check the legal and regulatory risk of the ownership structure
If the buyer is using:
- a nominee
- offshore company
- trust
- layered company ownership

you should identify the **ultimate beneficial owner** and ensure the structure is lawful and transparent. Be cautious if the structure appears designed to obscure ownership or avoid scrutiny.

## 11) Keep a clear audit trail
You should file note:
- what was received
- why the transaction is higher risk
- why you were satisfied the funds were legitimate
- the checks made on source of wealth/funds
- any explanation for unusual movements or account routes
- any decision to proceed or escalate

## Practical minimum checklist for Dubai funds
At a minimum, I would want:

1. Certified ID and address evidence  
2. PEP/sanctions/adverse media screening  
3. Source of wealth evidence  
4. Source of funds evidence  
5. Bank statements showing accumulation and transfer  
6. Explanation for any third-party or company funding  
7. Beneficial ownership checks if company/trust involved  
8. EDD where risk indicators are present  
9. Clear file note of rationale

## Final point
If anything does not add up, or if the client cannot provide a convincing paper trail, you should consider whether you can safely proceed and whether a **SAR** is required.

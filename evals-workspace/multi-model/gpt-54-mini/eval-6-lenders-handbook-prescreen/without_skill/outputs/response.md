In UK conveyancing, the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook** doesn’t give a single universal rule for “72 years left” or “loft conversion without Building Regs sign-off”; it depends on the **particular lender**. But in practice the Handbook points you to a few core issues you must check and report on:

## 1) Lease term: 72 years remaining
A lease with **72 years unexpired** is generally considered **short** for mainstream lending purposes.

What the Handbook requires in substance is that you:

- **check the lender’s minimum lease term requirement**
- **report if the lease falls below that lender’s threshold**
- **advise if the lease may become a problem for value, marketability or future sale/refinance**
- **check whether the borrower intends to extend the lease and whether that is needed as a condition of the loan**

Many lenders will not lend, or will only lend on special terms, where the lease is below certain thresholds. Common lender policies are roughly:
- minimum **70–85 years unexpired at completion**, or
- minimum term at the **end of the mortgage term** as well

With **72 years remaining**, you should assume there is a **likely lender concern** unless the lender’s panel instructions say otherwise.

### Practical conveyancing point
If the lease is close to or below a lender’s limit, you normally need to:
- **report the short lease to the lender**
- obtain instructions
- consider whether the buyer should negotiate a **lease extension** or price reduction

Also, because a lease under **80 years** triggers **marriage value** on statutory extension, many lenders view this as more serious.

---

## 2) Loft conversion without Building Regulations approval
The Handbook requires you to ensure that the property complies with planning/building control requirements **or** to report any breach and how it is being dealt with.

A loft conversion with **no Building Regulations completion certificate / sign-off** is a **potential title and lending issue**. The lender will usually expect one of the following:

- a **regularisation certificate** from the local authority, or
- an appropriate **indemnity insurance policy**, if acceptable to the lender, or
- satisfactory evidence that the works were carried out lawfully and safely, depending on the facts and lender policy

### Important distinction
- **Building Regulations sign-off** is about structural/safety compliance.
- **Planning permission** may also be relevant, but the absence of Building Regs approval is its own issue.

### What the Handbook generally expects you to do
You should:
- **check whether the conversion required approval**
- **ascertain whether approval was obtained**
- if not, **report the lack of certificate to the lender**
- ask the lender whether they will accept:
  - indemnity insurance, or
  - no action, or
  - require regularisation

### Caution
Indemnity insurance is not always enough where:
- the lender thinks there is a real safety/structural risk
- the property’s value may be affected
- there is evidence of actual non-compliance, not just missing paperwork

---

## 3) Combined risk: short lease + missing Building Regs
Taken together, these are **two separate reportable issues** under lender instructions:

1. **Lease term risk**: 72 years is short and may be below lender minimum.
2. **Title/compliance risk**: loft conversion lacks Building Regulations approval.

A prudent conveyancer would normally **report both issues to the lender before exchange** and wait for instructions. Do not assume you can proceed just because:
- the surveyor has not commented, or
- the seller says it was done ages ago, or
- insurance can be arranged

---

## 4) What you should do in practice
You should:
1. **Check the specific lender’s Part 2 Handbook instructions**
2. **Confirm the exact unexpired term** and whether there is any extension right already in place
3. **Ask for evidence** regarding the loft conversion:
   - completion certificate
   - regularisation certificate
   - building control approvals
   - invoices/specification
4. If none exists, **report to the lender**
5. Consider whether the buyer should:
   - obtain indemnity insurance
   - negotiate a lease extension
   - renegotiate the price
   - withdraw if lender won’t lend

---

## 5) Bottom line
The Handbook effectively means:

- **72 years remaining** is likely to trigger a **lease-term report to the lender**
- **No Building Regs sign-off for the loft conversion** is a **separate defect that must be reported**
- the lender may:
  - refuse to lend,
  - lend subject to conditions, or
  - accept indemnity insurance / further evidence

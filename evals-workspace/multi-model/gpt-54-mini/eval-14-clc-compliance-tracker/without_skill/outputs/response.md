As a CLC-regulated firm, you need to treat this as a **formal complaint under your complaints procedure** and handle it in line with the **Council for Licensed Conveyancers’ complaints handling requirements**.

In practice, you should:

1. **Acknowledge the complaint promptly**
   - Confirm receipt in writing.
   - Identify the matter complained about, even if the client has only raised “delay” generally.

2. **Investigate the complaint properly**
   - Review the file, key dates, correspondence, internal notes, and any reasons for the delay.
   - Speak to the fee earner and supervising lawyer, if relevant.
   - Consider whether the delay was avoidable, whether the client was kept informed, and whether service standards were met.

3. **Respond within your published complaints procedure timescales**
   - The CLC expects you to have a **clear written complaints procedure** that is given to clients.
   - Your procedure should set out who handles complaints and the timetable for dealing with them.
   - If you cannot resolve the complaint quickly, you should keep the client updated and explain why.

4. **Provide a substantive written outcome**
   - Explain your findings and whether you uphold the complaint in whole or in part.
   - If you accept there were failings, apologise and say what you will do to put matters right.
   - This may include:
     - expediting the transaction where possible,
     - offering a reduction in fees,
     - refunding part of the costs,
     - or another appropriate remedy.

5. **Tell the client about escalation rights**
   - Your complaint response should explain the client’s right to take the complaint further if dissatisfied.
   - For CLC-regulated firms, this usually includes escalation to the **Legal Ombudsman** where the complaint falls within its jurisdiction and time limits.

6. **Keep records**
   - Record the complaint, the investigation, any correspondence, the outcome, and any remedial action taken.
   - The CLC can expect evidence that complaints are being monitored and used to improve service.

7. **Use the complaint to identify any regulatory issues**
   - If the delay suggests poor supervision, systemic bottlenecks, or repeated service failures, you should consider whether you need to take internal corrective action.
   - If there is a serious breach of the CLC Code or a pattern of poor conduct, you may need to assess whether additional regulatory obligations are triggered.

### Key point
The CLC does not just expect you to “answer” the complaint — it expects you to have **an effective, accessible complaints procedure**, **deal with the complaint promptly and fairly**, **write to the client with the outcome**, and **signpost the Legal Ombudsman** where appropriate.

Yes, **potentially**, but **72 years remaining is a borderline/important issue** for all three lenders and may affect both **mortgage approval** and **future saleability**.

## The key point
Most mainstream lenders want the lease term to be:
- **long enough at the start of the mortgage**
- and to **extend beyond the mortgage term by a margin**

A lease with **72 years remaining** is not automatically unmortgageable, but it can be a problem because:
- some lenders have **minimum unexpired lease term requirements**
- once a lease drops below **80 years**, the cost of extending it increases sharply because of **marriage value**
- many lenders are cautious if the lease is already “short”

## Halifax
Halifax is generally considered one of the more flexible high-street lenders on lease length, but **72 years may still be acceptable only in some cases** depending on:
- the type of property
- whether it is a flat or house
- the length of the mortgage term
- whether the lease is being extended as part of the transaction
- whether the property is a new-build or ex-local authority, etc.

In practice, Halifax commonly requires the lease to have a reasonable amount of term left beyond the mortgage term, and **72 years may be on the edge**. A conveyancer or broker would normally check Halifax’s current handbook criteria before assuming acceptance.

## Nationwide
Nationwide is also usually fairly mainstream, but it tends to be more specific on lease term. **72 years may be acceptable in some situations, but it is often too short if the mortgage term is long**, especially for flats.

Nationwide may be happier if:
- the lease has a sensible remaining term at completion,
- the mortgage term is shorter,
- or there is a **formal deed of variation / lease extension** being completed.

If you are buying a flat with 72 years left, Nationwide may ask for:
- an assignment of the seller’s statutory lease extension rights, or
- evidence that the lease will be extended, or
- a reduced mortgage term.

## Barclays
Barclays is often **more cautious** about short leases. A lease with **72 years remaining may be problematic** and could fall below their preferred threshold in many cases.

Barclays may require:
- a longer unexpired term than 72 years, or
- a lease extension before or at completion, or
- that the property meets stricter criteria under their mortgage conditions.

So among these three, **Barclays is the most likely to object** to a 72-year lease.

## Practical reality
Even if one of these lenders will lend, a 72-year lease is still usually a red flag because:
- the lease is already below 80 years
- the extension premium will generally be higher than it would be today
- buyers can struggle to resell without extending
- some solicitors will flag it as a title issue requiring action

## What usually happens in practice
If you’re buying:
1. **Ask the lender or broker to check the exact product criteria**
2. **Ask your solicitor to review the lease term against the lender’s handbook**
3. Consider negotiating:
   - a **price reduction**
   - a **seller-funded lease extension**
   - or the seller serving and assigning a **section 42 notice** if eligible

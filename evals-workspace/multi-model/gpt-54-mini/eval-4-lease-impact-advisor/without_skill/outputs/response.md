A 68-year lease is **short enough to matter materially to buyers and lenders**.

Key points you should know as the agent:

### 1) It is below the 80-year threshold
In the UK, once a lease drops below **80 years**, the cost of extending it usually rises sharply because of **marriage value** being payable on a statutory lease extension. That means:
- buyers will often see it as a “short lease”,
- the property may be harder to mortgage,
- and the sale price can be affected.

At **68 years**, this flat is clearly in short-lease territory.

### 2) Mortgageability may be an issue
Many mainstream lenders are cautious with short leases. Requirements vary, but at 68 years:
- some lenders may refuse outright,
- others may require a minimum unexpired term at completion or at mortgage expiry,
- lenders often want the lease to extend beyond a certain point after the mortgage term ends.

So you should **check likely lender criteria early**, because this can affect your buyer pool.

### 3) Buyers may expect either a price discount or a lease extension
A flat at £425,000 with 68 years remaining will usually be valued with the lease length heavily in mind. Buyers may factor in:
- the premium for extending the lease,
- legal/valuation costs,
- and the time and risk involved.

Some buyers may only proceed if:
- the seller starts the statutory extension process, or
- the seller agrees a price reduction reflecting the short lease.

### 4) Statutory lease extension rights may be relevant
If the seller is a qualifying leaseholder, they may have the right to extend the lease by **90 years** and reduce the ground rent to a **peppercorn** under the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993.

Important practical point:
- A buyer normally has to own the flat for **2 years** before exercising this right.
- But the seller may be able to **start the process and assign the benefit** to the buyer on completion, which can make the flat much more saleable.

This is often a key selling point for a short-lease flat.

### 5) You should check the ground rent and lease terms too
Lease length is only part of the picture. Also check:
- current ground rent and whether it doubles or escalates,
- service charge level and history,
- any restrictive covenants,
- remaining term at completion vs. now,
- whether the lease extension premium estimate has been obtained.

A flat with 68 years remaining and a harsh ground rent clause can be significantly harder to market.

### 6) Marketing strategy matters
For a lease this short, it helps to be transparent in the listing and advise buyers to:
- get mortgage advice early,
- take legal advice early,
- and obtain a lease extension quote/valuation if serious.

If the seller is willing, you may want to market it as:
- “68 years remaining, suitable for cash buyers or purchasers comfortable with lease extension costs,” or
- “seller may consider serving notice for lease extension.”

### 7) As a rough practical rule
At **68 years**, most informed buyers will treat the flat as requiring an extension soon, and the nearer the lease gets to 60 years and below, the more difficult and expensive it becomes.

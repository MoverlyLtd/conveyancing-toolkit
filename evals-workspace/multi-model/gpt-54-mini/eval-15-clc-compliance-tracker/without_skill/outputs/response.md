Under the **CLC (Council for Licensed Conveyancers) Code of Conduct and Accounts Code**, your obligations around **client money** are strict and broadly revolve around **holding it safely, using it only for the proper purpose, accounting for it accurately, and returning it promptly when it is no longer needed**.

Here’s a practical summary of the main obligations.

## 1) Keep client money separate from business money
You must:
- **Pay client money into a designated client account** promptly.
- **Not mix** client money with your own office/business funds.
- Only keep money in the client account if it is genuinely **client money** and needs to be retained there.

Client money generally includes money you hold:
- on behalf of a client,
- for a third party in connection with a conveyancing transaction,
- or money that is intended for the payment of costs/disbursements connected with the matter.

## 2) Use client money only for the correct client/matter
You must ensure money held for one client or matter is **not used for another** unless:
- you have clear authority,
- it is permitted by the rules,
- and you remain able to account properly.

In practice, you should maintain **proper client-ledger records** for each matter so you can always identify:
- who the money belongs to,
- what it is for,
- and how much is held.

## 3) Keep proper accounting records
CLC regulation requires accurate and up-to-date records, typically including:
- client ledgers,
- cash books,
- bank statements/reconciliations,
- transfer records,
- receipts and payments,
- and supporting documentation.

You must be able to show at any time:
- the total client money held,
- the balance attributable to each client/matter,
- and that the total of client ledger balances matches the bank account balance.

## 4) Reconcile client accounts regularly
You must carry out **regular client account reconciliations** to ensure:
- the client bank account balance matches your accounting records,
- any discrepancies are investigated promptly,
- and any shortage or overage is corrected immediately.

This is a core compliance requirement because failures here often indicate misuse, error, or poor controls.

## 5) Promptly account to the client and pay out money due
You should:
- **account to clients promptly** for money received,
- **pay out money only when properly due** and authorised,
- and **return surplus funds** to the client without unnecessary delay.

If the transaction is complete and there is a balance left over, you should not retain it unless there is a lawful reason to do so.

## 6) Only withdraw money when permitted
You may only withdraw client money from the client account for proper purposes, such as:
- paying completion monies to another party,
- paying SDLT/LTT or Land Registry fees,
- paying disbursements,
- transferring agreed fees if you are entitled to do so and the client has been informed,
- or refunding the client.

You must not:
- make unexplained cash withdrawals,
- transfer client money to office account without proper entitlement,
- or use client money to fund the firm’s working capital.

## 7) Do not breach the “no overdraft” principle
Your client account must not be used in a way that creates a **debit balance** or shortage.  
If a client account is overdrawn or a client ledger is in deficit, that is a serious breach and may amount to misuse of client money.

If an error or bank charge creates a shortfall, it needs immediate correction and investigation.

## 8) Protect client money and minimise risk
You should have systems and controls to safeguard money, including:
- bank account controls and dual authorisation,
- segregation of duties where possible,
- identity and authority checks before paying out,
- anti-fraud procedures,
- and internal checks on bank details changes.

A common compliance risk in conveyancing is fraud involving false change-of-bank-details emails, so robust verification procedures are essential.

## 9) Handle residual balances properly
If money remains on a file after completion and you cannot immediately return it because:
- the client cannot be traced,
- the amount is small,
- or the entitlement is uncertain,

you should follow a proper residual-balance procedure in line with CLC requirements and your firm’s policy. This usually involves:
- making reasonable attempts to contact the client,
- keeping records,
- and dealing with the funds in accordance with the rules rather than simply leaving them dormant indefinitely.

## 10) Notify and report breaches
If there is a **serious breach** involving client money, such as:
- a client account shortage,
- misappropriation,
- unexplained transfers,
- or inability to account for money,

you will usually need to **report it promptly** to the CLC and take immediate remedial action. The CLC expects openness and effective incident management.

## 11) Ensure compliance with undertakings and transaction timing
In conveyancing, client money often relates to completion undertakings, redemption monies, SDLT, registry fees, and stakeholder arrangements. You must ensure:
- funds are held until the relevant contractual or legal conditions are met,
- completion can be funded properly,
- and any undertakings given are capable of being honoured.

## 12) Have a written policy and trained staff
You should have:
- a written **accounts/client money policy**,
- clear authority levels,
- documented procedures for receipts, transfers, and payments,
- staff training,
- and periodic internal review.

A failure by a cashier or accounts clerk is still the firm’s regulatory responsibility.

---

# In short
Your core CLC client money duties are to:
- **hold client money separately, safely, and accurately,**
- **only use it for the right purpose and with proper authority,**
- **keep full accounting records and reconcile regularly,**
- **return or pay it out promptly when due,**
- and **report and remedy any breach immediately.**

You should advise your client that this is a **potential title / compliance issue**, but not necessarily a deal-breaker.

### What the issue is
Replacement windows fitted in 2018 in England/Wales should usually have been either:

1. **installed by a FENSA/CERTASS registered installer**, with the appropriate self-certification certificate, or  
2. **notified to and approved by Building Control**.

If neither exists, then from a conveyancing perspective you have an **evidential gap**: there is no formal proof that the windows complied with the relevant Building Regulations at the time of installation.

### What to do next
I would advise:

1. **Ask the seller for evidence**
   - invoice / contract from the local company
   - installer details
   - any guarantees or warranty documents
   - any correspondence showing FENSA registration or Building Control involvement

2. **Check whether the installer was actually FENSA registered**
   - sometimes sellers say “local company” but the installer may have been registered and the certificate can still be reissued or traced

3. **If no proof can be obtained, consider a retrospective solution**
   - **indemnity insurance** is the usual conveyancing answer if the windows appear physically satisfactory and there is no suggestion of enforcement
   - alternatively, the seller can apply for **retrospective Building Regulations approval** through the local authority building control department

### Practical point on indemnity insurance
For replacement windows, **indemnity insurance is commonly accepted** by lenders and buyers, provided:
- there has been no approach to the local authority already,
- no enforcement action is known or threatened,
- and the issue is simply lack of documentation.

However, indemnity insurance **does not prove compliance**; it only covers the risk of enforcement and associated loss if the authority later takes issue.

### Important caution
If the seller, buyer, or agent has **already contacted Building Control or the council** about the missing certificate, that may **invalidate the availability of indemnity insurance** in many cases. So it is important to check whether any enquiries have already been made.

### What I would tell your client
A sensible summary would be:

> “The absence of a FENSA certificate or building control approval means we cannot verify compliance with Building Regulations for the replacement windows. I recommend we seek documentary evidence from the seller/installer first. If none is available, we should usually ask the seller to provide a suitable indemnity policy, or consider retrospective building control approval if the buyer is prepared to accept that process.”

### If your client is the buyer
If acting for the buyer, I would also say:
- this is **fairly common** in conveyancing,
- it is often resolved by **indemnity insurance**,
- but if the windows are clearly defective or the property is otherwise unusual, the buyer may want a **surveyor’s inspection** or insist on proper regularisation.

### If your client is the seller
If acting for the seller, the advice is:
- gather all paperwork you can,
- ask the installer if they can reissue the certificate or confirm FENSA registration,
- if not, expect the buyer’s solicitor to request indemnity insurance or building control regularisation.

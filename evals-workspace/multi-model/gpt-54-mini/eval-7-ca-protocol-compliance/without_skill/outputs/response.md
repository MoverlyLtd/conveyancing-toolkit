Under the **Conveyancing Association (CA) Protocol**, a rear extension built in **2021** with **no planning permission record** is treated as a **potential title/consent issue that must be investigated and reported on**, rather than something to ignore because it may be “obviously” low risk.

In practical terms, the Protocol approach is:

### 1) Raise the issue with the seller’s solicitor
You should ask for:
- the **planning permission** if it was required,
- **building regulations approval/certificates**,
- any **completion certificate**,
- and any **certificate of lawfulness** if the seller says permission was not needed.

### 2) Check whether planning permission was actually required
A rear extension may have been:
- **permitted development**, or
- subject to a planning application that was never found because the local authority record search is incomplete or the works were carried out under **prior approval**/permitted development rights.

If the extension was built in 2021, it is still well within the period where the local authority could take enforcement action, so it is not usually safe to assume the absence of records means no issue.

### 3) Check building regulations separately
Even if planning permission was not required, the extension may still have needed **building regs approval**. The CA Protocol expects conveyancers to verify that the property complies with the usual title and regulatory checks.

### 4) Report to the buyer
If the documents cannot be produced, the conveyancer should **advise the buyer clearly** that:
- there is **no evidence of planning consent** on file,
- the extension may have been unauthorised,
- enforcement risk may remain,
- and the lender may require a solution.

### 5) Consider an indemnity policy — but only if appropriate
The usual practical response, where permission/certificates cannot be obtained, is often:
- **planning indemnity insurance** and/or
- **building regulations indemnity insurance**.

But under standard conveyancing practice, insurance is only suitable if:
- no contact has been made with the local authority about regularising the works, and
- no approach is planned that would invalidate the policy.

### 6) Do not just rely on indemnity if the issue is material
If the extension is substantial or visibly significant, the Protocol would expect the conveyancer to consider whether insurance alone is enough, or whether the buyer should:
- insist on retrospective regularisation, or
- treat it as a matter for negotiation/withdrawal.

### Bottom line
The CA Protocol would expect the conveyancer to **investigate, obtain evidence, report the lack of planning records to the buyer, and resolve the issue if possible**. If no permission can be found, the usual fallback is **indemnity insurance**, provided it is appropriate and not undermined by contact with the local authority.

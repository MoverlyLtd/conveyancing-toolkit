A few points to consider, but the short answer is: **I would not assume the covenant is unenforceable just because the benefiting neighbour has extended**. It may, however, give you a **strong practical and legal argument** that the covenant has been **discharged, waived, abandoned, or is otherwise unlikely to be enforced**.

## 1. Start with the wording and scope of the covenant
You say it prevents **“any alteration to the external appearance”**. I would first check:

- the exact drafting,
- whether it is a **full prohibition** or subject to consent,
- whether it applies to the whole property or just certain parts,
- whether “alteration” would clearly include a rear extension,
- and whether there are any qualifying words such as “without the consent of the beneficiary”.

A rear extension is very likely to be caught if it changes the external appearance.

## 2. The neighbour’s extension is highly relevant, but not decisive
The fact that the **benefiting property has itself had a large extension built in 2015** may support an argument that:

- the beneficiary has **waived** the covenant,
- the covenant has been **abandoned** through a pattern of non-enforcement,
- or the beneficiary would face difficulty proving prejudice.

But legally, **one breach by the beneficiary does not automatically extinguish the covenant**. In particular:

- A restrictive covenant can still subsist even if the dominant owner has breached a similar covenant on its own land.
- The beneficiary’s own extension does **not necessarily amount to release**.
- Unless there is a **deed of release**, formal variation, or evidence showing the covenant is no longer intended to be enforced, it remains potentially enforceable.

That said, if the beneficiary has carried out a **material extension** in breach of the same or similar covenant, that is often strong evidence that the covenant has not been actively protected and may be vulnerable to challenge.

## 3. Check whether the covenant is actually enforceable by this neighbour
You should verify:

- who has the benefit of the covenant,
- whether the benefiting land can be identified with certainty,
- whether there has been any assignment of benefit,
- whether the covenant is annexed to land or merely personal,
- and whether the neighbour still owns the benefitting land.

If the covenant is only enforceable by a particular plot owner, you need to confirm that your neighbour is in fact that party.

## 4. Consider whether the covenant might be obsolete or susceptible to modification/discharge
If the surrounding area has changed and the beneficiary has extended, there may be scope to argue that the covenant is **obsolete** or that its continuance is unreasonable.

The main route is an application to the **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)** under **section 84 Law of Property Act 1925**, on grounds such as:

- the covenant is **obsolete**,
- it impedes some reasonable use of the land,
- or the beneficiary would not suffer adequate injury from discharge/modification.

The fact that the neighbouring property has already been extended would help an argument that:

- the character of the area has changed,
- the covenant is no longer serving the original purpose,
- and/or refusal to allow your client’s rear extension would be unreasonable.

However, section 84 applications are not quick, cheap or guaranteed.

## 5. Practical advice: seek release/consent first
Before moving to an application or proceeding with the build, I would usually advise:

1. **Approach the beneficiary** for a formal deed of release or written consent.
2. If they refuse, ask on what basis they say the covenant still matters.
3. Consider whether the neighbour’s own extension creates leverage for a negotiated release.
4. If consent is given, ensure it is:
   - in writing,
   - from the correct beneficiary,
   - and ideally by **deed** if a formal release is intended.

If consent is being used, check whether the covenant wording allows consent and whether the consent must be recorded or registered.

## 6. Title indemnity insurance may be possible, but only if there is no contact with the beneficiary
If the client wants to proceed without dealing with the neighbour, a common conveyancing solution is **restrictive covenant indemnity insurance**.

But note:

- insurance is usually only available if there has been **no approach** to the beneficiary about the breach,
- and no prior dispute or notice.

Because the neighbour has already built an extension, the risk profile may be more nuanced, but insurance may still be available depending on the facts and insurer appetite.

## 7. The neighbour’s extension may indicate acquiescence, but watch limitation and enforcement
The beneficiary’s own extension in 2015 may also raise issues of:

- **acquiescence**,
- **delay**,
- or whether they are estopped from objecting.

But again, this is fact-sensitive. A neighbour’s past breach does not automatically prevent them enforcing the covenant against your client, unless their conduct makes enforcement inequitable.

## 8. What I would advise overall
My advice would be:

- **Do not treat the neighbour’s extension as a free pass.**
- Obtain and review the exact covenant wording and title details.
- Check who has benefit and whether they are still entitled to enforce.
- Consider whether the 2015 extension gives grounds to argue waiver, abandonment, or changed character.
- Seek a **deed of release/consent** if feasible.
- If not, consider **indemnity insurance** or a **section 84 application**, depending on appetite for risk, timing and cost.

## Bottom line
The beneficiary’s own large extension is helpful evidence, but **not enough on its own** to conclude your client can lawfully build the rear extension. The safest route is either:

- a **formal release/consent**, or
- if that is not possible, a risk-managed approach via **insurance** or **section 84** if the client wants to press ahead.

For a **CQS renewal audit**, the assessor will usually want to see that your firm is not just “paper compliant” but that your **conveyancing processes, controls, supervision, and training** are actually operating in practice. The exact audit focus can vary a bit depending on your firm profile and any previous issues, but the key areas are broadly consistent.

Below is a practical checklist of what they will usually examine and the evidence you should have ready.

---

# 1) Practice quality and file handling

## What they’ll check
- Whether conveyancing work is being handled by competent staff
- Whether files show a consistent, controlled process from opening to completion
- Whether supervision is effective
- Whether risk points are identified and managed
- Whether checklists/templates are being used properly

## Evidence to have ready
- A sample of completed conveyancing files, usually covering:
  - residential sale
  - purchase
  - re-mortgage
  - leasehold matter
  - possibly transfer of equity or new build if applicable
- File opening documents and ID/AML checks
- Client care letters and terms of business
- Matter risk assessments / file risk grading
- Supervision notes and review records
- Internal checklists used at each stage
- Evidence of post-completion procedures and reconciliation

## Common issues they look for
- Missing signed client care letters
- Inadequate supervision on files handled by less experienced staff
- No documented progression checks
- Key undertakings not properly tracked
- No evidence of checks on source of funds/source of wealth where required

---

# 2) CQS Core Practice Management Standards

The assessor will expect your firm to show compliance with the **CQS Core Practice Management Standards** and the related conveyancing quality expectations.

## What they’ll check
- Governance and accountability for the CQS scheme
- Systems and controls for conveyancing work
- Clear roles and responsibilities
- Compliance culture
- File and document management
- Complaints handling and lessons learned

## Evidence to have ready
- Organisational structure / reporting lines
- Named CQS Responsible Officer and key contacts
- Policies and procedures manual
- Staff role descriptions
- File review / audit reports
- Management meeting minutes showing conveyancing/compliance issues discussed
- Action plans from previous audits or internal reviews

---

# 3) AML, source of funds and source of wealth

This is one of the most scrutinised areas.

## What they’ll check
- Risk-based AML procedures
- Client identification and verification
- Beneficial ownership checks where relevant
- Source of funds and source of wealth enquiries
- Ongoing monitoring and escalation of red flags
- Sanctions and PEP screening, if used
- Compliance with the firm’s AML policy and the Money Laundering Regulations

## Evidence to have ready
- AML policy and procedure
- Firm-wide risk assessment
- Matter risk assessments
- ID/verification evidence on files
- Source of funds evidence:
  - bank statements
  - sale proceeds evidence
  - savings evidence
  - gifted deposit letters and donor ID where relevant
- Source of wealth evidence where higher risk
- Screening results, if your systems produce them
- SAR/escalation procedures and records of internal escalations
- Training records for AML training

## Common gaps
- Accepting funds without adequate explanation of origin
- Relying only on ID and not doing source of funds checks
- Incomplete third-party funding checks
- No documented rationale for low-risk decisions

---

# 4) Mortgage lender compliance

## What they’ll check
- Adherence to lender instructions
- Correct handling of mortgage conditions
- Proper reporting of title defects, incentives, gifted deposits, lease issues, etc.
- Avoidance of conflicts
- Compliance with panel obligations
- Evidence that undertakings and completions are managed correctly

## Evidence to have ready
- A selection of files showing lender instructions followed
- Checks against the CML/Lenders’ Handbook requirements
- Reports to lender where issues arose
- Disclosure of incentives on new build or part-exchange transactions
- Evidence of gifted deposit reporting and approval
- Completion statements and undertakings
- Panel membership records, if relevant

## Common issues
- Failing to report lease defects or title issues to the lender
- Not advising lender of gifted deposits or incentives when required
- Proceeding without checking special lender conditions
- Poor control of undertakings

---

# 5) Duty of care, client care and communications

## What they’ll check
- Clear advice at the outset and throughout the matter
- Realistic timescales and costs information
- Updates to clients
- Proper documentation of advice
- Managing expectations, especially in chains and where delays occur

## Evidence to have ready
- Client care letters
- Costs estimates / fee information
- Email/written advice on key issues
- Attendance notes
- Complaint examples and how they were handled
- File notes showing updates/chase activity

## Common issues
- Generic client care letters not tailored to the matter
- Poor records of telephone advice
- No evidence of explaining risks or delays
- Complaints not logged or reviewed for trends

---

# 6) Conflict checking and confidentiality

## What they’ll check
- Initial and ongoing conflict checks
- Proper handling of joint clients and family transactions
- Information barriers where relevant
- Confidentiality protections
- Checks where acting for lender and borrower in the same transaction

## Evidence to have ready
- Conflict checking policy
- Conflict search results or logs
- Procedures for escalation of conflicts
- Attendance notes on any conflict decisions
- Confidentiality/information barrier procedures if used

---

# 7) Undertakings and post-completion controls

This is a big audit point because failures here can create serious risk.

## What they’ll check
- Undertakings are recorded, monitored, and complied with
- Completion funds are handled properly
- Redemption undertakings are tracked
- SDLT and Land Registry post-completion work is done promptly
- Reconciliations are carried out
- No undertakings are left open without explanation

## Evidence to have ready
- Undertaking register or tracker
- Completion checklist
- Bank reconciliation records
- Post-completion checklist
- SDLT submission confirmations
- Land Registry application receipts / title updates
- Evidence of redemption statement checks and mortgage discharge follow-up
- File closure checklist confirming all undertakings satisfied

## Common issues
- No central undertaking log
- Delayed SDLT or AP1 submissions
- Failure to chase lender discharge
- Missing evidence that completion monies were correctly transferred and reconciled

---

# 8) Financial controls and client account

## What they’ll check
- Segregation of duties
- Proper client account operation
- Receipt, transfer, and holding of client money
- Reconciliations
- Approval controls for payments
- Anti-fraud measures

## Evidence to have ready
- Client account procedures
- Recent three-way reconciliations
- Payment authorisation policy
- Evidence of dual control for transfers, if applicable
- Bank mandate / signatory list
- Fraud prevention procedures
- Procedures for changes to bank details and verification of payment instructions

## Common issues
- Weak controls on bank detail changes
- Poor reconciliation practice
- Insufficient oversight of client money movements
- Reliance on email instructions without call-back verification

---

# 9) Fraud prevention and cyber security

Given conveyancing fraud risk, this is often closely examined.

## What they’ll check
- Verification of bank details and change requests
- Staff awareness of invoice redirection fraud and spoofing
- Email security and safe transfer controls
- Use of warnings to clients about fraud risks
- Reported incidents and lessons learned

## Evidence to have ready
- Fraud prevention policy
- Cyber security policy
- Call-back verification procedures
- Client warnings/templates about bank detail changes
- Staff training records on phishing/fraud
- Records of any incidents and how they were handled

---

# 10) Training and competence

## What they’ll check
- Staff training is current and relevant
- Conveyancing staff understand key risk areas
- Training is logged and monitored
- New staff are inducted properly
- CPD is meaningful and not just tick-box

## Evidence to have ready
- Training matrix for all staff
- Induction records
- CQS-specific training records
- AML and fraud training records
- Technical update logs
- Supervision/training plans for junior staff
- CPD logs

---

# 11) Complaints and claims handling

## What they’ll check
- Whether complaints are logged, investigated, and learned from
- Whether the firm identifies trends
- How near-misses, negligence issues, and claims are escalated
- Whether there is a process for notifying insurers where needed

## Evidence to have ready
- Complaints policy
- Complaints register
- Sample complaint files/resolution letters
- Root cause analysis or lessons learned
- Claims/incident log
- Evidence of reporting to insurers where appropriate

---

# 12) Equality, diversity and inclusion / conduct / professionalism

Depending on the current audit approach, the assessor may also want to see that professional conduct standards are embedded.

## Evidence to have ready
- Equality and diversity policy/training records
- Code of conduct / staff handbook
- Dignity and behaviour policy
- Whistleblowing policy
- Disciplinary procedures

---

# What to have ready as a “renewal audit pack”

A good practical approach is to prepare a single folder, physical or digital, with:

## Governance documents
- CQS Responsible Officer details
- firm structure and conveyancing team list
- policies and procedures manual
- compliance calendar
- internal audit/review schedule

## Core compliance policies
- AML policy and firm-wide risk assessment
- conflict policy
- client account and financial controls policy
- fraud/cyber policy
- complaints policy
- undertakings/post-completion policy
- training and supervision policy

## File evidence
Prepare a representative sample of files showing:
- client care/terms
- AML/ID/SOF evidence
- conflict checks
- lender reporting
- advice notes
- undertakings
- completion/post-completion documents
- closure evidence

## Management evidence
- training logs
- supervision records
- file review results
- meeting minutes
- action plans and follow-up evidence
- complaints and incident logs

---

# How the assessor is likely to test you

They will often ask:
- “Show me how you open a conveyancing file.”
- “How do you decide what source of funds evidence is sufficient?”
- “How do you monitor undertakings?”
- “How do you know lender instructions were followed?”
- “What do you do when bank details change?”
- “How do you supervise junior fee earners?”
- “Show me evidence of recent file reviews and any corrective action.”

So it helps to be able to show not only policies, but actual examples of them being used.

---

# Practical preparation tips

- Do a **mock file review** on 5–10 recent matters.
- Check for:
  - missing ID/AML documents
  - incomplete source of funds evidence
  - unsigned client care letters
  - absent lender reporting notes
  - unrecorded undertakings
  - late post-completion work
- Review your **previous CQS renewal feedback** and confirm remedial actions were completed.
- Make sure your staff can explain your procedures clearly.
- Ensure your policies are **up to date and consistent** with what actually happens on files.

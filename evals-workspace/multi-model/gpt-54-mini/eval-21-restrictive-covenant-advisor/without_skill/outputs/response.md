In England and Wales, the starting point is that a restrictive covenant **against business use** can still be enforceable even if it is very old and has never been enforced. A “home office” may or may not breach it, depending on the exact wording and what is actually being done from the property.

## 1) First step: check the wording of the covenant
The key question is whether the covenant prohibits:

- **“business use” generally**, or
- only **use that causes nuisance**, customer visits, signage, traffic, alterations, etc.

A small, internal home office with:
- no public access,
- no signage,
- no extra traffic,
- no staff or deliveries beyond normal domestic use,
- and no material change to the character of occupation,

is often arguable as **ancillary to residential use** rather than a true business use.  
But that is a **risk assessment**, not a guarantee.

## 2) Practical options

### Option A: Proceed on the basis it is not a breach
If the work is genuinely home-based and incidental to living there, some practitioners take the view that there is **no material breach**. This is often the cheapest route, but the risk is that a beneficiary of the covenant could later argue otherwise.

This approach is more defensible where:
- the property remains primarily a home,
- no external business activity takes place,
- no customers, suppliers, employees, or signs are involved,
- and the business is desk-based/silent.

### Option B: Obtain a deed of release / variation
If the person who benefits from the covenant can be identified and is willing to cooperate, they can:
- **release** the covenant,
- or **vary** it to permit home office use.

This is the cleanest legal solution, but often difficult because:
- the beneficiary may be unknown,
- there may be multiple beneficiaries,
- or they may demand a substantial premium.

### Option C: Apply to the Upper Tribunal under section 84 Law of Property Act 1925
If the covenant is obsolete, impedes reasonable use, or nobody benefits in a meaningful way, an application can sometimes be made to the **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)** to discharge or modify it.

Grounds commonly relied on include:
- the covenant is **obsolete**,
- it impedes **reasonable use** and provides no practical benefit of substantial value,
- or the beneficiaries have **consented/waived**.

This can work, but it is:
- slower,
- more expensive,
- and uncertain.

### Option D: Take indemnity insurance
This is the common transactional solution where:
- the covenant is potentially problematic,
- but no one wants to spend time/ money on formal release or tribunal proceedings.

Insurance typically covers:
- legal costs of defending a claim,
- compensation/settlement to a beneficiary,
- diminution in value in some policies,
- and sometimes costs of injunctive proceedings.

Important:
- Insurance does **not** make the covenant disappear.
- It just covers the financial risk of enforcement.

## 3) Does non-enforcement since 1965 help?
Yes, but only to a limited extent.

A covenant can become unenforceable if:
- the beneficiary has clearly **abandoned** it, or
- their conduct amounts to **waiver/acquiescence**, or
- circumstances have changed so much that enforcement would be unreasonable.

However, mere non-enforcement over decades does **not automatically** mean it is dead. If no breach has previously occurred, the fact that no one enforced it may just mean there was nothing to enforce.

## 4) What about a small home office?
In practice, lenders and conveyancers often accept that ordinary home-working is not the sort of “business use” intended to be prohibited, especially if:
- it’s internal,
- no external impact,
- and the property is still used mainly as a dwelling.

But if the covenant is drafted broadly, it may technically catch:
- any business activity from the premises,
- even if no visitors attend.

So the answer depends heavily on the drafting.

## 5) Typical indemnity insurance cost
For a routine restrictive covenant policy on a residential property in England and Wales, premiums are often:

- **£100–£300** for straightforward, low-risk residential transactions,
- sometimes **£300–£600** if the risk is a bit higher or the property value is greater,
- occasionally more for unusual risks or higher-value homes.

For a covenant specifically about **business use/home office**, a typical premium might still be in the **low hundreds of pounds**, often around:
- **£150–£400** for standard residential cover,
- but it depends on:
  - property value,
  - whether there has already been any approach to the covenant holder,
  - whether there has been any breach,
  - whether the business activity is purely incidental,
  - and the insurer’s assessment of enforceability.

## 6) Critical point about insurance
You usually **must not contact** the person with the benefit of the covenant before taking out indemnity insurance, because that can:
- alert them to the issue,
- and potentially invalidate the policy or make it unavailable.

If you want insurance, the usual approach is:
1. assess whether the activity is likely to be a breach,
2. obtain a policy **before** any approach to beneficiaries,
3. then proceed cautiously.

## 7) Bottom line
For a **small, not externally visible home office with no visitors**, the most common routes are:

- argue it is **not a material business use** and continue; or
- obtain **indemnity insurance** if the client wants cover against challenge.

If the covenant is particularly strict, or if a lender/conveyancer insists, then the cleaner options are:
- a **deed of release**, or
- an **Upper Tribunal application**.

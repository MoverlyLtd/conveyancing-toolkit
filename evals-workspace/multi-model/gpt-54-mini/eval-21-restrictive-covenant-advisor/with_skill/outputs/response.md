First, one important check: **has anyone approached the covenant beneficiary at all** — by letter, phone, email, informal chat, or even via a planning application consultation? If yes, the **“no approach” rule** means **indemnity insurance is no longer available**. If no, keep it that way until the strategy is decided.

On the facts you’ve given, this looks like a **very low-risk / possibly no-breach** situation:

- A **small home office**, with **no clients visiting** and **no visible external change**, is usually unlikely to amount to a prohibited “business use” in practical terms.
- If the covenant is a typical “no trade or business” restriction, enforcement risk is generally much lower where the activity is indistinguishable from ordinary residential use.
- The covenant being from **1965** and **never enforced** helps the client’s position a little, though it does **not** automatically extinguish it.

## Options

### 1) Do nothing
Often the most sensible route if:
- the office is purely internal,
- there’s no signage, deliveries, staff, or client visits,
- and the use remains residential in character.

This is especially reasonable where the activity is **invisible** and non-intrusive.

### 2) Indemnity insurance
If the client wants comfort, insurance may be available **provided no approach has been made**.

- Typical cost for restrictive covenant indemnity insurance: **£50–300**
- For a low-risk, hidden home-office use, it would usually be at the **lower end** of that range.

Insurance is often used where there is some residual risk but no desire to take formal steps.

### 3) Apply to the Upper Tribunal under **s84 Law of Property Act 1925**
If the covenant is genuinely preventing reasonable use, or if insurance is unavailable, the statutory route is an application to the **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)** under **s84 LPA 1925**.

Potential grounds:
- **s84(aa)** — the covenant impedes reasonable use of the land and does not secure practical benefits of substantial value, or money compensation is adequate
- **s84(a)** — the covenant is obsolete, if the character of the neighbourhood or property has changed
- **s84(c)** — no injury to the beneficiary, if discharge/modification would not harm them

But for a discreet home office, a Tribunal application would usually be disproportionate unless the client is planning a more visible or intensive business use.

- Typical Tribunal/application cost: **£1,000–5,000+**
- Timescale: often **6–18 months**

### 4) Negotiate a release
If the beneficiary is identifiable and contactable, a negotiated release is another route.

But note: if you want to preserve insurance eligibility, **do not contact the beneficiary first**.

## Practical view
Because this is:

- and the use is **internal, non-visible, and without client traffic**,

the risk is likely low enough that many conveyancers would either:
- **do nothing**, or

A 95-year lease is **usually not a red flag in itself**, but it **does need checking carefully** in a leasehold purchase.

## Why 95 years matters
A lease is a wasting asset, so the shorter the term, the more it can affect:
- **mortgageability**
- **future saleability**
- **the cost of extending the lease later**

In the UK, many lenders are comfortable with leases of 95 years, but what matters is often the **remaining term at completion and at the end of the mortgage term**, not just the headline figure.

## When to be concerned
You should be more cautious if:

### 1. The lease is already under 100 years
Once a lease drops below **80 years remaining**, the situation becomes much more serious because:
- the premium for a lease extension increases significantly due to **marriage value**
- some lenders become more restrictive
- buyers may struggle to resell

At **95 years**, you are not at that critical threshold yet, but you are partway through the period where the lease will gradually become less attractive.

### 2. The buyer intends to get a mortgage
You need to check the lender’s requirements. Many lenders are fine with 95 years, but some require:
- a minimum unexpired term at completion
- a minimum term remaining at the end of the mortgage

For example, a lender might want the lease to have at least 70–85 years remaining at completion, or 30 years beyond the mortgage term. Requirements vary, so the specific lender matters.

### 3. The lease extension route is unclear
If the lease is 95 years, the buyer may be able to wait before extending, but it is still important to know:
- the **ground rent**
- whether the lease contains **escalating rent**
- service charge provisions
- whether there are any unusual covenants or restrictions

A 95-year lease with a high or doubling ground rent can be more problematic than a 95-year lease with a nominal rent.

## Practical conveyancing point
The main question is not simply “is 95 years too short?” but:
- **How old is the lease now?**
- **What is the ground rent?**
- **Is the flat mortgageable with the intended lender?**
- **Does the client plan to keep the flat long term?**

## Bottom line
**Usually, no immediate concern** with 95 years remaining, especially if the ground rent is modest and the buyer is mortgage-free or using a lender happy with that term.  
But you should **check lender criteria and lease terms carefully**, and flag that the lease will need monitoring over time.

Under the **Conveyancing Association (CA) Conveyancing Protocol**, a **rear extension built in 2021** with **no planning permission records** is treated as a **material alteration** and must be handled through a **risk‑based, evidence‑led approach**, not by automatically demanding “planning permission” or refusing to proceed.

## 1) Treat it as a “Material Change” that must be raised and evidenced
The Protocol expects the conveyancer to identify anything that appears to be a **significant alteration/extension** and then:

- **Raise a specific additional enquiry** (beyond the standard TA6/TA10 replies) asking:
  - what works were done,
  - when they were done,
  - who did them,
  - whether the seller obtained **planning permission** and **building regulations approval**, and
  - to supply copies of permissions, approvals, completion certificates and any warranties/guarantees (e.g., FENSA for glazing if relevant).

Even if planning permission might not have been required (e.g., permitted development), the Protocol position is that the conveyancer should still ask for the **documentary evidence** needed to assess the risk.

## 2) Planning and Building Regulations are separate—check both
The Protocol approach is to avoid conflating:
- **Planning** (whether permission was needed / obtained / complied with), and
- **Building Regulations** (structural and compliance sign‑off).

So for a 2021 extension, the Protocol expects enquiries and evidence on **both regimes**, because a lack of either can affect:
- mortgageability,
- future saleability,
- value, and
- enforcement risk.

## 3) If no planning record exists, consider lawful alternatives (don’t assume breach)
The Protocol steers conveyancers to explore legitimate explanations and appropriate evidence, such as:

- **Permitted Development**: ask for confirmation that the works were carried out under PD rights and request supporting documents (plans/spec, measurements, etc.).
- **Certificate of Lawfulness** (existing use/development): if available, this is strong evidence the works are lawful in planning terms.
- **Planning search / local authority checks**: the Protocol expects appropriate searches and follow‑up where something material is identified.

## 4) 2021 is inside typical enforcement windows—so risk is “live”
Although the Protocol itself isn’t a planning law textbook, its risk framework means you should treat **recent works (like 2021)** as higher risk because:

- if the extension required planning permission and didn’t get it, it is likely still within the local authority’s enforcement period, and
- lenders are more likely to insist on satisfactory evidence for recent works.

## 5) Indemnity insurance is not a default fix and should not be used if it’s been “tipped off”
A key Protocol theme is: **don’t use indemnity insurance as a substitute for proper investigation where evidence can be obtained**, and don’t do anything that would invalidate a policy.

In practice, the Protocol-compliant approach is:

- First try to obtain the missing documents (permissions / completion cert / lawful development evidence).
- Only consider **indemnity insurance** if:
  - the risk remains after reasonable investigation,
  - the buyer and lender accept it, and
  - **no approach has been made to the local authority** (or other steps taken) that would prejudice cover (insurers typically require the council not to have been contacted about the breach).

Also, the Protocol encourages you to ensure indemnity is **in the correct name(s)**, for the right risk (planning vs building regs), and for an adequate limit.

## 6) Report to the client (and lender) clearly, with options and risk
The Protocol requires the conveyancer to **explain the legal and practical risk** and present options. For a 2021 extension with no planning record, that typically includes:

- obtain missing permissions/regularisation documents (if possible),
- obtain a **Certificate of Lawfulness** if it should be PD but no record exists,
- renegotiate/retain funds/undertakings (less common but possible),
- obtain indemnity insurance (where appropriate), or
- proceed acknowledging residual risk (only if the buyer/lender accept).

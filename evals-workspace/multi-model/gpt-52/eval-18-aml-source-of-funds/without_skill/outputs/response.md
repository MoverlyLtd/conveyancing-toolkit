When the purchase monies are coming from Dubai (UAE), you should treat this as an **international source of funds / source of wealth scenario** and apply a **risk‑based uplift** to your normal AML file. In practice, that usually means: (1) enhanced client due diligence if the overall risk profile warrants it, and (2) more robust evidence of **how the client accumulated the wealth** and **how the specific funds moved** to the UK.

Below is a practical checklist of **additional checks** commonly expected beyond “standard” UK source‑of‑funds.

---

## 1) Do an explicit country/transaction risk assessment (and record it)
- Record why funds are from Dubai and the **client’s connection to the UAE** (residency, employment, business, family, property sale, etc.).
- Consider risk factors: **non‑UK jurisdiction**, complexity of route of funds, cash‑intensive industry, use of intermediaries/agents, any time pressure, unusual payment structures.
- Decide and document whether **EDD** is required (often is where funds are from higher‑risk jurisdictions or the client profile/structure is complex). The UAE is not on the UK “high‑risk third country” list at the time of writing, but many firms still treat certain Gulf/overseas funds cases as higher risk depending on the fact pattern.

---

## 2) Apply Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) triggers (if applicable)
Even if UAE isn’t automatically “high‑risk”, EDD is appropriate if any of the following apply:
- **PEP** (client or close associate/family)
- Complex ownership structures, corporate vehicles, trusts
- Unusually large funds relative to known profile
- Use of **third‑party funding**
- Rapid movement of funds through multiple accounts
- Adverse media indicators

**EDD measures** commonly include:
- More granular source of wealth evidence (see below)
- Independent verification checks (reputable databases/adverse media)
- Senior management approval (firm policy dependent)
- Increased ongoing monitoring (e.g., re-checks if completion delayed)

---

## 3) Strengthen identity verification for an overseas client
Beyond standard ID/address:
- Use **robust electronic ID&V** that works cross‑border and/or obtain **certified copies** of passport and proof of address (certification by a regulated professional—check your firm policy and what you accept).
- If address proof is difficult in UAE (common), accept alternatives **only if your policy allows** and document rationale (e.g., government/utility, bank statement, tenancy contract, Emirates ID residence details).
- If you cannot get comfort, consider **video verification** plus additional corroboration.

---

## 4) Source of Wealth (SoW): evidence how the client accumulated the wealth (not just this pot of money)
For overseas funds, regulators typically expect you to evidence **where the overall wealth came from**, not merely that money exists.

Examples (take what fits the narrative):
- **Employment income in UAE:** employment contract, payslips, UAE bank salary credits, end‑of‑service gratuity evidence.
- **Business income:** company accounts/audited statements, trade licence, dividends, sale of business documentation, tax returns where applicable.
- **Property sale in UAE/elsewhere:** sale contract, completion statement, land registry extract (if available), evidence of proceeds credited to the client’s bank.
- **Inheritance/gift:** probate documents, will/letters, evidence of distribution, plus donor SoW/SoF where it’s a gift (see below).
- **Investments/crypto:** statements from regulated platforms, trading history, evidence of fiat on/off ramps and original funding. (This often warrants EDD.)

Tip: Make the SoW evidence **proportionate to the purchase price and the client profile**—bigger/less intuitive wealth = more documentation.

---

## 5) Source of Funds (SoF): trace the specific purchase monies from origin to your client account
For Dubai-origin monies, go beyond a single bank statement:
- Obtain **bank statements showing build‑up** of funds (often 3–6 months; longer if needed) in the **account the money will be remitted from**.
- If funds came from another account first, obtain the **“chain”**: statements covering the transfer(s) between accounts.
- Obtain supporting documents explaining **large credits** (salary bonus, property sale, dividend, loan drawdown, etc.).
- If currency exchange is involved, get the **FX contract/receipt** and evidence of the debit/credit.

---

## 6) Verify the remitting bank and payment route (sanctions/compliance comfort)
- Confirm funds will come from a **regulated bank** in the UAE (or an international bank) in the client’s name.
- Run **sanctions screening** (client + beneficial owners +, where your systems allow, the remitting bank/recipient/intermediary names if unusual).
- If the client proposes using a **money service business/exchange house**, treat as higher risk:
  - Verify the MSB is **properly licensed/regulated in the UAE**.
  - Obtain documents showing the MSB’s role, the client’s account with them, and transaction records.
  - Consider whether your firm/client account rules permit receipt from that route; many firms prefer funds come from a **mainstream bank account** in the client’s name.

---

## 7) Third‑party funds / gifts: do full checks on the donor or payer
If any part of the purchase money is coming from someone else (family member in Dubai, employer, business partner):
- Identify and verify the **third party** (ID&V).
- Obtain their **SoW/SoF** evidence.
- Get a **gifted deposit declaration** / confirmation of no repayment (or, if it is a loan, document it and consider lender requirements).
- Trace the route of funds from donor to client and then to you (or directly to you if permitted—many firms avoid this).

---

## 8) Beneficial ownership / corporate structures (if client is not an individual)
If buying via UAE company, offshore SPV, trust, etc.:
- Obtain constitutional documents, registers, and **verify UBOs** to 25%+ (or lower if your risk assessment requires).
- Confirm authority of signatories.
- Understand the **commercial rationale** for using that structure.
- Expect EDD, especially where nominee shareholders/directors are used.

---

## 9) Adverse media and PEP screening (wider and deeper)
For overseas funds, go further than basic screening:
- Conduct **adverse media** searches (English +, if possible, relevant language sources) on the client/UBOs/donors and associated businesses.
- Document results and how you resolved any hits.
- If PEP or high-profile business/political connections exist, apply full **PEP EDD** (establish SoW particularly clearly; senior sign-off; ongoing monitoring).

---

## 10) Practical “red flags” to look for (and escalate)
Consider escalating to MLRO / making further enquiries if you see:
- Reluctance to provide UAE bank statements or “privacy” objections
- Funds routed through multiple accounts/third countries without clear reason
- Use of cash deposits, exchange houses with minimal paperwork, or crypto as the immediate source
- Inconsistency between declared occupation/business and available funds
- Unusual urgency or pressure to exchange/complete without evidence

---

## 11) Document everything and align with firm policy / lender requirements
- Keep a clear audit trail: risk assessment, what you asked for, what you received, why it was sufficient.
- Check your **firm’s AML policy** and your **professional body guidance** (SRA/Law Society), and any **lender handbook** requirements (some lenders are stricter on overseas/third-party funds).
- If you cannot obtain adequate evidence, you should **not proceed** and consider whether a SAR is required (MLRO decision).

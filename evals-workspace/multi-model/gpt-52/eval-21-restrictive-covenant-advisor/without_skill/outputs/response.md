A “no business use” restrictive covenant is very common on post‑war estates. Whether a quiet home office breaches it depends on (1) the wording and (2) the actual use. Even if there is technically a breach, the practical risk may be low, but you still need to manage lender/buyer requirements and the client’s appetite for certainty.

## 1) Start with the wording and the facts
Key points to check in the covenant and title:
- **Exact wording**: “No trade or business whatsoever”, “no business to be carried on”, “not to use otherwise than as a private dwellinghouse”, “no profession”, etc. Some are absolute; some allow “private dwelling” use only; some carve out “domestic office” type activity (rare but exists).
- **What the client will do**: purely admin/desk work, no staff, no clients, no deliveries, no noise/odour, no signage, no storage, no alterations.
- **Is it a change of use in planning terms?** Planning and restrictive covenants are separate, but the practical analysis often overlaps: a home office that remains ancillary to residential use is usually not a planning “material change of use”, which supports an argument that it remains “as a private dwelling”.
- **Who can enforce now?** Identify the **benefiting land** / persons with benefit (often the original developer’s retained land or other estate plots). If benefit is not clear or has been lost, enforceability may be weak.

## 2) Is a low‑key home office actually a breach?
Often you can argue **no breach** where:
- the property remains a private dwellinghouse; and
- the “business” is merely ancillary to residential occupation (e.g., working from home on a laptop with no external manifestations).

However:
- some covenants are drafted so widely (“no trade or business whatsoever”) that **even a home office could technically breach**, regardless of visibility.
- if the client later adds **client visits, employees, regular deliveries, signage, conversions**, the risk increases materially.

Because covenant interpretation is fact‑sensitive, many conveyancers treat “home office only” as **low risk**, but not always “no risk”.

## 3) “Never enforced since 1965” — how much does that help?
It helps, but it is not a complete answer:
- **Delay/lack of enforcement** can reduce practical risk, but it does not automatically extinguish the covenant.
- If the estate remains intact and neighbours still have the benefit, enforcement can still happen if someone becomes motivated (even if unlikely).
- Evidence of **widespread similar breaches** on the estate can support an argument that the covenant is **obsolete** or that the character has changed (useful for a Tribunal application), but again it is not definitive.

## 4) Options in practice
### Option A — Proceed on the basis it’s not a breach / de minimis
Appropriate where:
- the activity is strictly internal admin work;
- no external change and no nuisance;
- you are comfortable the covenant is aimed at “trade” causing nuisance/traffic.

Risk: a future buyer/lender’s solicitor may still raise it; and if the use drifts beyond “quiet home working” the position worsens.

### Option B — Restrictive covenant indemnity insurance (most common)
This is often the pragmatic solution if:
- there is a **technical breach** or uncertainty; and
- the client wants comfort for lender/saleability without approaching beneficiaries.

Typical cover: costs of defending enforcement action and/or diminution in value (wording varies).

**Important practical point:** insurers generally require that **no approach is made to anyone with the benefit** of the covenant (developer/management company/neighbours) and no dispute is brewing. If you contact beneficiaries for consent, you can **invalidate** the ability to insure.

### Option C — Seek a deed of release/consent/variation
Best for long‑term certainty, but:
- you must correctly identify all parties with the benefit (can be multiple owners on an estate);
- can be time‑consuming and costly;
- may involve a premium/payment and legal fees on both sides.

Also note: once you start asking, **indemnity insurance usually becomes unavailable**.

### Option D — Application to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) under s.84 LPA 1925
Possible grounds include:
- covenant is **obsolete** due to changes in the character of the property/neighbourhood;
- it **impedes reasonable use** and does not secure practical benefit of substantial value/advantage; or
- beneficiaries have **expressly/implicitly agreed** or would suffer no injury.

This is the “nuclear option”: slower, evidentially heavy, and more expensive, usually only justified where the business use is more substantial or a release cannot be agreed.

### Option E — Keep use strictly within “incidental home working”
A practical behavioural option: advise client to avoid anything that creates an “outward sign”:
- no signage, no clients, no staff, no unusual deliveries, no business rates registration if it would flag change, no noise/odour, no structural alterations without advice.
This doesn’t remove risk but keeps it low.

## 5) Indemnity insurance cost — what is typical?
Premiums vary by insurer, property value, perceived enforceability, and whether there is an **actual breach** vs “risk of breach”. For a standard residential property, a “restrictive covenant” indemnity policy is often a **one‑off premium** (not annual).

As a rough market guide (England & Wales, typical mainstream insurers):
- **Low risk / no known contact / quiet home office scenario**: often **~£50 to £250**.
- **Higher value property or clearer technical breach** (e.g., covenant expressly bans “any business whatsoever”; evidence of existing business use): often **~£200 to £750**.
- **Very high value properties** or more contentious risks can go **£750–£2,000+**.

What drives it up:
- higher property value / higher limit of indemnity requested (often the purchase price or mortgage advance);
- evidence that beneficiaries are identifiable, nearby, active management company, prior complaints;
- anything suggesting the breach is obvious or likely to prompt objection.

Your broker/insurer will quote quickly once you provide:
- title wording, date, whether breached, how long, any approaches made, property value, and whether there have been threats/objections.

## 6) Practical conveyancing strategy
1. **Obtain and review the full covenant wording** and any building scheme/estate documents.
2. **Confirm the exact intended use** (and record it) — “admin only, no visitors, no staff, no signage”.
3. Decide between:
   - “not a breach/low risk” (with client advice), or
   - **indemnity insurance** (often simplest for lender and future saleability).
4. **Do not approach** neighbours/developer/management company if insurance is the likely route.
5. If the client anticipates the use may expand (clients, staff, signage), consider **release/variation** or a more bespoke solution early.

It depends on the lender’s current lease-term criteria *and* the product/loan profile, but as a rule 72 years unexpired is in the “possible but often problematic” zone. Many mainstream lenders want a longer term remaining either **at completion** and/or **at the end of the mortgage term**, and some also have specific cut-offs around **70–80 years** because (a) saleability/resale risk and (b) lease extension cost accelerates once the lease drops below 80 years.

Below is a practical conveyancing-style view of **Halifax, Nationwide and Barclays**. (Lenders do change criteria, and the definitive position is always their current lending policy and what their valuer will accept.)

---

## The key issues at 72 years
1. **Below 80 years**: “Marriage value” becomes payable on a statutory lease extension, making extension significantly more expensive. Lenders factor this into valuation and marketability.
2. **End-of-term requirement**: Some lenders require a minimum unexpired term **at the end of the mortgage** (e.g., 25–30 years remaining at mortgage expiry), not just at the start.
3. **Valuer discretion**: Even if policy permits, the valuation can come back with conditions (e.g., “lend only if lease is extended”) or down-valued.

---

## Halifax
**Often cautious** on shorter leases. In many cases, **72 years is likely to be unacceptable unless the lease is being extended (or will be extended) as part of the transaction**, or the case fits within Halifax’s current minimum-term rules (which can depend on remaining term and mortgage length).

**Typical outcome in practice**:
- **If you’re buying with a standard 25–35 year term mortgage**: Halifax may require a longer lease term at completion and/or insist on a lease extension being completed **before** or **simultaneously with** completion.
- Halifax’s valuer may also flag it as a resale risk.

**Conveyancer takeaway**: If Halifax is the chosen lender, assume you may need to **extend the lease** (or structure the deal around an extension) to keep the transaction mortgageable.

---

## Nationwide
Nationwide is also generally **risk-averse** on short leases. A 72-year lease can be a **problem**, especially if the mortgage term is long.

**Typical outcome in practice**:
- Nationwide frequently expects the lease to run for a minimum period beyond the mortgage term (i.e., not just “is it above X today?”).
- At **72 years**, you may fall short of their internal comfort level, and the valuer may require an extension.

**Conveyancer takeaway**: Nationwide may lend in some scenarios, but **expect conditions** or refusal unless the lease is extended.

---

## Barclays
Barclays can be **more flexible in some cases**, but 72 years is still borderline and commonly triggers valuation concerns and/or minimum-term rules.

**Typical outcome in practice**:
- Barclays may consider it if their “at completion” and “at end of mortgage term” requirements are met, but at 72 years that’s tight for many standard mortgage terms.
- Valuer may reduce loan-to-value or require lease extension.

**Conveyancer takeaway**: More “maybe” than an outright yes—still often easiest to proceed with a lease extension plan.

---

## What usually solves this (and what your solicitor will suggest)
### 1) Lease extension before completion (best for mortgageability)
- Seller completes the lease extension and you buy the extended lease.

### 2) Seller serves a Section 42 notice and assigns it to you
If the seller has owned the flat for **at least 2 years**, they can serve a **statutory lease extension notice** and **assign the benefit** to you on completion. This allows you to continue the statutory process immediately after purchase (without waiting 2 years).  
However, **many lenders still want the extension completed** (not just “in progress”), so check lender stance carefully.

### 3) Informal extension
Sometimes quicker, but can come with worse terms (ground rent clauses etc.). Lenders are increasingly sensitive to onerous ground rent.

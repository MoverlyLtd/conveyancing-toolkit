With 68 years unexpired, the lease term is a **material issue** for saleability, buyer demand, mortgage availability and (crucially) the **cost of extending**. At this length you’re already in the zone where values are discounted and the extension premium can rise quickly.

## 1) Why 68 years matters (the key thresholds)
### Below ~80 years: “marriage value” becomes payable
- In statutory lease extensions, once the lease has **< 80 years** remaining, the premium usually increases significantly because **“marriage value”** is payable to the freeholder.
- Marriage value is broadly the additional value created by extending the lease, and the landlord is entitled to **50%** of that uplift (under the statutory formula).
- At **68 years**, you’re **well past** this threshold, so buyers (and their solicitors) will flag it and price it in.

### Below ~70 years: lending can get harder
- Many mainstream lenders become cautious as terms drop, and some have hard cut-offs around **70 years** (or require a minimum unexpired term at completion, or minimum remaining at the end of the mortgage term).
- This **shrinks the buyer pool** and increases the risk of down-valuations or mortgage refusals late in the transaction.

### Below ~60 years: often “problem” territory
- Not your current position, but it’s worth knowing that if it slips further, the flat can become increasingly cash-buyer-only and extension costs rise further.

## 2) Impact on price and marketing
- A 68-year lease typically leads to:
  - **Price negotiation** (buyers will ask for a reduction reflecting extension premium + hassle + risk).
  - **Longer transaction times** (more enquiries, lender scrutiny, valuation queries).
  - **More fall-through risk** (if the lender/valuer objects).

Asking price **£425,000** may still be achievable depending on location, service charge, ground rent, and comparable “short lease” sales—but expect buyers to benchmark against “long lease” comps and then subtract extension cost and risk.

## 3) Lease extension basics you should understand (and can explain accurately)
### Statutory route (most common/valuable for buyers)
If the flat qualifies and the seller has owned it long enough:
- Under the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993, a qualifying leaseholder can claim:
  - **An additional 90 years** on top of the existing term (so 68 + 90 = **158 years**), and
  - **Ground rent reduced to a peppercorn** (effectively zero).
- This statutory route is attractive because it gives a clear legal outcome and removes ground rent.

**Key qualification point:** the current leaseholder usually must have owned the flat for **at least 2 years** to start the statutory process.

### Informal route (direct deal with freeholder)
- Can be quicker, but the freeholder may:
  - Offer fewer years,
  - Keep or increase ground rent (sometimes aggressively),
  - Include terms that lenders dislike (e.g., escalating ground rent).
- Informal deals can look cheaper upfront but be worse long-term and affect mortgageability/resale.

## 4) What you can do now to help the sale
### Option A: Seller extends before marketing/sale
Pros:
- Widest buyer pool, best mortgageability, cleaner transaction.
Cons:
- Takes time; cost is paid upfront by seller.

### Option B: Seller starts the statutory claim and assigns it to the buyer (very common)
If the seller has owned for 2+ years, they can:
- Serve a **Section 42 Notice** (statutory lease extension claim),
- Then **assign the benefit of the claim** to the buyer on completion, so the buyer doesn’t have to wait 2 years.

This is often the best compromise: you can market now, but give mortgage buyers comfort that they can extend under the statutory regime immediately after purchase.

Practical points:
- The seller will need a **specialist valuer** and a solicitor to serve the notice.
- The buyer’s solicitor will insist the assignment is properly documented in the contract.

### Option C: Price reflects the short lease (no notice served)
- Works better for cash buyers/investors.
- Higher risk of mortgage issues and renegotiation.

## 5) Expect buyer enquiries on these lease terms (get the info early)
Have the seller/solicitor ready with:
- Current **ground rent** amount and **review pattern** (doubling clauses are a red flag).
- **Service charge** level and last 3 years’ accounts.
- Major works status (Section 20 notices).
- Buildings insurance arrangements.
- Any deed of variation, licences to alter, disputes, arrears.
- Freeholder/managing agent details and typical responsiveness (affects sale timeline).

Short leases plus problematic ground rent can be a double hit for lending.

## 6) Mortgage and valuation realities (what tends to happen)
- Even if a lender is willing in principle, the **valuer** may down-value because of the short lease and the extension cost.
- Some lenders require evidence that:
  - the lease will be extended **simultaneously with completion**, or
  - the seller has served and will assign a **Section 42 notice**.

So it’s not just “can the buyer get a mortgage?”, it’s “will the lender accept the lease position at completion?”

## 7) Costs: avoid quoting a figure, but guide sensibly
You should generally avoid giving a definitive extension premium (that’s for a specialist valuer), but you can accurately say:
- At **68 years**, the premium is likely to be **material** because marriage value applies.
- Costs include:
  - Premium to the freeholder,
  - The leaseholder’s own valuation and legal fees,
  - The freeholder’s reasonable valuation and legal fees (payable by the leaseholder under the statutory process),
  - Land Registry fees (and possibly SDLT in rare/high-premium cases—solicitor to confirm).

If you want to give ballpark context in conversation, frame it carefully as “can vary widely based on ground rent, review terms, value and yields—valuer required.”

## 8) How to present it in the listing (without overstepping)
- Be transparent: “**68 years remaining**” should be clearly stated (it’s material information).
- If the seller is willing, a strong line is:
  - “Seller able to **serve and assign a Section 42 Notice** (subject to eligibility)”  
  This signals a practical route for mortgage buyers.
- If the lease extension has already been started or completed, highlight it prominently.

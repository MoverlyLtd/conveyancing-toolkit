Under the **Law Society Conveyancing Protocol (England & Wales)**, delays caused by a party not dealing with enquiries are exactly the kind of issue the Protocol is designed to reduce. While the Protocol is **not legislation**, it sets out **expected standards of co‑operation, communication and timescales** between conveyancers. The key points you can rely on are:

## 1) General duty to co‑operate and progress the matter
The Protocol expects both sides to:
- **act proactively**, progress the transaction and **avoid unnecessary delay**
- **communicate clearly and promptly**
- raise and respond to issues in a way that is **proportionate** and focused on what is needed for exchange/completion.

So, a party simply “going silent” (or not dealing with enquiries) is contrary to the Protocol’s approach.

## 2) Enquiries must be raised and answered promptly (and kept under review)
The Protocol’s approach is that:
- **enquiries should be raised as soon as reasonably possible** once contract papers are received and reviewed (and searches/other information start coming in);
- enquiries should be **limited to what is necessary**, avoiding “standard” or unnecessary rounds; and
- **responses should be provided promptly** and in a way that actually answers the point (or explains why the information cannot be provided and what alternative evidence is available).

Where a party can’t answer immediately, the Protocol expects them to:
- **acknowledge** the enquiry,
- **explain what is being done** to obtain the information (e.g., from managing agents, landlord, client, lender),
- and give a **realistic timescale** for a full reply.

## 3) Keeping the client informed and managing expectations
The Protocol places emphasis on keeping clients informed about:
- what is outstanding,
- why it is outstanding,
- and how it affects the likely exchange/completion timetable.

If the other side’s non-responsiveness is jeopardising a target date, the Protocol contemplates that you:
- **flag this clearly** to them, and
- **explain the consequences** (e.g., inability to exchange, risk to mortgage offer expiry, removals costs, chain impact).

## 4) Escalation: chasing and setting deadlines (and warning of consequences)
The Protocol supports a staged, professional escalation:
- chase in writing (email/portal) with a **clear list of outstanding enquiries**;
- give a **reasonable deadline** for response;
- if no engagement, escalate to a **supervisor/partner** on the other side;
- if the matter is in a chain, consider a **measured “chain update”** (without breaching confidentiality) to ensure pressure is applied appropriately.

The Protocol encourages avoiding point-scoring, but it does support making clear that:
- **exchange cannot take place** until replies are received/satisfactory; and
- any agreed target dates may need to move unless they comply.

## 5) What it *doesn’t* do
It’s worth being candid about limits:
- The Protocol does **not** give you a hard statutory deadline or a direct enforcement mechanism.
- If the other side continues not to respond, your practical remedies are procedural/strategic: escalate, renegotiate dates, or in the extreme advise your client on whether to continue.

## 6) Practical wording you can use in a chase (Protocol-aligned)
You can write along these lines (adapt as needed):

> “This matter is being progressed under the Law Society Conveyancing Protocol. Please treat this as a formal chase for replies to the outstanding enquiries listed below. The Protocol envisages prompt co‑operation and timely responses to avoid delay.  
> If we do not receive substantive replies (or a clear timescale for providing them) by [date/time], our client will need to reconsider the proposed timetable for exchange/completion and we will report accordingly.”

## 7) If you tell me the context, I can be more precise
The Protocol expectations bite slightly differently depending on whether this is:
- **freehold vs leasehold** (leasehold often needs managing agent/landlord input),
- whether replies are via **TA forms vs additional enquiries**,
- whether there is an agreed **target exchange/completion date**, and
- whether the delay relates to **title issues, management pack, building safety (EWS1), planning/building regs, or LPE1**.

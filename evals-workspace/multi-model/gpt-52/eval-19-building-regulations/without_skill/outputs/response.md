In England & Wales, a loft conversion carried out in 2019 should ordinarily have Building Regulations approval and a **Building Control Completion Certificate** (or equivalent “final certificate”) from either the local authority building control (LABC) or an approved inspector. If the seller cannot produce it and the works weren’t self‑certified under a competent person scheme, it raises both **legal/conveyancing risk** and **practical safety/resale risk**.

Below are the key implications and the usual remedies.

---

## 1) Why it matters (what Building Regulations cover in a loft conversion)

A loft conversion typically involves controlled work affecting matters such as:

- **Structural stability** (new floor joists/steel beams; trimming around stair opening)
- **Fire safety** (protected stair route, FD30 doors, mains‑wired interlinked smoke alarms, escape windows, sometimes sprinklers; fire separation to rooms)
- **Stairs** (pitch, headroom, handrails, guarding)
- **Thermal insulation/ventilation** (roof insulation, condensation risk)
- **Means of escape** and fire resistance of floor/partitions
- **Electrics** (Part P) and sometimes plumbing/drainage

The completion certificate is the “evidence” that the conversion was inspected and signed off.

---

## 2) Implications of no completion certificate

### A. Conveyancing / lender issues
- **Mortgage lender may refuse to lend** or may ask for evidence of compliance or a regularisation certificate. Lenders vary; some will accept an indemnity policy (often not ideal for lofts), others insist on sign‑off.
- Future buyers (and their lenders) may raise the same issue, affecting **resaleability** and **value**.

### B. Local authority enforcement risk
- Building Regulations enforcement is **time‑limited**. For works in 2019:
  - The local authority generally has **12 months** from completion to prosecute in the Magistrates’ Court.
  - They may also have power to seek an **injunction** (rare, but legally possible) to require compliance/remedial works.
- Practically, after several years, the **enforcement risk is lower**, but not zero (injunction risk is the one that can bite later, albeit uncommon).

### C. Insurance / liability / safety
- Lack of sign‑off can be a red flag for **fire safety and structure**—the two big risks in loft conversions.
- If there is a loss (e.g., fire), insurers usually focus on non‑disclosure and causation. No certificate doesn’t automatically void cover, but it can complicate claims and liability arguments.

### D. Misdescription / “habitable room” and marketing
- If the loft is marketed as a bedroom without evidence of Building Regs sign‑off, a buyer may argue it is not a compliant habitable room. This can affect:
  - valuation (valuer may down‑value or ignore the room)
  - negotiations
  - whether it should be described as “loft room” rather than “bedroom”.

---

## 3) Remedies / options in practice

### Option 1: Seller obtains a **Regularisation Certificate** (best evidential fix)
This is the most robust solution where possible.

**How it works**
- Application is made to **Local Authority Building Control** for “regularisation” of unauthorised works (even if done years ago).
- Building Control will inspect and usually require **opening up** of parts of the work and/or engineer’s calculations and reports.
- If defects are found, **remedial works** must be carried out before a certificate is issued.

**Pros**
- Strongest comfort for you and any mortgage lender.
- Helps resale and valuation.

**Cons**
- Time (can take weeks/months).
- Cost (fees + potential remedial works + opening up).
- There is a risk it exposes non‑compliance that must be rectified.

**Typical conveyancing approach**
- Buyer insists the seller applies and obtains regularisation **before exchange**, or
- Buyer agrees to proceed with a **retention** (money held back) until regularisation is obtained post‑completion (less common in residential, but possible).

### Option 2: Commission independent reports (practical risk management)
Even if regularisation isn’t pursued, you can reduce risk by commissioning:

- **Building survey** specifically addressing the loft conversion
- **Structural engineer report** (floor/steel/stability; load paths)
- **Electrical installation condition report (EICR)** and evidence of any Part P compliance (if available)
- Fire safety assessment elements (doors, alarms, escape route)

**Pros**
- Gives real information about safety/quality.
- Useful for negotiating price/works.

**Cons**
- Still doesn’t give Building Control sign‑off.
- Lender may still object.

### Option 3: **Indemnity insurance** (limited, often misunderstood)
A “lack of building regulations” indemnity policy is commonly offered, but you should understand what it does and doesn’t do.

**It generally covers**
- Financial loss arising from **local authority enforcement action** due to lack of approval/certificate.

**It generally does NOT cover**
- The cost of putting defects right because the work is unsafe/defective.
- Diminution in value because it’s poorly built (unless linked to enforcement).
- Personal injury, poor workmanship, or future maintenance issues.

**Key practical point**
- Many policies become invalid if you (or the seller) **approach the local authority** about the lack of certificate **before** the policy is put in place. So you typically choose **either** regularisation **or** indemnity—trying to do both can kill the insurance option.

**When it’s used**
- Often used where the works are older and the enforcement risk is low, and the buyer/lender is content to accept the residual practical risk.

**For a 2019 loft conversion**
- Some lenders/buyers consider indemnity alone too weak because the core risk is safety/compliance, not enforcement.

### Option 4: Renegotiate / require remedial works / walk away
If the loft is integral to why you’re buying (e.g., you need the extra bedroom), you can:
- renegotiate the price to reflect uncertainty and potential remedial costs,
- require the seller to carry out specified remedial works (with evidence),
- or withdraw if lender or survey flags unacceptable risk.

---

## 4) Specific things your conveyancer should ask for (even without a completion cert)

1. **Any Building Control paperwork** at all: initial notice, plans approval, inspection records, correspondence.
2. **Plans, structural calculations, and steel/beam specs** (often from an architect/engineer).
3. **Party Wall documentation** (if applicable) – not Building Regs, but commonly relevant to loft works in terraced/semi houses.
4. **Electrical certificates**: installation certificate, minor works, Part P notification (if any).
5. **Guarantees** (less important than regs, but useful).
6. Confirm whether the loft is being described as a **bedroom** and ensure the contract/TA forms are accurate.

---

## 5) Practical decision framework (what tends to be “acceptable”)

- **If you need mortgage finance and the valuer flags it**: expect to need **regularisation** or very strong alternative evidence + lender approval.
- **If the conversion is a major part of the property value**: regularisation and/or detailed reports are more appropriate than indemnity alone.
- **If you’re cash buyer and comfortable with risk**: you might proceed with survey/engineer checks + price adjustment, possibly with indemnity as a backstop (recognising its limits).

---

## 6) Recommended “buyer‑protective” approach

1. **Ask the seller to apply for regularisation** and obtain the certificate pre‑exchange (best outcome).
2. If seller refuses or timescales don’t work:
   - obtain a **full building survey** plus **structural engineer** input specifically on the loft;
   - confirm your **lender’s** position in writing (through your broker/solicitor);
   - negotiate a **price reduction** to reflect risk/cost;
   - consider indemnity **only if** your lender accepts it and you’re satisfied on safety/quality.

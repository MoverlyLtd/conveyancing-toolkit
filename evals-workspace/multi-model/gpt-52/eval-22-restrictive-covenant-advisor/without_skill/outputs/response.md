A restrictive covenant “not to alter the external appearance” is capable of biting on a rear extension, even if it isn’t visible from the road. The fact it dates from 1992 and the likely beneficiary has extended in 2015 is helpful, but it doesn’t automatically make the covenant unenforceable. I’d advise along the following lines.

## 1) Establish the covenant’s scope and who can enforce
**Review the exact wording and title context** (both burdened and benefitting titles):

- **What is prohibited?**  
  “Alteration to the external appearance” can capture extensions, dormers, external cladding, new windows/doors, roof alterations etc. Check if it’s absolute or qualified (e.g. “without the consent of…”). If there is a consent mechanism, that is usually the first route.

- **Who has the benefit?**  
  You need to identify the person/body with the benefit:
  - Is the benefit expressly granted to the neighbour’s plot (e.g. “for the benefit of No. X”)?
  - Or is it a **building scheme / estate scheme** covenant benefiting multiple plots on a development (common with 1990s estates)? If so, *many* neighbours (or a management company) may have enforcement rights, not just the adjoining owner.
  - Is the original covenantee a developer that may have retained land or assigned the benefit?

- **Is it properly protected on title?**  
  Most restrictive covenants will bind successors in equity without separate registration on older titles, but you should confirm whether anything affects enforceability (e.g. whether the benefit has been properly annexed/assigned; whether it is a scheme of development). This is a technical but crucial step—don’t assume only the immediate neighbour matters.

## 2) Assess enforcement risk in light of the neighbour’s 2015 extension
The neighbour extending can support several arguments, but none is a guaranteed “get out”:

- **Acquiescence / waiver / estoppel**  
  If the *same party who can enforce against your client* has themselves built in breach and/or encouraged your client to proceed, that can weaken their position. However:
  - A beneficiary can sometimes still enforce even if they’ve breached themselves (unless it’s clearly a waiver/estoppel situation).
  - If there are **multiple beneficiaries** (estate scheme), the neighbour’s conduct doesn’t stop others enforcing.

- **“Changed character” argument**  
  If the estate has widely changed since 1992 (numerous extensions, alterations tolerated), that can support an application to modify/discharge (see section 4). You’ll want evidence: photos, planning history, and whether the covenant has been routinely ignored.

- **Practical point**  
  The neighbour’s extension suggests they may be less inclined to object, but if their extension was done **with consent** (from a management company/developer) then the covenant may still be very much “live” and enforced through consents.

## 3) Consider the cleanest route: obtain consent / deed of release
If the covenant is expressed as “no alterations without consent”, or even if it’s absolute, the lowest-risk route is usually:

- **Approach the party/entities with the benefit** for **written consent** or a **deed of release/variation**.
- Ensure you identify *all* beneficiaries who could enforce (management company + relevant plot owners if a scheme).
- Agree a fee and legal costs, plus plans/specifications.  
- Record consent properly (ideally a deed, and register it against the title).

**Caution:** Don’t contact potential beneficiaries until you’ve thought about indemnity insurance (below). Once you “tip off” beneficiaries, it can make insurance unobtainable.

## 4) If consent isn’t possible: Upper Tribunal application (s.84 LPA 1925)
If the covenant materially impedes reasonable use, your client can apply to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) to **modify or discharge** it. Common grounds:

- **Obsolescence** (s.84(1)(a))  
  Covenant is obsolete due to changes in the character of the property/neighbourhood or other circumstances.

- **Reasonable use with no practical benefit** (s.84(1)(aa))  
  The restriction impedes reasonable use, and:
  - it secures no practical benefits of substantial value/advantage to beneficiaries; or
  - it’s contrary to public interest; and
  - money would be adequate compensation if loss arises.

Neighbour’s 2015 extension and any wider pattern of similar extensions can help demonstrate diminished practical benefit/consistent relaxation. But this route is **time-consuming, uncertain, and costly**, and beneficiaries can object.

## 5) Indemnity insurance (often used, but fact-sensitive)
Restrictive covenant indemnity insurance is commonly used where:
- the breach already exists or will occur,
- the risk of enforcement is judged low,
- and **no approach has been made** to beneficiaries.

However, for a **planned** extension, insurers often require:
- planning permission (sometimes),
- that the works don’t attract attention (no neighbour disputes),
- no prior contact with beneficiaries,
- and sometimes that the extension is not “obvious” or that time has passed since breach (varies by insurer).

Also:
- Insurance doesn’t make the covenant go away; it covers losses/costs if enforcement occurs (subject to terms).
- If there’s an estate management company known to enforce, insurance may be harder/expensive.

Given your facts, the neighbour’s own extension may make insurers more comfortable (suggesting low enforcement appetite), but if it’s an estate scheme with active management, that cuts the other way.

## 6) Practical next steps I would recommend
1. **Obtain and review**:
   - Burdened title register and deed creating the covenant (and plan).
   - Benefitting land title(s) and any transfer/estate documentation indicating building scheme.
   - Local planning history for both properties and nearby plots (evidence of relaxation).

2. **Work out who can enforce**:
   - Single neighbour? Multiple estate owners? Management company?

3. **Risk-grade the proposed extension**:
   - Size, visibility, effect on outlook/light, proximity to boundaries—these affect likelihood of objection and Tribunal prospects.

4. **Decide strategy before any contact**:
   - If the client wants **certainty**, pursue consent/release (accepting time/cost and negotiation).
   - If the client wants **speed** and risk is low, explore indemnity insurance (but avoid contacting beneficiaries first).
   - If consent is refused and the covenant is clearly impeding reasonable use with little benefit, consider **s.84**.

5. **Client advice on timing**:
   - Planning permission/building regs do **not** override covenants.
   - Starting works without addressing the covenant risks injunction (including potentially requiring demolition/alteration), although outcomes depend on facts and beneficiary conduct.

## 7) How the neighbour’s 2015 extension plays into your advice
- It is **useful evidence** that the covenant may no longer be delivering the “practical benefit” originally intended, particularly if similar extensions are common.
- But it is **not a complete defence**: they may have obtained consent, or other beneficiaries may still enforce.
- It can strengthen your negotiating position for consent/release (“you have extended; we propose a similar form; any alleged benefit is limited”).

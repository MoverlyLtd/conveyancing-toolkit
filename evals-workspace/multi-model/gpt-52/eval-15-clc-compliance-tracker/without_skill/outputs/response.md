Under the Council for Licensed Conveyancers (CLC) regulatory framework, your obligations around client money are mainly set by:

- **CLC Accounts Code** (core rules on holding/handling client money)
- **CLC Code of Conduct** (acting in clients’ best interests, integrity, proper governance/controls, transparency on costs/interest)
- **CLC Regulatory Arrangements** more broadly (management, reporting, cooperation, compensation arrangements, etc.)

Below is a practical, conveyancing-focused summary of the key duties you should be working to. (Firms often also mirror **SRA-style** best practice, but you must follow the **CLC’s** own Code.)

---

## 1) Keep client money safe and separate

### a) Use a proper **client account**
You must keep client money **separate from office money** in a designated **client account** at an authorised bank/building society.

- Client money should not sit in the office account.
- The client account must be clearly identifiable as a **client** account (labeling/mandate).

### b) No improper mixing (no “borrowing”)
You must **not use client money** to fund your business cashflow or pay your firm’s expenses. Client money is held **on trust** for the client (or beneficiary) and must only be used for the matter/purpose it was provided for.

---

## 2) Only receive and hold “client money” where appropriate

“Client money” (in conveyancing) commonly includes:
- Purchase monies and deposit funds
- Mortgage advance monies (once received)
- SDLT/LTT funds collected from the client pending submission/payment
- Money held to redeem a mortgage
- Retentions held post-completion (e.g., for service charges, outstanding works, etc.)

**Office money** commonly includes:
- Your fees once properly billed and transferred
- Money received for your costs where it is clearly for fees and not held on account (careful in practice—often it begins as client money until billed)

---

## 3) Prompt banking and prompt use for the correct purpose

### a) Bank promptly
Client money received should be paid into client account **promptly** (typically same/next working day as a control expectation, subject to your internal systems and the Accounts Code requirements).

### b) Use it only when due and only as authorised
Payments out of client account should be:
- **Properly authorised** (internal approvals)
- Supported by a **proper underlying transaction** on that file
- Made **only for the matter** for which funds are held (e.g., don’t use one client’s surplus to cover another client’s shortfall)

---

## 4) Maintain accurate books, ledgers, and reconciliations

You must keep proper accounting records that show, at all times:
- The firm’s **client bank balance**
- Each client matter’s **client ledger balance**
- Any client money held as **retentions**
- Any unallocated receipts (ideally none, or promptly investigated and corrected)

### Reconciliations
You’re expected to carry out **regular reconciliations** (typically at least **monthly**) including:
- Bank statement balance (client account)
- Cashbook balance
- Total of client ledger balances

You must investigate and correct discrepancies promptly and document what was done.

---

## 5) Ensure proper controls and supervision (governance)

CLC expects robust systems of control, which in practice means:

- Clear written **accounts policies and procedures**
- Segregation of duties where possible (e.g., different people for setting up payees, authorising payments, releasing payments)
- Controls over **online banking** (dual authorisation, limits, audit trails)
- Strong controls for **change of bank details** (anti-fraud)
- File-to-ledger discipline: no payments without a file/matter reference and completion statement authority

Senior management have a heightened responsibility to ensure the firm is run so client money is safeguarded.

---

## 6) Client money should not be held longer than necessary

You should only hold client money for as long as there is a genuine reason connected to the transaction (e.g., pending completion, redemption, SDLT payment, retention).

If money is left over:
- Return it **promptly** to the client (or other rightful owner), or
- Deal with it under your firm’s residual balance procedures (see section 10 below)

---

## 7) Interest on client money

You must have a clear **interest policy** compliant with the CLC Accounts Code and communicate it to clients (often in your client care letter/terms).

In general:
- Account to clients for **interest when it is fair/appropriate** in line with your stated policy and the Code.
- Some firms set a de minimis threshold (administratively reasonable) but it must be justifiable and disclosed.

---

## 8) Transfers of costs from client to office account

You can only transfer money from client to office account when:

- A **proper bill** (or other written notification of costs) has been delivered, and
- The amount transferred matches what is properly due, and
- The client ledger remains accurate after transfer.

You should not “help yourself” to funds in advance of billing (except as permitted by the Accounts Code and clearly reflected in the client ledger).

---

## 9) Prohibited/controlled banking arrangements and third-party managed accounts

Be careful if you use:
- **Third Party Managed Accounts (TPMAs)**, or
- Non-standard banking products, or
- Any arrangement where client money is held outside a conventional client account.

CLC’s position is generally that client money still needs to be safeguarded to the required standard and you must ensure:
- You remain compliant with the Accounts Code
- Clients are properly informed
- Risk and liability are clearly understood (and insurers/CLC expectations met)

(If you are considering a TPMA, it’s worth checking the latest CLC guidance and your PII terms.)

---

## 10) Residual client balances (leftover funds)

You must have and follow a process for:
- Identifying residual balances
- Attempting to trace/repay the client
- Keeping records of steps taken

If you cannot return funds after proper attempts, there are controlled routes (depending on amount, circumstances, and the Accounts Code) such as paying to a charity or other method permitted by the CLC, with documentation. You should not simply move money to office account.

---

## 11) Reporting and cooperation duties (breaches, shortages, fraud)

### a) Shortages or suspected misuse
If there is any **shortfall** on client account, or suspicion of:
- Theft
- Misappropriation
- Cyber-fraud (e.g., diverted completion funds)
- Material accounting irregularity

…you have duties to:
- Take immediate protective action (freeze activity if needed, investigate)
- Notify relevant parties internally (COLP/COFA equivalents in CLC firms—often managers responsible for accounts compliance)
- **Report to the CLC** where required
- Consider reporting to police/Action Fraud, bank, insurers, and comply with AML obligations where relevant

### b) Qualified accountant’s involvement
CLC firms are typically subject to requirements around engaging an accountant and providing **accountant’s reports** when required. You need to ensure your accountant understands the CLC Accounts Code and that reporting deadlines are met.

---

## 12) Anti-money laundering (AML) overlay

While AML isn’t “client money” regulation per se, it affects how you handle funds:

- Verify source of funds/wealth as appropriate
- Be alert to third-party funds, unusual payment patterns, and last-minute payer changes
- Ensure you comply with the Money Laundering Regulations and your firm’s risk assessments

Failing AML controls often correlates with client account risks (and the CLC treats this seriously).

---

# Practical checklist for compliance (day-to-day)

- Client account is clearly designated and used only for client money  
- Money in/out is matter-based, evidenced, and authorised  
- Bills delivered before transferring costs to office account  
- Monthly 3-way reconciliation, signed off and retained  
- Strong payment controls and bank-details verification process  
- Clear interest policy communicated to clients  
- Residual balances procedure documented and followed  
- Incident reporting pathway (CLC/insurers/bank) understood and tested

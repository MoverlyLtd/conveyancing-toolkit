In England & Wales, the “rules” on restrictive covenants affecting a **freehold** property come from (1) the **title documents** for that land and (2) a body of **property law** about when covenants bind future owners and how they can be enforced, varied or insured against. Here’s where to look, in a practical order.

## 1) Start with the property’s registered title (HM Land Registry)
Most freehold land is registered, and the key information is in:

### A. **Official Copy of the Register of Title**
Request from HM Land Registry (HMLR). Look particularly at:

- **B: Proprietorship Register** (less relevant to covenants, but confirms owner)
- **C: Charges Register** — this is usually where restrictive covenants are **set out or referred to**.  
  Typical entries say things like:
  - “The land is subject to the restrictive covenants contained in a Conveyance dated…”
  - Or they may quote the covenant wording in full.

### B. **Title Plan**
Helps confirm what land is burdened/benefited and whether the covenant relates to a particular part (e.g., “no building on the strip tinted brown”).

### C. **“Filed” or “Deeds referred to” documents**
If the register says the covenants are “contained in a Conveyance/Transfer dated [date]”, you need that deed. You can:
- Order “**official copies of the deed**” referred to on the register from HMLR (if available), or
- Get it from the seller’s solicitor in a transaction.

**Why this matters:** the register often only summarises; the **original deed** may include:
- full covenant wording,
- plans,
- definitions (e.g., what counts as “building”),
- who can enforce,
- any exceptions/consents procedure.

**Where:** HM Land Registry “Search for property information” service (official copies).

---

## 2) Check the “chain” document where the covenant was created
Restrictive covenants are commonly imposed on a sale/transfer of part of a larger estate. The deed might be:
- a **Conveyance** (older),
- a **Transfer (TR1)** (more modern),
- a **Deed of Covenant**.

Look for:
- **The covenant clause(s)** (e.g., “not to build”, “not to use other than as a single dwelling”, “no trade/business”, “no caravans”).
- **Any express “benefit” wording**: does it say the covenant is “for the benefit of the vendor’s retained land” or “for the benefit of each and every part of the estate”?
- **Any consent clause**: e.g., “with the consent of the Transferor (not to be unreasonably withheld)”—important for extensions/alterations.

---

## 3) Identify who can enforce it (this is often the real issue)
A restrictive covenant is only a practical problem if someone has **the benefit** and is likely/able to enforce.

To work that out, you usually look at:
- The deed wording (benefit/annexation/assignment),
- Any “building scheme”/estate scheme wording,
- Whether the benefitting land is identifiable and still exists.

**Where to look:**
- The covenant deed itself,
- Neighbouring titles (sometimes) to see whether benefit was annexed/assigned,
- Estate documentation if it’s a managed development.

In conveyancing, solicitors often discuss this under “**enforceability**”, “**benefit and burden**”, “**annexation**”, “**building scheme**”.

## 4) The core legal rules (statutes/case law) your solicitor relies on
If you want the “rulebook” beyond the documents:

### Key statutory sources
- **Law of Property Act 1925**
  - s.79 (sometimes relevant: covenants presumed to bind successors unless excluded)
  - s.84 (important: power to **modify/discharge** restrictive covenants via the Upper Tribunal)
- **Land Registration Act 2002** (how matters appear on the register / protection)

### Key legal principles (common law/equity)
- Restrictive covenants can “run with the land” in equity (classic principle from **Tulk v Moxhay**), but only if conditions are met (e.g., negative in substance, intended to run, benefit land, notice/registration position, etc.).

**Where to read these in plain-English form:**
- Practical Law (subscription), LexisPSL (subscription),
- “Megarry & Wade: The Law of Real Property” (textbook),
- “Gray & Gray: Elements of Land Law” (textbook),
- Law Society conveyancing practice notes (some are accessible; many aimed at practitioners).

## 5) If you want to change/remove a covenant: where to look
### A. **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) – LPA 1925 s.84 applications**
This is the formal route to modify/discharge if:
- covenant is obsolete,
- impedes reasonable use, etc.

**Where:**
- Gov.uk / Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) guidance,
- Published decisions (searchable online) to see how similar covenants are treated.

### B. **Negotiated release/consent**
If you can identify the beneficiary (often a neighbour, management company, or original developer), you may negotiate a **deed of release/variation**.

### C. **Restrictive covenant indemnity insurance**
Often used where:
- breach already exists (e.g., historic extension),
- enforcement risk is considered low,
- beneficiary is unknown or difficult to locate.

Important practical note: approaching the potential beneficiary for consent can sometimes make insurance unavailable—this is case-specific.

---

## 6) Additional places to check during due diligence
Depending on the covenant, also check:
- **Planning permissions and conditions** (separate from covenants, but often confused)
- **Building regulations approvals**
- **Local search results** (again separate, but relevant to use/building)
- **Management company/estate rentcharge documents** on modern estates (may contain “estate covenants”)

---

## 7) What to ask for / what to tell your conveyancer
If you’re buying/selling or planning works, ask your solicitor to:
1. Provide the **exact covenant wording** (not just a summary).
2. Explain **who has the benefit** and how it is enforceable.
3. Advise on risk given your intended use/works (extension, business use, Airbnb, outbuildings, pets, parking, etc.).
4. Set out options: **consent**, **insurance**, **variation**, **s.84 application**.

As a CLC‑regulated conveyancing practice, you must deal with the complaint under the **CLC’s Code of Conduct/Regulatory Arrangements on complaints** (your “complaints handling procedure”) and in a way that is **prompt, fair, transparent and client‑focused**. For a delay complaint, the CLC’s expectations are mainly about **having a proper written procedure, acknowledging and investigating quickly, keeping the client informed, providing a clear outcome (and remedy where appropriate), and signposting the Legal Ombudsman**.

Below is what you should do in practice (and what the CLC would expect to see evidenced on file).

---

## 1) Treat it as a formal complaint and trigger your complaints procedure
- **Record it immediately** as a complaint (date received, who received it, matter ref, issues raised, desired outcome).
- **Allocate it to the right person** under your procedure (typically a **Complaints Handler/Manager** or a senior person not directly responsible for the day‑to‑day handling if possible).
- **Check vulnerability / needs** (communication preferences, reasonable adjustments, language needs).

**CLC expectation:** You have a **written complaints policy** that is accessible to clients, and staff follow it consistently.

---

## 2) Acknowledge promptly and explain next steps
Send a written acknowledgement (email/letter) that:
- Confirms you’re treating it as a complaint.
- Identifies the person handling it and their contact details.
- Explains **what will happen next**, what information you may need, and an indicative timescale.
- Requests any missing details (key dates, what “delay” means to them, any losses).

**CLC expectation:** Early acknowledgement and clear communication about the process.

---

## 3) Investigate properly (and document it)
For a delay complaint, a proper investigation usually includes:
- **Chronology** of key steps taken and when (instruction date, contract pack, searches ordered/received, enquiries, mortgage offer, report, exchange, completion).
- **Root cause** analysis:  
  - Was delay caused by your firm (capacity, supervision, failure to chase, missed diary dates, poor handover, waiting on client ID/funds)?  
  - Or by third parties (seller’s solicitors, lender, management company, local authority searches)?
- **File review**: check attendance notes, emails, chasers, undertakings, requisitions, lender requirements.
- **Service standard**: compare what happened against your own service commitments and what is reasonably expected in conveyancing.
- **Supervision check**: was the file appropriately supervised and risk-managed?

**CLC expectation:** Complaints are investigated competently and the reasoning/evidence is recorded.

---

## 4) Keep the client updated during the complaint (and the conveyancing)
Because the underlying complaint is about delay, you should actively address both:
- **Complaint updates** (progress of investigation; any slippage in response times).
- **Matter updates** (what is happening now to progress the transaction; next milestones; what you need from the client; what you’re chasing).

**CLC expectation:** Ongoing communication is a key service duty, especially where delay is alleged.

---

## 5) Provide a clear written outcome (“final response”)
Your outcome letter should:
- **Answer each point** of the complaint in a structured way.
- Set out the **timeline** and what caused the delays.
- Say whether you **uphold / partially uphold / do not uphold** the complaint and why.
- Explain what you will do to put things right, which might include:
  - A corrective action plan for the transaction (priority handling, named fee earner, supervisor oversight, diarised chasing schedule).
  - **Apology** where appropriate.
  - **Fee reduction / waiver** or compensation for distress/inconvenience (where justified).
  - Reimbursement of direct, evidenced losses caused by your delay (where appropriate and proportionate).
- Confirm any **learning/remedial steps** internally (training, supervision, process changes).

**CLC expectation:** The response is reasoned, not defensive, and offers an appropriate remedy where service fell short.

---

## 6) Signpost escalation: Legal Ombudsman and the CLC (as appropriate)
Your final response should include **clear signposting** to the **Legal Ombudsman (LeO)** if the client remains dissatisfied, including:
- How to contact LeO (website/phone/post).
- The **time limits** for taking a complaint to LeO (you should state the applicable LeO deadline position in your letter—commonly: within a set period of your final response and subject to any longstop from the act/omission; check current LeO published time limits when drafting).

You should also:
- Distinguish between **service complaints** (LeO) and **conduct/regulatory issues** (CLC). A pure delay/service complaint is normally for **LeO**, but if the complaint indicates potential regulatory breaches (dishonesty, misuse of client money, serious competence issues), it may need separate consideration/reporting.

**CLC expectation:** Clients are told about their right to go to LeO and how to do so.

---

## 7) Consider whether the complaint reveals a reportable issue to the CLC
Most delay complaints will be a service issue, but you should still assess whether it indicates:
- A **systemic failure** (e.g., widespread backlogs, inadequate staffing, repeated missed deadlines).
- A **serious breach** of the CLC Code (e.g., failure to act in client’s best interests, serious lack of competence, misleading the client).
- **Client money risk** or other serious risk.

If it meets your internal threshold for a **material breach / serious issue**, follow your internal escalation to the COLP/Compliance Manager and consider whether the CLC must be notified under your regulatory reporting obligations.

**CLC expectation:** You identify and escalate material risks, not just respond to the individual complainant.

---

## 8) Maintain proper records and use the complaint for improvement
- Keep a complete **complaint file**: the complaint, acknowledgements, investigation notes, evidence relied on, final response, and any remedy.
- Log it in your **complaints register** and analyse trends.
- Implement and record **remedial actions** (process changes, training, supervision).

**CLC expectation:** Firms learn from complaints and can evidence governance and continual improvement.

---

## 9) Practical pointers specific to “delay” complaints
To align with CLC expectations on client care and communication, your response should usually address:
- What you told the client about **likely timescales** at instruction and any caveats.
- What you did to **chase third parties** and how often.
- Whether you **kept the client informed proactively** when timelines slipped.
- Whether you advised on **expiry risks** (mortgage offers, searches, related chain deadlines).
- Whether your firm’s **capacity** impacted progress and how you mitigated it.

---

### What you should do now (quick checklist)
1. Log complaint + assign handler.  
2. Acknowledge in writing with process/timescales.  
3. Investigate (chronology + cause + supervision).  
4. Update client and progress the transaction.  
5. Send final written outcome with remedy (if any).  
6. Signpost Legal Ombudsman with correct time limits.  
7. Consider CLC reporting if it indicates a serious/material breach.  
8. Record and feed into improvements.

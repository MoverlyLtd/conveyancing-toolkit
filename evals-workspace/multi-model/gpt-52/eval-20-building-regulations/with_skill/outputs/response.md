For 2018 replacement windows you should treat this as a **Building Regulations compliance evidence** issue (notably **Part L – conservation of fuel/energy efficiency/thermal performance**; also safety glazing requirements now sitting within Part K).

### 1) First: do **not** contact the local authority (yet)
If **anyone** (seller/buyer/agent/conveyancer) approaches Building Control “to check”, then **indemnity insurance will usually become unavailable** for this issue. Decide the strategy first.

### 2) Check whether the installer self-certified (quickest fix)
Most 2018 window replacements should have been done under a competent person scheme.

- Ask the seller for:
  - **FENSA** or **CERTASS** certificate, or
  - the installer’s scheme membership details / invoice showing who did the work.
- Independently search via **https://www.competentperson.co.uk** (often you can locate the certificate if it exists, or identify the scheme/provider).
  - For windows, the key schemes are **FENSA** and **CERTASS**.

If you can obtain the certificate (or a replacement copy from the scheme), that is usually treated as equivalent evidence of compliance and satisfies most lenders.

### 3) If no certificate exists: decide between regularisation vs indemnity
You essentially have two routes:

**A) Regularisation (retrospective Building Control approval)**
- Apply to the local authority for retrospective sign-off.
- **Typical cost:** **£400–800** (varies).
- **Timescale:** often **4–8 weeks**.
- **Pros:** actual compliance evidence; **generally preferred by lenders**.
- **Cons:** delay; inspection may require remedial works if non-compliant.

**B) Building Regulations indemnity insurance**
- **Only possible if no approach has been made to the local authority**.
- **Typical cost:** **£50–200** one-off.
- **Pros:** quick; commonly used for missing window certificates.
- **Cons:** covers enforcement/diminution risk **not defects** (e.g., poor installation, failed safety glazing, inadequate thermal performance); and **not all lenders accept** indemnity—check the lender’s requirements (UK Finance Lenders’ Handbook Part 2).

In practice, for replacement windows (a common, relatively low-enforcement-risk issue), many transactions proceed with **indemnity** if the lender agrees—*provided nobody has contacted the council*.

### 4) What to tell the client (and what you should do next)
Advise your client along these lines:

1. **We will first try to obtain evidence of self-certification** (FENSA/CERTASS) by:
   - requesting paperwork from the seller and
   - checking **competentperson.co.uk**.
2. **Do not contact the council** about the windows, because it can prevent indemnity insurance.
3. If no certificate is available, we will:
   - **check lender policy** (Part 2) on whether they will accept **indemnity** for missing window compliance, and
   - if acceptable, require the seller to provide a **Building Regulations indemnity policy** at their cost (usually £50–200) on completion; **or**
   - if the lender won’t accept indemnity (or the buyer wants certainty), push for **regularisation** (typically £400–800, several weeks) or renegotiate/retain.

### 5) Practical seller enquiries to raise
- Name/address of installer, invoice/receipt, any guarantee paperwork.
- Was the installer FENSA/CERTASS registered at the time?
- Any documentation showing U-values/installation spec (helpful but not a substitute for certification).

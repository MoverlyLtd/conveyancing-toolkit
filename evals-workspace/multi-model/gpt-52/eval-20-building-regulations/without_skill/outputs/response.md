In England & Wales, replacement external windows/doors installed since 1 April 2002 are “controlled work” under the Building Regulations. Compliance is usually evidenced by either:

1) a **FENSA/CERTASS (or other Competent Person Scheme) certificate**, or  
2) a **Local Authority Building Control (LABC) completion certificate**.

If neither exists for 2018 replacements, you should treat this as a **potential Building Regulations compliance gap** and advise on the practical options and risk.

## 1) Establish the facts (first steps)
- **Check the scheme register(s):** Ask for the installer name and search **FENSA** (and also **CERTASS**) by postcode. Sometimes sellers “misplace” certificates but a record exists and a duplicate can be ordered.
- **Get documentary details from seller:** invoice/contract, guarantee paperwork, installer details, any energy rating labels etc. These don’t replace Building Regs evidence but help with audit trail and may assist if you need to pursue retrospective sign-off.
- **Confirm scope:** were *all* windows replaced, any doors, any structural alterations (e.g., enlarged openings, removed lintels)? If openings were altered, the risk profile is higher.

## 2) Explain the legal/risk position to your client and lender
### Building Regulations risk
- Absence of a certificate does **not automatically mean non-compliant**, but you have **no proof** of compliance.
- Local authority has theoretical enforcement powers (time-limited in practice), but the bigger issues tend to be:
  - **future saleability** (your buyer later faces same query),
  - **lender requirements** (some lenders insist on evidence or indemnity),
  - **safety/performance** (toughened safety glass to critical locations, means of escape, ventilation, thermal performance, trickle vents, etc.).

### Time limits (practical enforcement)
- Councils generally have limited windows to take formal action, but that does **not** cure non-compliance. Also, if there’s a safety issue, the buyer still carries the defect.

## 3) Options you can recommend (with pros/cons)

### Option A — Obtain the correct certificate (best outcome)
**Preferred**: seller provides a duplicate **FENSA/CERTASS** certificate if the installer was registered.
- This is usually quick and avoids workarounds.

If the installer wasn’t registered (or cannot provide certificate), then:

### Option B — Retrospective Building Control “Regularisation” certificate
- The seller can apply to LABC for a **Regularisation Certificate** (retrospective approval).
- LABC may require:
  - inspection (possibly invasive—opening up trims/reveals),
  - evidence of glass spec, safety glazing, ventilation,
  - and if they can’t verify, they may require **remedial works**.
- This gives the cleanest conveyancing position but can cause **delay and cost**.

**When to push this:**  
- higher-value property, risk-averse buyer, nervous lender, or any indication openings were altered/structural work done.

### Option C — Indemnity insurance (common compromise, but limited)
If the buyer wants to proceed without regularisation, you can consider a **Building Regulations indemnity policy** (seller usually pays).

**Important limitations to explain clearly:**
- It generally covers losses arising from **local authority enforcement action** due to lack of approval—**not** the cost of putting defective windows right.
- It will typically be **invalidated** if:
  - the local authority has already been contacted about the works, or
  - an application for regularisation is made (or sometimes if the council is made aware in any way).
- It doesn’t address:
  - workmanship defects, water ingress, failed units,
  - fire/escape issues,
  - reduced value due to poor installation (unless tied to enforcement).
- Some lenders accept indemnity; some want proper certification.

**So you should only recommend indemnity** where:
- your client and lender are comfortable with the residual risk, and
- you have no reason to suspect defective installation or altered openings.

### Option D — Price reduction / retention / require remedial works
If there are concerns about quality or compliance:
- negotiate **price reduction** to reflect risk,
- in some transactions consider a **retention** (less common in standard residential purchases), or
- require seller to **replace/upgrade** and provide certification (but that can create new timing/certification issues).

## 4) Practical conveyancing steps you should take
- Raise a **specific additional enquiry**: confirm date of installation, name of installer, whether installer was FENSA/CERTASS registered, and request certificates/duplicates; ask whether any openings were enlarged or lintels altered.
- Check the **TA6** replies (and any guarantees) for consistency.
- Check **lender instructions** (UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook / part 2) and comply—if certificate absent, many firms will:
  - report to lender, and/or
  - ensure acceptable indemnity is in place if lender permits.
- Advise client to consider an **independent glazing/condition inspection** if they are at all uneasy (especially if windows are obviously poorly fitted, misted units, draughts, water staining, or escape windows appear undersized).

## 5) What I would typically advise (balanced recommendation)
1. **Ask seller to obtain a duplicate FENSA/CERTASS certificate** (fastest clean solution if it exists).  
2. If none exists, and there’s time: **push for LABC regularisation** (best evidentially, but may delay).  
3. If client wants to proceed quickly and lender agrees: **take Building Regs indemnity insurance**, *provided* no approach has been made to the council and there are no red flags about the installation/structural changes.  
4. If there are red flags or a cautious buyer: don’t rely solely on indemnity—**seek regularisation or renegotiate**.

## Key warning to give your client
- Indemnity insurance is **not a warranty of quality**. If the windows are poorly installed, the buyer pays to fix them. The policy mainly protects against the (unlikely, but not impossible) scenario of council enforcement because paperwork is missing.

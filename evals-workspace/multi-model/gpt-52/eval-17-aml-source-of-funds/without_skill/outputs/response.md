## A) £200,000 from client’s savings (Source of Funds)

### 1) Bank/building society statements showing accumulation and current holding
**What to obtain (typical):**
- **Bank statements for the account(s) holding the £200,000**, showing:
  - The running balance and that the funds are genuinely held by the client; and
  - The build‑up/credits consistent with the client’s explanation; and
  - The funds still available and earmarked for completion.
- **How many months?** There’s no fixed SRA number; a common baseline is **3–6 months**, but you should go **further back** if:
  - The £200k appeared as a **large recent credit**;
  - There are **multiple transfers** from other accounts/investments;
  - There are any **cash deposits** or unusual activity.

**SRA expectation in practice:** statements must evidence a credible trail. If the “savings” are long‑held and stable, shorter coverage may be proportionate; if the funds landed recently, you must trace their origin.

### 2) Evidence of the underlying source where needed (SoW/SoF link)
If the statements show the £200k came from identifiable sources, you should evidence those sources. Examples:

- **Employment income savings**
  - Recent **payslips** (e.g., last 3) and/or **P60**, and/or
  - **Employment contract**/letter confirming salary (if needed to support plausibility).

- **Bonus/dividends**
  - Dividend vouchers, share plan statements, or employer/plan administrator documentation.

- **Sale of investments**
  - Investment platform statements showing **sale/redemption** and transfer to the client’s bank.
  - Contract notes / transaction confirmations.

- **Sale of a previous property**
  - Completion statement from prior conveyancer and bank statement showing receipt.

**Key point:** You don’t always need full “life history” SoW for routine matters, but you do need enough to explain any **material lump sums** or **inconsistencies**.

### 3) If funds are moving through multiple accounts
- Statements for **each intervening account** to maintain continuity (no “black holes” in the audit trail).
- If using fintech/online banks, ensure statements are **downloaded PDFs** with name, account number, and date range (screenshots are weaker).

## B) £100,000 gift from parents (Source of Funds + donor due diligence)

A gifted deposit typically requires you to evidence:
1) **Donor identity** (CDD),  
2) **Donor’s source of funds** for the gift, and  
3) **No repayment / no interest in the property** (and lender requirements).

### 1) Donor CDD (parents)
Obtain for **each parent** who is gifting (even if only one is the account holder, you still identify both if both are donors):
- **Photo ID**: passport or driving licence.
- **Proof of address**: recent utility bill/bank statement/council tax statement (per your firm policy).
- Consider **electronic verification** in addition (common best practice).

### 2) Gift letter / declaration (also aligns with lender requirements)
A signed letter (or your firm’s form) confirming:
- Amount: **£100,000**.
- It is an **unconditional gift**.
- **No repayment** is expected/required.
- The donors will have **no beneficial interest** and will not reside at the property (if applicable).
- Relationship to the buyer.
- Confirmation funds are from the donors’ own monies (and not, e.g., borrowed).
- Consent to AML checks.

### 3) Donor Source of Funds evidence (trace the £100k)
You need to see where the parents’ £100k comes from and how it reaches your client.

**Core evidence:**
- **Bank statements** for the donor account from which the gift will be paid (commonly **3–6 months**, longer if risk indicates), showing:
  - The funds are held there; and
  - The outgoing transfer to the buyer (or to your client account if permitted) is identifiable.

**If the £100k is a lump sum that appeared recently**, you must evidence the origin, e.g.:
- **Property sale**: completion statement + bank statement showing receipt.
- **Savings over time**: longer run of statements showing gradual accumulation.
- **Investments**: sale/redemption statements + bank credit.
- **Pension lump sum**: pension provider statement + bank credit.
- **Inheritance**: probate/estate accounts or solicitor letter + bank credit.
- **Company funds/dividends**: dividend voucher, accounts, board minute (if relevant) + bank credit. (Be cautious if it looks like business money rather than personal funds.)

**If the parents are overseas / funds originate overseas:**
- Expect to obtain **more**:
  - Evidence of the foreign account;
  - Evidence of conversion/transfer route;
  - Consider certified translations if not in English;
  - Assess sanctions/PEP/adverse media and jurisdiction risk.

### 4) Transfer route controls (practical)
Preferably:
- Gift paid **from donor’s bank directly to your client’s bank** (then to you), or in some cases direct to client account if your policy/lender allows.
- Avoid unexplained third‑party payments into your client account.

You should be able to show a **clear chain**: donor account → client account (or solicitor) → completion.

## C) £450,000 mortgage (Source of Funds)

For the mortgage advance, SoF evidence is comparatively straightforward because it’s coming from a regulated lender and is documented.

### Evidence to retain:
- **Mortgage offer** (showing lender name, borrower, amount, property).
- **Mortgage conditions** / special conditions (re deposit/gift confirmation requirements).
- On completion: evidence the mortgage advance came from the lender (your **completion statement / ledger**, and lender’s funds arrival confirmation in your client account records).

**AML point:** you generally don’t “trace” the lender’s funds beyond the fact it is a regulated mortgage advance, but you must check it is consistent with the transaction and the buyer’s profile.

## File-level cross-checks the SRA will expect you to be able to demonstrate

### 1) Reconcile funds to the completion statement
You should be able to show, on file, a simple reconciliation:
- Purchase price £750,000  
- Less mortgage £450,000  
- Less gift £100,000  
- Less client savings £200,000  
= balance funded.

### 2) Consistency and plausibility checks
- Does £200k savings + £100k gift make sense given the client/donor circumstances?
- Any unusual features (e.g., complex layering, multiple third parties, crypto, cash deposits, rapid movement of funds) should trigger **enhanced due diligence** and potentially **SoW** enquiries.

### 3) Timing and “build-up” risk
If large sums arrive shortly before exchange/completion, you should:
- Identify the incoming credits;
- Obtain evidence explaining them;
- Keep a clear audit trail of transfers between accounts.

### 4) Record keeping
Maintain copies of:
- Statements (PDFs), not screenshots;
- ID documents/verification results;
- Gift letter/declaration;
- Notes of enquiries and rationale (risk-based decisions).

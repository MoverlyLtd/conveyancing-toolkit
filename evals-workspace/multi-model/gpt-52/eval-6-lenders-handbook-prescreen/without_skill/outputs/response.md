For UK conveyancing you need to look at the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook** (formerly CML). The key point is: **the Handbook doesn’t give one universal rule** for “72 years remaining” or “no building regs sign‑off”. It sets out (a) **Part 1** instructions that apply generally and (b) **Part 2** lender‑specific requirements. In practice, these two issues are both classic **“report to lender / don’t assume”** items unless you can satisfy the lender’s specific criteria.

Below is what the Handbook position is in substance, and how it usually plays out.

---

## 1) Lease term: 72 years unexpired

### What the Handbook is getting at
Under **Part 1 (general instructions)** you must:

- **Check the lease term** and ensure it meets the lender’s requirements for the whole mortgage term (and typically beyond).
- **Report to the lender** if the lease term is **short**, or if it will fall below the lender’s minimum during the mortgage term, or if it is otherwise likely to affect value/marketability.
- Ensure the lease meets general “good and marketable title” requirements and **does not contain unacceptable provisions** (ground rent, review clauses, forfeiture, restrictions on assignment etc. are also relevant, but you asked specifically about length).

### Why 72 years is a red flag
Even without quoting a single “Part 1 minimum”, **72 years is in the zone most lenders treat as short**. Many mainstream lenders want something like:

- a minimum unexpired term at completion (often **70–85 years** depending on lender), and/or
- a minimum term remaining at the end of the mortgage (e.g. **30+ years remaining** after the mortgage ends), and/or
- compliance with valuation requirements (the valuer may downvalue or mark unacceptable).

Also, once a lease drops under **80 years**, “marriage value” becomes payable on a statutory lease extension (which affects cost and marketability). Lenders know this and some harden criteria around 80 years.

### Handbook action you should take
- **Check the lender’s Part 2** for their exact minimum lease term requirement.
- If the lease is below that requirement (or borderline), you generally must **report to the lender** and/or ensure it is remedied.
- Usual “remedies” lenders accept (subject to Part 2):
  - **Lease extension completed on or before completion** (best outcome).
  - **Simultaneous completion** of lease extension and purchase.
  - Sometimes a lender will accept an undertaking/retention approach, but many won’t where the lease is already short because it’s a title/marketability issue rather than a “snag”.

**Practical point:** with **72 years**, you should expect a lot of lenders/valuers to insist on a **lease extension** (or at least that it’s in hand) rather than lending on the existing term.

## 2) Loft conversion without Building Regulations completion certificate

### What the Handbook is getting at
Part 1 generally requires you to:

- Ensure the property has **all necessary consents** (planning/building regulations) where required.
- **Report any breaches** of statutory requirements or lack of necessary consents that could:
  - prejudice the lender’s security,
  - affect marketability,
  - lead to enforcement action, or
  - invalidate insurance.

The Handbook’s general theme is: if something is missing that could affect value/marketability, **don’t just rely on assumption**—check the lender’s Part 2 and report where required.

### Building Regulations: why lenders care
A loft conversion without building regs sign‑off raises:
- **Safety/structural** risk (fire separation, escape routes, floor loading, stairs, insulation).
- **Enforcement/regularisation** risk (less common after time, but still an issue).
- **Valuation** risk: the valuer may disregard the additional area/bedroom if it lacks sign‑off.

### Handbook-compliant approach (typical)
You should consider:

1) **Ask for evidence**  
   - Building Regulations completion certificate / final sign‑off, or
   - Local authority records, or
   - Approved Inspector final certificate (if private building control used).

2) **If missing, consider regularisation**  
   - A **Regularisation Certificate** from the local authority (may require opening up works).  
   - This is often the “gold standard” if obtainable, but may not be practical before completion.

3) **Indemnity insurance** (only sometimes acceptable)  
   Many lenders will accept a **lack of building regulations** indemnity policy **if**:
   - no contact has been made with the local authority/building control about the works, and
   - there is no ongoing dispute/enforcement, and
   - the works are not very recent (insurers may have age requirements), and
   - the valuer is satisfied.

   **Important:** Indemnity insurance does **not** confirm safety/quality; it typically only covers loss arising from enforcement action/loss in value. Some lenders (and many valuers) treat that as inadequate for structural/fire safety concerns.

4) **Report to lender / follow Part 2**  
   If there’s no certificate, you typically must:
   - check the lender’s Part 2 stance on missing building regulations for alterations, and
   - **report** if required, especially if the valuer has noted it or if the loft forms a material part of the accommodation/value.

## 3) How these two issues interact (valuation and lender decision)
When you have **both**:
- **a short lease (72 years)** and
- **an undocumented loft conversion**,  

the risk is compounded because:
- the valuer may reduce the valuation for the loft (or ignore it), and
- the lender may be stricter on lease term if marketability is already impaired.

So even if the lender might tolerate one issue with conditions, they may not tolerate both without remediation.

---

## 4) What you should do next (Handbook-driven checklist)
1) **Identify the lender** and pull their **Part 2** requirements for:
   - minimum lease term; and
   - alterations / building regulations / indemnity insurance acceptability.
2) **Check the valuation report** (or ask what the valuer has assumed). If the valuer flags the loft or lease term, you almost certainly must report/comply.
3) **Advise the client** early:
   - Lease extension likely needed (and costs jump below 80 years).
   - Building regs evidence/regularisation vs indemnity.
4) **If in doubt, report to the lender** with the facts and proposed solution (lease extension plan; evidence/insurance/regularisation).

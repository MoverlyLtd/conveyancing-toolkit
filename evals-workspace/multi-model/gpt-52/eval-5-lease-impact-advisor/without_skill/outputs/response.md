A 95‑year lease is generally not a “problem” in itself, but it is a point to check carefully because lease length affects (1) mortgageability, (2) resale value, and (3) the cost/need to extend in future. With 95 years unexpired you are in a relatively healthy zone, but you should still plan ahead.

## 1) Mortgageability / lender requirements
Most mainstream UK lenders will lend on 95 years remaining (subject to their usual criteria). Typical lender rules are along the lines of:
- a minimum unexpired term at completion (often 70–80 years, sometimes higher); and/or
- a requirement that the lease has a certain number of years remaining at the end of the mortgage term (often 25–30 years).

At 95 years, you are usually comfortably above these thresholds. Your conveyancer should still confirm the specific lender’s criteria if you’re getting a mortgage.

## 2) The “80‑year” issue (marriage value)
A key cliff-edge is **80 years unexpired**. Once a lease drops below 80 years, the premium to extend typically increases because the leaseholder must pay **“marriage value”** (broadly, part of the increase in value created by granting the longer lease).

With **95 years**, you’re **above** that threshold, which is good. But it’s still worth thinking long-term:
- If you plan to own the flat for, say, 10–15 years, the lease will fall closer to 80, and extending later could become more expensive.
- Many buyers choose to extend while still above 80 years (or at least start planning for it).

## 3) Can you extend immediately?
Under the statutory route (Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993), a qualifying leaseholder generally has the right to:
- a **90‑year extension** on top of the existing term, and
- **peppercorn ground rent** (effectively nil),
provided they have owned the flat for **at least 2 years**.

As a buyer, you usually **cannot** use the statutory right immediately unless:
- the seller has owned for 2+ years **and**
- the seller serves a **Section 42 notice** before completion and assigns the benefit of that notice to you on completion (a common conveyancing strategy if an extension is needed).

At 95 years, you may not *need* to do this now, but it can still be negotiated if you want the comfort of dealing with it early.

## 4) Ground rent and lease terms matter as much as length
Even with 95 years left, other lease terms can cause real issues. Ask your conveyancer to check in particular:

### Ground rent clauses
- Is ground rent high now or does it **double** every 10/15/20 years or rise by RPI with aggressive terms?
- Some lenders are cautious about “onerous” ground rent terms (especially doubling clauses).
- Post-extension (statutory), ground rent should drop to peppercorn, but until then it can affect value and mortgageability.

### Service charge and building management
- Current service charge level, any major works planned (Section 20 notices), reserve fund.
- Any disputes with the freeholder/managing agent.
- Fire safety position (EWS1/external wall system), cladding, remediation costs—these can dwarf lease-length issues.

### Restrictions in the lease
- Subletting rules, pets, alterations, flooring restrictions, use of balconies, etc.

## 5) Resale and value considerations
A 95‑year lease is usually marketable. However:
- Flats do tend to become less attractive as they head towards 85–80 years, because buyers know extension costs rise below 80.
- If comparable flats nearby have very long leases (e.g., 125–999 years or share of freehold), that can affect pricing.

## 6) Practical guidance: should you be concerned?
**Usually: no immediate concern** about a 95‑year lease, **provided**:
- your lender is happy (normally yes),
- the ground rent isn’t onerous,
- service charge/building issues are acceptable, and
- you have a sensible plan for extension in the future (even if it’s “review in 5–10 years”).

## 7) What to ask your conveyancer (and/or surveyor)
1. Confirm the **exact unexpired term** and the date it was calculated from.  
2. Confirm lender acceptability and any lease defects.  
3. Review ground rent: amount now, review pattern, and whether it triggers lender concerns.  
4. Ask whether extending now is advisable and obtain an indicative valuation from a specialist lease extension valuer if you want certainty.  
5. Check service charge accounts, major works, and fire/building safety documentation.

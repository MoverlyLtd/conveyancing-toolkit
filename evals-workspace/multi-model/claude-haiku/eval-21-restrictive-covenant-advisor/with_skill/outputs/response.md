Okay, let's work through the key considerations for this restrictive covenant scenario:

1. The Covenant Wording
- The covenant restricts "use for any trade, business, or profession" on the property.
- This is a common restrictive covenant type, often used to preserve the purely residential nature of a neighborhood.

2. Enforceability Assessment
- The covenant is from 1965, so it predates the automatic annexation provisions of s78 Law of Property Act 1925. However, the lack of enforcement suggests the benefit may have been properly transmitted.
- The fact that the covenant has never been enforced, despite likely breaches by neighbors, significantly weakens its enforceability. This is an "acquiescence/waiver" argument that would help your client's case.
- Additionally, if the character of the neighborhood has changed over time, this could provide grounds for discharging the covenant under s84(a) LPA 1925 as "deemed obsolete".

3. Proposed Use and Breach Assessment
- Your client's intended use for a small home office, with no visible business activity or client visits, is likely to be considered a relatively low-risk "technical" breach. It does not fundamentally change the residential character of the property.
- More visible commercial uses, such as signage, regular deliveries, or client traffic, would be considered a more material breach with higher enforcement risk.

4. Options for Dealing with the Covenant
Given the assessment so far:

A. Do nothing: With the low-impact home office use and the historic lack of enforcement, this may be a viable option. The risk of the beneficiary suddenly choosing to enforce is relatively low.

B. Indemnity insurance: If no approach has been made to the beneficiary, indemnity insurance could be an option. Typical costs range from £50 to £300, depending on the insurer's assessment of the risk.

**⚠️ The critical point is that no approach must have been made to the beneficiary. Once contact occurs, the "no approach" rule means insurance becomes unavailable.**

C. Upper Tribunal application (s84 LPA 1925): If the covenant is considered obsolete due to the change in neighborhood character and the historic lack of enforcement, you could make a strong case for modification or discharge under s84(a) of the Act. Tribunal fees start from around £1,000, plus legal costs which could be £4,000-£5,000+.

D. Negotiate release: If the beneficiary can be identified, it may be possible to negotiate a release of the covenant, either for a one-off payment or an ongoing fee. The cost would depend on the beneficiary's bargaining position.

In summary, I would advise your client to:
1. Confirm no prior approach has been made to the beneficiary.
2. If not, obtain indemnity insurance in the region of £50-£300 as the most cost-effective option.
3. If insurance is not available due to prior contact, consider making a s84 application to the Upper Tribunal to seek modification or discharge of the covenant.

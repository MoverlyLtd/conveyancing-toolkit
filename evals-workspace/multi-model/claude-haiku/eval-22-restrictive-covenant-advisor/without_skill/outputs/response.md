Based on the information provided, here is the advice I would provide:

1. Review the restrictive covenant:
   - Obtain a copy of the restrictive covenant and carefully review the exact wording. Understand the scope and limitations of the covenant.
   - Determine whether the proposed rear extension would be considered an "alteration to the external appearance" and thus be prohibited by the covenant.

2. Assess the covenant's enforceability:
   - Examine the duration of the covenant. Restrictive covenants typically have a limited lifespan, and this one was imposed in 1992, over 30 years ago.
   - Consider whether the covenant has been abandoned or waived by the beneficiary's own extension in 2015. This may indicate that the covenant is no longer being actively enforced.
   - Investigate whether the covenant is still "relevant and enforceable" given the changes in the surrounding area and the passage of time.

3. Engage with the beneficiary:
   - Approach the beneficiary (the neighbour who has the benefit of the covenant) and attempt to negotiate a waiver or release of the covenant.
   - Explain the planned extension and its impact, and see if the beneficiary is willing to consent to the works.
   - Offer a compromise, such as agreeing to specific design elements or mitigation measures to address the beneficiary's concerns.

4. Obtain legal advice:
   - Consult a conveyancing solicitor or real estate lawyer to provide a professional opinion on the enforceability of the covenant and the best course of action.
   - The solicitor can advise on the legal process for seeking a modification or discharge of the covenant, if necessary.

5. Explore alternative options:
   - If the covenant is deemed enforceable and the beneficiary is unwilling to consent, consider alternative design options that may not violate the covenant, such as a less intrusive extension or internal remodelling.
   - Explore the possibility of obtaining planning permission for the proposed works, as this may override the covenant.

The key considerations are the specific wording of the covenant, its enforceability given the time elapsed and the beneficiary's own actions, and the willingness of the beneficiary to agree to the proposed extension. A careful legal analysis and engagement with the beneficiary are essential to determine the best approach for your client.

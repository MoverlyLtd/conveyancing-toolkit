To understand the rules around restrictive covenants on a freehold property in the UK, you should primarily look at the following sources:

1. The Title Deeds:
   - The title deeds for the property will contain details of any restrictive covenants that apply to the property. These covenants are usually recorded in the freehold title or in earlier deeds in the property's history.
   - The restrictive covenants will outline the specific restrictions or obligations that the current and future owners of the property must adhere to.

2. Land Registry:
   - The Land Registry holds records of the registered title for the property, including any registered restrictive covenants.
   - You can obtain a copy of the title register and title plan from the Land Registry, which will provide information on any registered restrictive covenants.

3. Planning Regulations:
   - Local planning authorities may also have information on restrictive covenants that have been imposed as part of the planning process for the property or the local area.
   - You should check with the local planning authority to see if there are any planning-related restrictive covenants that apply to the property.

4. Conveyancing Solicitor:
   - When purchasing a freehold property, your conveyancing solicitor will review the title deeds and other relevant information to identify any restrictive covenants that apply to the property.
   - The solicitor can advise you on the implications of the restrictive covenants and whether they pose any issues or limitations for your intended use of the property.

Key things to understand about restrictive covenants on freehold properties:
- They are legally binding obligations that run with the land, meaning they apply to the current and future owners of the property.
- Covenants can restrict the use of the property, such as prohibiting certain activities, limiting building work, or requiring the owner to maintain the property in a certain way.
- Breaching a restrictive covenant can result in legal action being taken by the party with the benefit of the covenant.
- It may be possible to modify or remove a restrictive covenant, but this usually requires the consent of the party with the benefit of the covenant.

By thoroughly reviewing the title deeds, Land Registry records, and any planning information, you can identify the specific restrictive covenants that apply to the freehold property and understand the implications for your intended use of the property.

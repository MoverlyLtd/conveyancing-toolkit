Okay, let's go through the specific AML source of funds evidence required for each funding element in this £750,000 property purchase:

Savings (£200,000):
- 12 months of bank/building society statements showing the accumulation of the £200,000 savings
- The account holder name on the statements must match the client
- Review the statements to ensure there is a consistent and credible savings pattern, and trace any large lump sum deposits within the 12 months

Gift from Parents (£100,000):
- Photographic ID (passport or driving licence) for the parents/giftors
- 3-6 months of bank statements for the parents/giftors showing they have the £100,000 to gift
- A signed gift letter confirming the amount, that it is a gift with no obligation to repay, and that the parents have no interest in the property
- Proof of the relationship between the client and the parents (e.g. birth certificates)
- Notification to the mortgage lender about the gifted deposit (per UK Finance Lender's Handbook)

Mortgage (£450,000):
- Mortgage offer from the FCA-regulated lender
- Verification that the lender is indeed FCA-regulated (check the FCA register)
- No further source of funds checks are required on the mortgage element itself, as the lender will have conducted its own customer due diligence

In addition to the above, you should:

- Risk-assess the overall transaction, each funding element, and the client profile
- Escalate to enhanced due diligence (EDD) if any red flags are identified, such as the client being a PEP, the funds originating from a high-risk jurisdiction, or any other unusual transaction features
- Document your assessments, the evidence obtained, and the reasons for your decisions throughout the process
- Continuously monitor for any changes to the funding structure during the transaction and re-assess as necessary (Regulation 28(12))
- Be prepared to file a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) with the MLRO/NCA if you have any suspicions of money laundering, even if you decide to continue acting (obtain a DAML if needed)

The key is to take a risk-based, not tick-box, approach and ensure you have robust evidence to verify the source and legitimacy of all funds used in the purchase.

Assuming the purchase is in **England or Northern Ireland**, your client is **buying an additional dwelling** (they already own and occupy a property and are not replacing their main residence), so the **higher rates for additional dwellings (HRAD)** apply under **Finance Act 2003, Schedule 4ZA** (the “3% surcharge” regime).

### 1) Rate to use
For HRAD, you add **3 percentage points** to each residential SDLT band (FA 2003 **Sch 4ZA**, and the residential rates in **FA 2003 s.55**).

### 2) SDLT calculation on £300,000 (second property)
Residential SDLT bands (standard) and HRAD equivalents:

- **£0–£250,000**: standard 0% → **HRAD 3%**
- **£250,001–£925,000**: standard 5% → **HRAD 8%**

Compute:

1. **First £250,000 at 3%**  
   £250,000 × 3% = **£7,500**

2. **Remaining £50,000 (£300,000 − £250,000) at 8%**  
   £50,000 × 8% = **£4,000**

**Total SDLT = £7,500 + £4,000 = £11,500**

### 3) What portion is the “additional dwelling surcharge”?
Compare to standard residential SDLT (no HRAD):

- Standard SDLT on £300,000:  
  £0–£250k at 0% = £0  
  £50k at 5% = **£2,500**  
  **Standard total: £2,500**

HRAD total is **£11,500**, so the **extra (surcharge) element** is:
**£11,500 − £2,500 = £9,000**  
(Which is also **3% of the whole £300,000 = £9,000**.)

### Practical checks / caveats (so you don’t misapply HRAD)
- HRAD generally applies if, at completion, the buyer owns another dwelling and is not replacing their only/main residence (FA 2003 **Sch 4ZA**). On your facts, they already own a £180k home and are buying a buy-to-let, so **HRAD applies**.
- If there are **multiple purchasers**, HRAD can apply if **any** purchaser meets the conditions (important for joint buyers).
- If the new purchase were a **replacement of main residence** (sell old home and buy new), HRAD may not apply or may be **refundable** in some scenarios—doesn’t sound applicable here.

**Answer: Total SDLT payable = £11,500 (including the additional dwelling surcharge).**

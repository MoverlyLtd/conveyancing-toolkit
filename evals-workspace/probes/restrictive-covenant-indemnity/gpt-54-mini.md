A covenant against “business use” is usually broader than most clients expect, but a quiet home office with no visitors is often arguable as **ancillary residential use** rather than a breach. That said, the risk depends on the exact wording.

## 1) First, check the wording carefully
Key distinctions:

- **“No business use” / “not to use for trade, business or profession”**  
  This can catch home-working in principle.
- **“Not to use as a shop, office, showroom…”**  
  Usually aimed at visible/commercial use and client traffic.
- **“No nuisance / no offensive trade”**  
  Much narrower and often not relevant to a small home office.

If the office is entirely internal, no signage, no deliveries beyond ordinary domestic levels, and no visitors, the argument is that the property remains **predominantly residential**.

## 2) Practical options

### Option A: Proceed and rely on low-risk interpretation
If the lender is happy and the covenant is old, dormant, and apparently unenforced, many people simply proceed without taking action.  
However, this leaves a theoretical risk of enforcement by the beneficiaries.

### Option B: Obtain a deed of release / variation
If you can identify the benefiting party, they can agree to release or modify the covenant.

Pros:
- removes the issue properly

Cons:
- may be impossible to identify who has the benefit
- may trigger questions and delay
- if the beneficiary is approached, it can also make indemnity insurance unavailable

### Option C: Apply to the Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)
If the covenant is obsolete or impedes reasonable use, an application can be made under:

- **Law of Property Act 1925, s.84**

Grounds potentially relevant:
- **s.84(1)(a)**: covenant is obsolete
- **s.84(1)(aa)**: continued existence impedes reasonable use and does not secure practical benefits of substantial value
- **s.84(1)(c)**: beneficiaries consent or agree

This is the formal route, but it is slower and more expensive than insurance.

### Option D: Buy indemnity insurance
This is the usual conveyancing solution where:
- there has been no known enforcement,
- no approach has been made to the beneficiary,
- and the client wants comfort without opening negotiations.

Important: if you contact the beneficiary before buying cover, insurers will usually refuse to insure.

## 3) Enforcement risk after 60 years
The fact the covenant was imposed in **1965** and has **never been enforced** is helpful, but it does **not extinguish** it.

Possible practical defences if challenged:
- the activity is not a true “business use” but an incidental domestic use;
- no actual loss or interference has occurred;
- no breach has occurred on the facts, so no cause of action arises.

But “long non-enforcement” alone is not a legal defence.

## 4) Indemnity insurance: typical cost
For a **standard restrictive covenant indemnity policy** on a mainstream residential property, premiums are often very modest.

Typical market ranges:
- **£100–£250** for lower-risk residential transactions
- **£250–£500** where the risk is slightly higher or the property value is higher
- sometimes more if the title is unusual, the covenant is particularly broad, or the insured value is high

A common pricing basis is based on:
- property value,
- mortgage advance,
- and perceived risk.

As a rough practical guide:
- a straightforward policy on a normal house can often be obtained for around **£150–£300**
- if the lender requires it as part of a mortgage transaction, the premium is often still in that range

Usually the policy covers:
- legal costs of defending a claim,
- diminution in value,
- and sometimes any settlement/successor loss, up to the policy limit.

## 5) What to avoid
Do **not**:
- contact the covenant beneficiary if you want indemnity insurance;
- invite a complaint by advertising the home office;
- make alterations that look commercial;
- assume “no enforcement since 1965” makes it safe.

## 6) Best conveyancing approach
In practice, the usual sequence is:

1. read the covenant wording precisely;
2. assess whether the proposed use is genuinely ancillary domestic use;
3. if there is still doubt and no beneficiary contact has been made, obtain indemnity insurance;
4. if the client wants a definitive solution and a beneficiary can be identified, consider release or a **s.84 LPA 1925** application.

### 1) Start with the wording (because “business use” covenants vary a lot)
The options depend heavily on whether the covenant prohibits **any trade/business whatsoever** or only a **nuisance/visible/retail-type** use. In older estates (1960s) you often see wording like:

- “Not to use the property for any trade or business whatsoever…”
- “Not to carry on any trade or business… so as to be a nuisance or annoyance…”
- “Not to use otherwise than as a private dwellinghouse…”

A **purely administrative home office** with no visitors, no signage, no deliveries, and no disturbance is often arguable as still being use “as a private dwellinghouse” (fact-sensitive), but if the wording is absolute (“any trade or business whatsoever”) the technical risk remains even if the breach is low-impact.

Practical step: obtain **official copies of the register and filed deed** (or the 1965 conveyance/transfer if not filed) and identify:
- the **exact covenant wording**;
- who has the benefit (developer/estate owners/management company/adjacent owners);
- whether the benefit is **annexed** to retained land / assigned / part of a building scheme.

---

### 2) Enforceability and real-world risk
Even if never enforced since 1965, that does **not** automatically extinguish it.

**Key points:**
- **Delay alone** does not necessarily bar enforcement of restrictive covenants (equitable relief can still be sought); however, long non-enforcement can make enforcement less likely in practice and can support arguments like acquiescence/waiver depending on facts.
- The biggest practical uncertainty is often **who can enforce** and whether they are still identifiable/active (e.g., successors to the developer, a residents’ company, or neighbouring owners under a building scheme).
- If the activity is genuinely “home working” (laptop/phone), the risk of complaint is typically low.

### 3) Options (from lowest friction to most definitive)

#### Option A — Treat it as not a breach (best where facts/wiring support it)
If the intended use is:
- no client visits
- no employees attending
- no signage
- no noise/odour/extra traffic
- no business rates / no planning change of use

…you may advise it is **unlikely to be actionable in practice**, and may even be arguably consistent with residential use, depending on wording.

**Pros:** no cost, no third-party contact, no delays.  
**Cons:** not a “clean” solution if an absolute covenant exists; future buyer/lender may still want comfort.

Practical advice: document the client’s intended use clearly (statement of intended low-impact home working). Avoid creating evidence that invites scrutiny (advertising the address as “premises”, Google business listing showing customer footfall, signage, etc).

---

#### Option B — Restrictive covenant indemnity insurance (common conveyancing solution)
A **restrictive covenant indemnity policy** is typically used where:
- there is a technical breach or risk of breach, but
- the risk of enforcement is low, and
- you do **not** want to approach the beneficiaries.

**Crucial practical warning:** if you (or the client) **contact the beneficiary** (or managing agent/residents’ company) to ask for consent, or otherwise “tip off” enforcement parties, you can invalidate the ability to obtain insurance (or make it more expensive/subject to exclusions). Insurers usually insist there has been **no approach** and **no known dispute/complaint**.

What it typically covers:
- legal costs of defending enforcement action,
- damages/compensation,
- sometimes diminution in value (policy-dependent),
- settlement sums.

Common exclusions:
- deliberate, significant intensification (e.g., client visits, staff, noisy use),
- anything that creates a nuisance,
- if enforcement has already been threatened/complained about,
- breach of planning/building regs (separate issues).

**Cost (typical ranges):**
Premiums vary by insurer, property value, and perceived risk, but for a single dwelling with low-risk “home office”:
- **~£50–£250** is common for “restrictive covenant / no business use” cover on standard residential transactions.
- If property value is higher or wording/beneficiary position increases risk, more like **£250–£750**.
- Very high values (e.g., £1m+) or higher perceived risk can push **£750–£2,000+**, but that’s less typical for a quiet home office scenario.

If you want “exact figures”: insurers don’t publish a fixed tariff, but in mainstream conveyancing practice you’ll frequently see quotations around:
- **£75–£150** for many sub-£500k homes, low-risk covenant,
- **£150–£350** for £500k–£1m,
subject always to underwriting.

Practical advice:
- place the policy **before** any contact with potential beneficiaries,
- ensure the client’s intended use fits within the insurer’s assumptions (no visitors/signage),
- keep the policy with the title deeds and ensure it is **assignable to successors in title and mortgagees** (most are).

---

#### Option C — Seek formal consent / deed of release or variation (most “definitive” but can be hardest)
If you can identify who has the benefit and they are willing, you can negotiate:
- **Deed of Release** (discharge of covenant) or
- **Deed of Variation** (permit home working / office use).

**Pros:** clean title; easiest for future sales without insurance caveats.  
**Cons:** can be slow, expensive, and can invite scrutiny/requests for payment; and once you approach, indemnity insurance may no longer be available.

Costs:
- Beneficiary’s legal fees (often **£500–£2,000+ VAT**),
- Your client’s legal fees,
- Plus any negotiated premium/consideration (could be modest or could be substantial, depending on leverage and estate policy).

---

#### Option D — Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber) application to modify/discharge (rare for “home office”)
Under **Law of Property Act 1925, s.84**, the Upper Tribunal can discharge/modify a restrictive covenant on certain grounds, including (most relevant here):

- **s.84(1)(a)**: covenant is obsolete due to changes in character of property/neighbourhood;
- **s.84(1)(aa)**: impedes reasonable use of land, and does not secure practical benefits of substantial value/advantage, or is contrary to public interest (with compensation possible);
- **s.84(1)(c)**: beneficiaries have agreed (expressly or impliedly by conduct);
- **s.84(1)(b)**: beneficiaries will not be injured by discharge/modification.

**Pros:** produces a legally definitive outcome.  
**Cons:** slow, contentious, and disproportionate for low-impact home working; costs can easily be **£10,000–£30,000+** (and potentially higher) with risk of adverse costs.

---

### 4) Planning is separate (but relevant)
Even if the covenant is dealt with, consider planning: many councils accept home working as incidental to residential use, but if it becomes material (visitors, staff, deliveries), planning could bite.

---

### 5) Recommended conveyancing approach for your scenario
Given:
- covenant from 1965,
- never enforced,
- small home office, not visible, no client visits,

Typical practitioner approach is:

1) **Do not contact** anyone who might have the benefit (until decision made on insurance).  
2) If the wording is absolute, assume technical risk and **place indemnity insurance** (Option B), ensuring the policy matches the intended low-impact use.  
3) Advise client to keep the activity low-key (no signage/clients/staff) to stay within “home office” parameters and insurer comfort.  
4) If the client anticipates any escalation (clients visiting, employees, therapy practice etc.), consider seeking **express consent** (Option C) rather than relying on insurance that may not cover intensification.

This is a common scenario in residential conveyancing. Whether a home office constitutes a breach of a "no business use" covenant depends on the specific wording and the nature of the use.

Here is the technical breakdown, options, and costs.

### 1. Is there actually a breach?
In English law, many "no business" covenants are interpreted as prohibiting use that changes the residential character of the property. 
*   **The "Incidental" Rule:** If the use is purely administrative, involves no employees, no visiting clients, and no external advertising, it is often considered "incidental to the enjoyment of a private dwelling house."
*   **Case Law:** In *Low v Stott [1970]*, it was suggested that use which does not affect the amenity of the neighborhood might not breach a covenant restricted to "private dwelling house only."
*   **Section 1 of the Business Tenancies (Extension of Protection) Act 2015:** While this relates to tenancies, it reflects a modern legal shift towards allowing "home businesses" that do not detract from the residential nature of the area.

**Practical Advice:** If the client is only using a laptop and a desk, many solicitors would argue no breach has occurred. However, for the purposes of a sale/purchase, the "safe" route is to treat it as a potential breach to satisfy mortgage lenders.

---

### 2. Option A: Restrictive Covenant Indemnity Insurance (Recommended)
This is the standard solution. It protects the owner (and lender) against legal costs and loss of property value if the person with the benefit of the covenant (PWB) attempts to enforce it.

*   **Eligibility:** The covenant must be at least 12 months old, and there must have been no contact with the PWB regarding the breach.
*   **The "No Tipping Off" Rule:** **Crucial.** If the client or their solicitor contacts the PWB (often a developer or successor in title) to ask for permission, they will be unable to obtain indemnity insurance.
*   **Estimated Costs:** For a property valued between **£300,000 and £500,000**, a one-off premium typically ranges from **£180 to £350**. For a property worth **£1m+**, expect **£500 - £900**.
*   **Coverage:** The policy lasts in perpetuity and usually passes to future owners.

---

### 3. Option B: Section 84 Law of Property Act 1925
A client can apply to the **Upper Tribunal (Lands Chamber)** to have the covenant discharged or modified under **Section 84(1)** of the Law of Property Act 1925.

*   **Grounds for Application:** Usually **Ground (a)** (the covenant is obsolete due to changes in the neighborhood) or **Ground (aa)** (it impedes reasonable user and provides no practical benefit of substantial value).
*   **Risks/Costs:** This is the "nuclear option." It is slow (12–24 months) and expensive. Legal fees and surveyor reports can easily exceed **£10,000–£15,000**. If the application is contested, the client may have to pay the other side's costs.
*   **Practical Advice:** This is rarely used for a simple home office. It is usually reserved for developers wishing to build multiple houses on land restricted to one dwelling.

---

### 4. Option C: Negotiation for a Release/Variation
The client can approach the PWB to formally vary the deed. 

*   **Risks:** If the PWB is a defunct development company, the "successor in title" can be hard to find. If you find them, they will likely demand a **"Release Fee"** (often calculated as a percentage of the uplift in property value) plus their own legal costs (approx. £1,000+).
*   **Practical Advice:** Avoid this. It is expensive and, as noted above, prevents you from getting insurance if the PWB says "no."

---

### 5. Mortgage Lender Requirements
If your client is selling or remortgaging, you must consult the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook**.
*   **Section 6.10:** Most lenders will accept a restrictive covenant if there is no evidence of it being enforced and you provide an indemnity policy.
*   **Enforcement history:** Since the covenant has not been enforced since 1965, the risk is statistically negligible. Under the principle of **Laches (unreasonable delay)**, if a PWB knows of a breach and does nothing for a long period (usually 20 years, but often as little as 2–6 years for active breaches), the court is unlikely to grant an injunction.

---

### Summary Checklist & Practical Advice
1.  **Do not contact the original covenantee.** This preserves the option for insurance.
2.  **Verify the wording.** Does it say "no trade or business" or "private dwelling house only"? The latter is slightly stricter but still subject to the "incidental use" test.
3.  **Check for "General Development Orders".** Ensure the home office doesn't require planning permission (it shouldn't, based on your description), as a planning application is a public record that could "tip off" the PWB.
4.  **Quote for Insurance:** Get a quote from a provider like *Countrywide*, *Dual*, or *Stewart Title*.
5.  **Final Recommendation:** Advise the client to **obtain indemnity insurance**. It is the cheapest, fastest, and most "marketable" way to resolve the issue for future buyers and lenders.

To comply with the **SRA Standards and Regulations** and the **Legal Sector Affinity Group (LSAG) Anti-Money Laundering Guidance** (the current gold standard for AML in the UK legal sector), you must satisfy yourself of both the **Source of Funds (SoF)** and, crucially for these amounts, the **Source of Wealth (SoW)**.

Under LSAG Section 6.18, you are required to understand how the client "accumulated" their total wealth, not just where the money is sitting now.

Here is the specific evidence required for each element:

---

### 1. Personal Savings (£200,000)
For a sum of this size, you cannot simply accept a bank statement showing the balance. You must evidence the **accumulation**.

*   **Evidence Required:**
    *   **Bank Statements:** At least 6 months of original statements showing the gradual build-up of funds. Look for regular transfers from a salary account.
    *   **Proof of Income:** If the savings came from salary, request the last 3-6 months of payslips or a P60.
    *   **Sale of Assets (if applicable):** If the £200k came from a previous house sale, provide the **Completion Statement** from the previous conveyancer and a bank statement showing the lump sum entering the account.
    *   **Dividends/Bonuses:** If sourced from business profits, provide dividend vouchers or a letter from an accountant.
*   **SRA Focus:** You are looking for "consistency." Does a £200k saving pot align with the client’s known occupation and age?

### 2. Gift from Parents (£100,000)
A £100,000 gift is a "high-risk" indicator. Under **LSAG 6.18.2**, you must treat the giftors (the parents) with a level of scrutiny similar to the client.

*   **Evidence Required from Parents:**
    *   **Evidence of Identity:** Certified ID and proof of address for both parents to satisfy KYC (Know Your Customer) requirements.
    *   **Signed Gift Letter:** This must state:
        1. The amount (£100,000).
        2. That it is an unconditional gift with no repayment required.
        3. That the parents will have no legal or equitable interest in the property.
    *   **Parents’ Bank Statements:** Showing the £100,000 sitting in their account and the transaction history of how *they* acquired it (e.g., pension drawdown, savings, or downsizing).
*   **Practical Advice:** Do not just check the money leaving the parents' account; you must see it *entering* their account if it arrived as a lump sum recently (e.g., from a maturing ISA or inheritance).

### 3. Mortgage (£450,000)
While the lender is a regulated entity, you still have a duty to ensure the mortgage details align with the transaction.

*   **Evidence Required:**
    *   **Copy of the Mortgage Offer:** Ensure the figures match the £450,000 stated.
    *   **Verification of Lender:** Ensure the lender is FCA-regulated (Standard UK lenders like Nationwide, HSBC, etc., are low risk).
*   **Section Reference:** **LSAG 18.2** notes that while you can generally rely on the fact that a regulated lender has done their own AML checks, you must still check for "red flags," such as the mortgage coming from an unusual offshore lender or a private un-regulated company.

### Practical Advice & Compliance Tips

1.  **The "Holistic" View (LSAG 6.18.1):** The SRA expects you to "evaluate" the evidence, not just collect it. If the client is 22 years old and has £200,000 in savings from "salary," this is a red flag that requires deeper questioning (e.g., high-frequency trading, inheritance, or crypto-assets).
2.  **Electronic IDV:** Use an electronic verification platform (like Thirdfort, Credas, or InfoTrack) to verify the parents and the client. Many of these tools now include **Open Banking**, which can automatically categorize source of funds and flag anomalies.
3.  **The Audit Trail:** Ensure your file contains a **Source of Funds/Wealth Memo**. This is a short note written by you explaining: *"I have reviewed the £100k gift from the parents; I have seen their bank statements confirming the funds came from a 2022 pension lump sum. I am satisfied this is low risk."*
4.  **Red Flags:** Watch out for "fragmented funds" (money arriving from multiple different accounts) or funds arriving from high-risk jurisdictions. If the parents' £100k is coming from an overseas account, you must apply **Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)** under **Regulation 33 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017**.

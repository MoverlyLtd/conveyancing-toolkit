Yes — **most likely they will need to serve a Party Wall notice**.

For a **single-storey rear extension** to a semi-detached house, if the proposed works include excavation **within 3 metres** of the adjoining owner’s building **and** the excavation will go **below the level of that neighbour’s foundations**, this is a **section 6** matter under the **Party Wall etc. Act 1996**.

## Relevant statutory basis
- **Section 6(1)** Party Wall etc. Act 1996: excavation within **3 metres** of an adjoining structure and to a lower level than its foundations.
- If the extension is built **on or up to** the boundary, **section 1** may also apply.
- If the works involve cutting into a party wall, inserting beams, or raising/thickening a party wall, **section 2** may apply.
- For a typical rear extension, the key issue is usually **section 6**; sometimes **section 1** as well if the build is on the line of junction.

## Do they need a notice?
### If the excavation criteria are met:
**Yes.** A notice under **section 6** must be served on the adjoining owner(s).

If the new wall will be:
- **on the line of junction**: serve a **Section 1 notice**
- **astride the boundary**: needs the neighbour’s **written consent**
- **within 3m and deeper than neighbour’s foundations**: serve a **Section 6 notice**
- **also affecting a party wall**: serve **Section 2 notice** as well

## Notice period / timeline
### Section 6 notice
- Must be served **at least 1 month before** the excavation starts.
- The notice must usually describe:
  - the proposed works,
  - the location and depth,
  - and generally be sufficient for the adjoining owner to understand the impact.

### Section 1 notice
- Must be served **at least 1 month before** the start of the new wall works.

### Section 2 notice
- Must be served **at least 2 months before** the works to the party wall/structure begin.

## What happens after service?
The adjoining owner has **14 days** to respond.

They can:
1. **Consent**
2. **Dissent** and appoint a **party wall surveyor**
3. Do nothing

If they do nothing:
- after 14 days, the notice is treated as **disputed**
- the surveyor appointment process starts

## If there is a dispute
If the neighbour dissents or fails to respond:

### Surveyor process
- Either:
  - each owner appoints a surveyor, or
  - both agree on **one agreed surveyor**
- The surveyor(s) then prepare a **Party Wall Award**

### Party Wall Award
This sets out:
- the works permitted,
- access rights,
- working hours,
- protective measures,
- how damage is dealt with,
- who pays the surveyor fees

### Timescale
There is no fixed statutory deadline for the Award, but in practice it often takes:
- **2–6 weeks** if straightforward
- longer if surveyors need to negotiate

## Practical advice for your client
1. **Check the foundations** of the neighbour’s property before serving notice.  
   The 3m rule only bites if the excavation goes **below** their foundation level.

2. **Get a party wall surveyor involved early** if there is any risk of dispute or complex foundations.

3. **Serve the notices before completion of the purchase** if possible, only if the seller is willing and has authority; otherwise the client will need to serve after they own the property.

4. **Do not start works** until:
   - the notice period has expired, and
   - either consent has been given or an Award is in place if there is dissent/default.

5. If the extension is close to the boundary, also check whether **section 1** notice is needed in addition to section 6.

## Bottom line
For a rear extension within **3 metres** of a neighbour’s house, your client will **usually need a Party Wall notice under section 6**, served **at least 1 month before** excavation, and if the works involve boundary construction or party wall interference, **sections 1 and/or 2** may also apply.

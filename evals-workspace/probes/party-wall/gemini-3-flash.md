Under the **Party Wall etc. Act 1996 (PWA 1996)**, your client will almost certainly need to serve one or more notices. Because the extension is a "rear extension" on a "semi-detached" house within 3 metres of the neighbour, three specific sections of the Act are likely engaged.

### 1. Statutory Requirements (The "Three-Metre Rule")

The most common trigger for a rear extension is **Section 6(1)** of the Act.

*   **Section 6 Notice (Adjacent Excavation):** If your client is excavating for foundations within **3 metres** of the neighbour’s building **and** that excavation will go **deeper** than the neighbour's own foundations, a notice is mandatory. 
    *   *Practical Note:* Most modern extensions require foundations at least 1 metre deep. Older semi-detached houses often have shallow footings (approx. 300mm–600mm). Therefore, Section 6 is almost always triggered.
*   **Section 1 Notice (Line of Junction):** If your client intends to build the new wall exactly on the boundary line (the line of junction), they must serve notice under Section 1.
*   **Section 2 Notice (Existing Party Wall):** If the extension involves "cutting into" the existing party wall of the semi-detached house (e.g., to insert a steel beam/lintel for the new opening), notice is required under Section 2(2)(f).

### 2. The Process

The process follows a strict statutory timeline:

#### Step A: Serving Notice
*   **Section 6 & Section 1:** Must be served at least **1 month** before work starts.
*   **Section 2:** Must be served at least **2 months** before work starts.
*   **Content:** The notice must be in writing and include the client’s name, a description of the work, and (for Section 6) a plan and sections showing the site and depth of the new excavation.

#### Step B: The Neighbour’s Response
The neighbour (Adjoining Owner) has **14 days** to respond in writing. There are three potential outcomes:
1.  **Consent:** The neighbour agrees in writing. The client can proceed immediately (subject to planning/building regs). A "Schedule of Condition" is still highly recommended to protect the client against fraudulent damage claims.
2.  **Dissent:** The neighbour objects or suggests changes. This triggers a "statutory dispute."
3.  **Deemed Dispute:** If the neighbour does not respond within 14 days, they are "deemed to have dissented." Under **Section 10**, surveyors must then be appointed.

#### Step C: The Award (If Dissenting)
If there is a dispute, surveyors are appointed to negotiate a **Party Wall Award**. This document sets out how the work will be carried out, working hours, and records the condition of the neighbour's property.
*   **Agreed Surveyor:** Both parties use the same surveyor (cheaper).
*   **Two Surveyors:** Each party appoints their own. The two surveyors then select a "Third Surveyor" to act as an adjudicator if they cannot agree.

### 4. Practical Advice for your Client

1.  **Informal Discussion First:** Advise your client to speak to their neighbour over tea before serving the formal papers. A friendly neighbour is more likely to "Consent," saving the client **£2,000+** in surveyor fees.
2.  **Costs:** Under the Act, the "Building Owner" (your client) usually pays **all** costs, including the neighbour's surveyor's fees, unless the work is to a shared structure that is in disrepair.
3.  **Schedule of Condition:** Even if the neighbour consents, tell your client to hire a surveyor (approx. £500–£800) to take photos and notes of the neighbour's house. This prevents the neighbour from claiming that pre-existing cracks were caused by your client's extension.
4.  **No Retrospective Power:** The Act cannot be used retrospectively. If your client starts digging without notice, the neighbour can obtain a **legal injunction** to stop the works, which is extremely expensive and will halt the project for months.
5.  **Section 10(4) Warning:** If the neighbour refuses to appoint a surveyor during a dispute, your client can eventually appoint one on their behalf to ensure the process moves forward.

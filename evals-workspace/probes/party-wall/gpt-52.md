Yes—quite possibly. For a single‑storey rear extension within **3 metres** of the neighbour’s building, the relevant trigger under the **Party Wall etc. Act 1996 (“the Act”)** is usually **excavation**, not the fact it’s close in plan.

## 1) When a Party Wall Notice is required

### A. Excavations near a neighbouring building (most common for extensions)
You must serve notice under **s.6** of the Act if you intend to:

1. **Excavate within 3 metres** of an adjoining owner’s building/structure **and** go **deeper than the bottom of their foundations** (**s.6(1)**), or  
2. **Excavate within 6 metres** and your excavation intersects a 45° line drawn down from the bottom of their foundations (**s.6(2)**).

In your scenario (“within 3 metres of the neighbour’s building”), a notice is needed **only if** the extension foundations (or any other excavation for drains/soakaways, etc.) will be **deeper than the neighbour’s foundation level**.

Practical point: Many older houses have relatively shallow foundations; modern extension foundations can be deeper—so in practice **s.6 is frequently triggered**. You normally need a basic understanding of:
- proposed foundation type and depth (e.g., strip/trench fill, piles), and  
- likely depth of the neighbour’s foundations (often unknown until investigation).

### B. Works directly to a party wall/party fence wall
A separate notice is required if the extension involves works to an existing **party wall** (the wall separating the two houses) or a **party fence wall** (a boundary wall astride the line of junction) under **s.2** (works to existing party structures) and **s.3** (notice requirement).

Common examples:
- cutting into a party wall to insert beams/flashings;
- raising/thickening the party wall;
- demolishing/rebuilding a defective party fence wall astride the boundary.

### C. Building at or on the boundary (line of junction)
If the extension wall is to be built:
- **astride** the boundary, or
- **up to** the boundary line,

then **s.1** applies (new wall on line of junction) and notice is required. Building **astride** the boundary requires the neighbour’s **express consent**.

So: being within 3m alone doesn’t automatically trigger the Act, but **excavations almost often do**.

## 2) Which notice, and minimum notice periods

- **Excavation Notice (s.6)**: must be served **at least 1 month** before the excavation/works start.  
- **Party Structure Notice (s.3, for s.2 works)**: **at least 2 months** before the works start.  
- **Line of Junction Notice (s.1)**: **at least 1 month** before works start.

(Those are the statutory minimums—real‑world programmes usually need longer.)

## 3) Contents and service (what to include)
For **s.6 excavation** notice, you should include (in practice, as drawings) the information required by **s.6(5)**, including:
- the building owner’s proposals;
- **plans and sections** showing the site and depth of excavation and foundations relative to the adjoining owner’s structure; and
- the intended start date.

Notices must be served on all relevant **“adjoining owners”** (freeholder and any leaseholders with leases exceeding a year are commonly treated as owners for Act purposes). Service rules are in **s.15**.

## 4) Process after service (consent vs dispute) and timeline

### Step 1: Serve notice(s)
Serve as early as possible once drawings/foundation approach are known.

### Step 2: Neighbour has 14 days to respond
Under the Act, the adjoining owner has **14 days** to:
- **consent** in writing; or
- **dissent** and appoint a surveyor; or
- do nothing (which is treated as dissent after the statutory steps below).

If they **consent**, there is **no “dispute”** and no Party Wall Award is required, though it is still sensible to agree a **schedule of condition** (photos and written record) to reduce later arguments.

### Step 3: If dissent (or no reply), surveyor(s) are appointed
If they dissent, the parties can:
- each appoint a surveyor, or
- agree on **one “Agreed Surveyor”**.

If the neighbour does not respond within 14 days, the building owner can issue a further request to appoint a surveyor; if they still do not, the building owner can appoint a surveyor on their behalf under **s.10(4)** after a further **10 days** (this is the usual “14 days + 10 days” pathway used in practice where someone ignores the notice).

### Step 4: Party Wall Award
Surveyor(s) then make an **Award** under **s.10**, typically covering:
- what work can be done and how (method statements, hours, access);
- protective measures;
- schedule of condition of the neighbour’s property;
- making good damage/compensation;
- surveyors’ fees (usually paid by the building owner).

**Typical time** to an Award: often **2–8+ weeks**, depending on neighbour cooperation and complexity.

### Step 5: Right of appeal
Either owner can appeal an Award within **14 days** of service of the Award (**s.10(17)**). Works can often proceed while an appeal window runs, but that is risk‑sensitive—surveyors and solicitors commonly advise caution.

### Step 6: Commencement deadline
Once notice is served, the works must be commenced within **12 months** of the notice date (commonly referenced as the Act’s commencement limit).

## 5) Practical conveyancing advice for your buyer client

1. **Do not exchange** on the assumption permitted development/planning/building control equals Party Wall compliance. It’s separate.
2. Ask the client’s designer/engineer early for:
   - foundation type and depth,
   - whether any drains/soakaways will be within 3m and deeper than neighbour’s foundations.
3. Advise budgeting for **surveyors’ fees** (often the building owner pays both sides if two surveyors are appointed).
4. Encourage early, friendly engagement with the neighbour—consent can save weeks and cost.
5. If the extension is close but the foundation depth is uncertain, assume **s.6 is likely** and plan the programme accordingly (serve notice early once you have sufficient detail).

## Bottom line for your scenario
- **If the extension involves excavating within 3m and deeper than the neighbour’s foundations, a Party Wall Excavation Notice under s.6 is required (minimum 1 month).**
- If the works also affect a **party wall** or the boundary line, **additional notices** (s.3/s.1) may be needed.
- Timeline: **serve notice → 14 days response → (if no consent) surveyor appointment pathway (14 + 10 days where ignored) → Award commonly 2–8+ weeks → 14‑day appeal window.**

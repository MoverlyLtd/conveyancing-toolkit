For a **£400,000 purchase** with a **95% LTV mortgage**, the figures are:

- **Purchase price:** £400,000
- **5% deposit:** £20,000
- **95% mortgage:** £380,000

If the contract requires a **standard 10% deposit on exchange**, that would normally be:

- **10% deposit on exchange:** **£40,000**

Your client only has **£20,000**, so they are **£20,000 short** if the seller insists on a full 10% exchange deposit.

## Main options

### 1) Negotiate a reduced deposit on exchange
This is the most common solution.

In practice, many sellers will accept a **5% exchange deposit** where the buyer is funding the balance through a 95% mortgage, especially if the buyer’s solicitor explains:
- the mortgage offer is in place,
- the deposit is the entire available cash,
- and the seller is still protected by the contractual remedies available if the buyer defaults.

So instead of £40,000, the parties may agree to exchange on **£20,000**.

#### Practical point
This should be agreed **before exchange** and reflected in the contract. If the seller wants the standard contractual deposit provision retained, your solicitor may need to ask for a special condition limiting the exchange deposit to £20,000.

---

### 2) Use the mortgage advance to make up the rest at completion
This is often misunderstood.

The **mortgage funds are not usually available at exchange**; they are paid on completion. So the client cannot normally “use the mortgage” to increase the exchange deposit. But if the deal is structured with a reduced exchange deposit, the mortgage advance plus the deposit will complete the purchase.

---

### 3) Ask whether the 10% is only a contractual default position, not a hard requirement
In many transactions, the “10% deposit on exchange” is the **usual rule**, but it is not always absolute. The seller can agree to accept less.

This is especially common in:
- new-build transactions,
- chain-free sales,
- transactions involving low-deposit buyers,
- and where the lender’s loan-to-value means the buyer cannot physically fund 10%.

---

### 4) Bridging or short-term family loan
If the seller will not reduce the exchange deposit, the buyer could cover the shortfall with:
- a short-term loan from family,
- an unsecured loan,
- or bridging finance.

But this needs caution because:
- any extra borrowing must be acceptable to the mortgage lender,
- it may affect affordability or mortgage conditions,
- and the client should not take on debt without confirming it will not breach the mortgage offer.

---

### 5) Gifts from family
If the client’s shortfall is being met by a family gift, that is often acceptable for the deposit and may be preferable to borrowing.

However:
- the lender will usually need the **gifted deposit evidence**,
- a gift letter,
- and confirmation the money is non-repayable and gives no interest in the property.

---

## Important legal point on exchange deposits
The standard contract position under the **Law Society Standard Conditions of Sale (5th edition)** is that the buyer pays the deposit on exchange, usually **10% of the purchase price**, unless otherwise agreed. The deposit is then held as security for completion.

If your client exchanges with only £20,000, the contract should expressly state that the deposit is **£20,000**, not 10%.

If completion later fails due to buyer default, the seller’s recovery may be limited to that reduced deposit unless the contract provides otherwise.

---

## Best practical route
For a client in this position, the usual advice is:

1. **Tell the seller’s solicitor immediately** that only £20,000 is available.
2. **Ask to exchange with a reduced deposit of £20,000**.
3. Ensure the **mortgage offer is in place** and the completion funds will be ready.
4. If the seller resists, explore:
   - family gift,
   - short-term funds,
   - or re-negotiation of contract terms.

---

## Bottom line
On a **£400,000 purchase**, the standard 10% exchange deposit is **£40,000**, but with only **£20,000 available**, the realistic options are:

- **negotiate a reduced exchange deposit of £20,000**, or
- **source the extra £20,000** via gift/loan/bridging if the seller insists on 10%.

In practice, **a reduced deposit is usually the cleanest solution** for a 95% LTV buyer.

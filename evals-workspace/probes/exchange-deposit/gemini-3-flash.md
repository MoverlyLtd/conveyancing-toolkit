This is a common scenario in high-LTV (Loan to Value) lending. While the standard contractual deposit is 10%, the reality is that many first-time buyers cannot provide this upfront.

Here are the specific options, legal references, and practical steps to resolve this.

### 1. Negotiate a 5% Deposit on Exchange
The most common solution is to vary the contract to allow a 5% deposit. Under the **Standard Conditions of Sale (5th Edition – 2018 Revision)**, specifically **SCS 2.2.1**, the deposit is fixed at 10% of the purchase price. To change this, you must insert a **Special Condition** in the contract.

*   **The Figures:** You pay **£20,000** on exchange instead of **£40,000**.
*   **The Risk/Advice:** You must advise your client that even if the seller accepts 5% on exchange, the client remains liable for the **full 10%** if they fail to complete. Under **SCS 7.1.1**, if the buyer defaults, the seller can claim the remaining 5% (£20,000) as a debt.
*   **Practical Step:** Draft a Special Condition: *"The Seller agrees to accept a deposit of £20,000 on exchange of contracts, provided that the Buyer's liability shall remain 10% of the purchase price (£40,000) in the event of the Buyer's breach of contract."*

### 2. Check the Chain (The "Passing Up" Rule)
If your client is at the bottom of a chain, you need to check the requirements of the parties further up.
*   **Standard Condition 2.2.5** allows a seller who is also buying a property to use a deposit received on their sale as the deposit for their purchase. 
*   **The Problem:** If your client's seller needs a full 10% (£40,000) for their own purchase further up the chain, they may refuse your client's 5% (£20,000) because it leaves them with a £20,000 shortfall.
*   **Practical Advice:** Contact the seller’s solicitor immediately to see if the seller is in a chain and if a 5% deposit is acceptable to the parties above.

### 3. Simultaneous Exchange and Completion
If the seller refuses a 5% deposit and the client cannot bridge the gap, you can suggest "Instant Exchange and Completion" (or a very short gap, e.g., 24 hours).
*   **How it works:** Because the mortgage funds (£380,000) arrive on the day of completion, the "deposit" becomes less relevant as a security measure. The full £400,000 is transferred at once.
*   **The Risk:** This is high-stress for the client. There is no legal certainty until the day of moving, and if the mortgage funds are delayed, the client has no "protection" from an exchange period.

### 4. Deposit Guarantee Indemnity
Though less common now, some providers offer a "Deposit Guarantee Bond." 
*   **How it works:** An insurance company issues a bond to the seller for the 10% value. The buyer pays a small premium. 
*   **The Figures:** The buyer would pay a premium (e.g., £200–£400) to "insure" the £40,000 deposit.
*   **Practical Advice:** This is rarely used in standard residential conveyancing today because most sellers prefer a cash deposit or a negotiated 5% via Special Condition.

### 5. Disclosure to the Lender (Crucial)
You must check the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook (Section 5.10.1)**. 
*   **Requirement:** Most lenders do not mind if the deposit on exchange is less than 10%, provided the total "cash" contribution from the buyer (the equity) remains as stated in the mortgage offer.
*   **Specific Advice:** You must ensure the client is not borrowing the *remaining* 5% from a third party (like a high-interest loan) without lender consent. If the £20,000 is all they have, and the lender is providing 95%, you must ensure the "Source of Funds" documentation matches the 5% (£20,000) figure exactly.

### Practical Summary for the Solicitor:
1.  **Contact the Seller's Solicitor:** Ask if a 5% deposit (£20,000) is acceptable. Most will say yes unless the chain above is difficult.
2.  **Draft Special Condition:** Explicitly state the deposit is 5% but the liability remains 10%.
3.  **Client Advice Letter:** Explicitly warn the client in writing: *"If you fail to complete through no fault of the seller, you will lose your £20,000 and be legally sued for a further £20,000 to bring the total to 10%, plus interest and costs."*
4.  **Check Mortgage Offer:** Ensure the offer reflects a 95% LTV and that you are not required by a specific lender condition to hold a full 10% on exchange (this is rare for 95% LTV products).

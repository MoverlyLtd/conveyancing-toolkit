In UK conveyancing, a 10% deposit on exchange is common but **not legally mandatory** unless the contract requires it. The key is managing (1) the contractual deposit position and (2) the buyer’s exposure if they default after exchange.

## 1) Negotiate a reduced deposit on exchange (most common)
### How it works
Ask the seller to accept **£20,000 (5%)** as the **deposit payable on exchange**, instead of 10%.

### Contract mechanics / references
- On the **Law Society Standard Conditions of Sale (5th ed.) (“SCS”)**, the “Deposit” is **10%** unless the contract provides otherwise (the deposit figure is inserted in the **contract particulars**).
- If less than 10% is paid, ensure the contract is amended so that:
  - the **“deposit” is defined as £20,000**, not 10%; and
  - there is no separate obligation to “top up” later.
- If you leave the deposit as 10% but only pay 5%, you risk arguments about **unpaid deposit** being immediately due and/or the seller treating it as breach.

### Practical advice (buyer risk)
If the buyer exchanges with only 5% paid and then defaults, the seller’s normal remedy is to **forfeit the deposit actually paid** (here, £20,000) and claim additional losses if any (difference on resale, costs etc.). Sellers often accept 5% if they understand the buyer’s LTV position and the chain position.

### Chain point
If there is an onward chain and the seller needs 10% to pass up, the seller may resist. But it’s still frequently workable if the seller can also negotiate a reduced deposit with *their* seller.

## 2) Use a “deposit guarantee” / deposit bond (rare in residential, but possible)
A deposit guarantee is effectively an insurance/bond undertaking to the seller for the balance up to 10% if the buyer defaults. In practice:
- **Uncommon** in mainstream residential conveyancing.
- Sellers (and their solicitors) often refuse.
- Costs can be material; availability depends on provider and buyer’s circumstances.

If you explore this route, ensure the seller’s solicitor accepts it **in writing pre-exchange** and that the guarantee form is acceptable to them.

## 3) Borrow the extra £20,000 (only if lender allows and it’s compliant)
To make up the “traditional” 10%, the buyer could borrow **£20,000** from:
- family (but then it’s a gift/loan issue), or
- unsecured borrowing (personal loan/credit), or
- further advance/second charge (unlikely at 95% LTV).

### Key practical warning
Most mortgage lenders require the deposit to be from the buyer’s own resources and/or an acceptable gift. If it is borrowed, it can:
- breach mortgage conditions,
- trigger lender refusal, and
- create disclosure obligations.

If family funds are involved:
- If it’s a **gift**, you’ll need a **gifted deposit letter**, ID/AML on donor, and evidence of source of funds.
- If it’s a **loan**, many lenders will not accept it (or will treat it as a commitment affecting affordability). You must check the specific lender’s requirements in the UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook (Part 1 plus lender’s Part 2).

## 4) Exchange with no/nominal deposit (possible but seller-dependent)
You can negotiate a **£0** or **£1** deposit on exchange, but:
- it gives the seller minimal security and is often refused unless the seller is highly motivated or there is a very short gap between exchange and completion.
- chain sellers often can’t do it because they need money to exchange onward.

If agreed, it must be clearly stated in the contract particulars.

## 5) “Simultaneous exchange and completion” (or very short gap)
If the seller won’t accept a reduced deposit but the buyer genuinely cannot raise more:
- **Simultaneous exchange and completion** removes the need for an exchange deposit in practice (because completion happens immediately).
- Alternatively, exchange with a very short completion period (e.g. 48–72 hours) can make a seller more comfortable with a 5% deposit.

This can be hard in a chain and may not suit removal/notice timings, but it’s a real option.

## 6) Agree a contractual term that the buyer will pay the 10% only if required (not ideal)
Sometimes solicitors propose wording where 5% is paid on exchange and the remainder becomes payable only on default. Sellers may ask for this. Be careful:
- If the contract still states the deposit is 10%, seller may argue the balance is due immediately.
- If you create a clause making the remaining £20,000 payable on default, you’ve effectively increased the buyer’s exposure beyond the cash paid (and you must be confident it’s enforceable and understood).

Usually cleaner to set the deposit at **£20,000** and leave the seller to ordinary damages if the buyer defaults.

In UK conveyancing, a loft conversion without a Building Regulations Completion Certificate is a high-risk issue. Because the work was completed in 2019, it falls outside the standard 12-month enforcement window for some actions but remains subject to others.

Here are the specific implications and remedies under the law of England and Wales.

---

### 1. The Legal and Practical Implications

#### A. Statutory Enforcement (Building Act 1984)
*   **Section 36 Notices:** Under **Section 36(1) of the Building Act 1984**, a Local Authority (LA) can serve a notice requiring the owner to pull down or rectify work that contravenes regulations. However, this notice must be served within **12 months** of the completion of the work. Since the work was done in 2019, the LA is now "out of time" to serve a standard Section 36 notice.
*   **Injunctions:** Under **Section 36(6)**, the LA can apply to the High Court for an injunction to remove non-compliant work. There is **no time limit** for this. While rare, it is used if the structure is deemed imminently dangerous.
*   **Section 35 Prosecution:** The LA can prosecute for a breach of regulations in the Magistrates' Court. The time limit is usually 2 years from completion (under the Building Safety Act 2022 amendments), meaning this risk has likely also expired for a 2019 build.

#### B. Mortgage Lender Requirements
Most lenders follow the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook**. Section 5.10.1 states that the conveyancer must ensure all necessary Building Regulations approvals were obtained.
*   If there is no completion certificate, the lender may refuse to provide a mortgage unless an **indemnity policy** is in place or a **Regularisation Certificate** is obtained.
*   **Valuation:** The surveyor may value the property as a "two-bedroom house with a loft room" rather than a "three-bedroom house." This can significantly lower the valuation.

#### C. Safety and Structural Integrity
Without a certificate, there is no proof that:
*   The floor joists are strong enough to support the new load.
*   Fire doors and smoke alarms are correctly installed (Part B of the Regulations).
*   The stairs meet "Headroom" and "Pitch" requirements (Part K).
*   Thermal insulation meets standards (Part L).

---

### 2. Remedies

#### Remedy A: Building Regulations Indemnity Insurance (Most Common)
This is the standard "quick fix" used in conveyancing to allow the sale to proceed.
*   **Cost:** Typically **£180 to £500**, depending on the property value. It is usually paid for by the Seller.
*   **What it covers:** Legal costs and loss in value if the Local Authority takes enforcement action.
*   **What it does NOT cover:** It does **not** guarantee the loft is safe, and it does **not** cover the cost of fixing structural defects.
*   **Crucial Condition:** The policy becomes **void** if the Buyer or their solicitor contacts the Local Authority to ask about the lack of building regs.

#### Remedy B: Retrospective Regularisation Certificate
Under **Regulation 18 of the Building (Local Authority Charges) Regulations 2010**, a owner can apply for a "Regularisation Certificate."
*   **Process:** The LA building control officer visits the site. They will likely require "opening up" works (e.g., cutting holes in ceilings to see joists or stripping plasterboard to check fire proofing).
*   **Cost:** Usually the standard building control fee plus a **50% uplift** (approx. **£800–£1,500** total).
*   **Risk:** If the work is non-compliant, the LA will issue a list of required remedial works. The indemnity insurance option is lost the moment the LA is contacted.

#### Remedy C: Structural Engineer’s Report
The buyer can hire a structural engineer to inspect the loft.
*   **Cost:** **£400–£700**.
*   **Benefit:** This provides practical peace of mind regarding safety, which indemnity insurance does not offer. It does not, however, solve the legal lack of paperwork for the lender.

---

### 3. Practical Advice

#### For the Seller:
1.  **Do not contact the Council.** If you do, you cannot provide the buyer with indemnity insurance, which will likely kill the sale.
2.  **Offer Indemnity.** Propose to pay for an indemnity policy at the outset to satisfy the buyer's lender.
3.  **Price Adjustment.** Be prepared for the buyer to negotiate the price down if the loft cannot be legally classed as a "habitable room" (bedroom).

#### For the Buyer:
1.  **Surveyor Instruction:** Explicitly tell your RICS surveyor that there is no completion certificate. Ask them to check for "telltale" signs of DIY loft conversions (e.g., bouncy floors, steep stairs, lack of fire doors).
2.  **Check the "Bedroom" status:** Ensure your mortgage offer isn't contingent on it being a bedroom if the surveyor flags it as a "storage room."
3.  **The "Safety vs. Paperwork" distinction:** Understand that an **Indemnity Policy** protects your bank account from the Council, but a **Regularisation Certificate** protects your life from fire or structural collapse. If you plan to live there long-term, demand the latter or a price reduction to cover the cost of making it compliant.

**Summary Recommendation:** If the loft appears structurally sound, the most pragmatic route is for the Seller to pay for **Indemnity Insurance** and the Buyer to commission a **Full Building Survey (Level 3)** to mitigate physical risk.

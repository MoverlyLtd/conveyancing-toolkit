A loft conversion completed in 2019 **without a Building Regulations completion certificate** and **not carried out under a competent person scheme** means there is **no formal evidence that the works were approved and inspected by Building Control**. In UK conveyancing, that usually raises two separate issues:

1. **Building Regulations compliance**
2. **Planning permission** — which may or may not also be relevant, depending on the size/extent of the conversion

## 1) Main implication: lack of Building Regulations sign-off

If the seller has no completion certificate, the local authority has not issued confirmation that the works complied with the Building Regulations 2010.

For a loft conversion, the key technical areas usually include:
- structural adequacy of joists/rafters/steelwork
- stair design and means of escape
- fire safety, including fire doors, smoke alarms, and protected escape route
- insulation and energy efficiency
- ventilation
- glazing/guarding
- load-bearing calculations

### Legal/conveyancing risk
Without a completion certificate:
- the buyer cannot be sure the works are compliant
- the lender may require evidence or indemnity insurance
- the local authority could, in theory, take enforcement action if the works are non-compliant

## 2) Enforcement position: time limits matter, but not completely risk-free

For most Building Regulations breaches, the local authority’s main enforcement route is under **section 36 of the Building Act 1984**.

### Section 36 Building Act 1984
A local authority can require removal or alteration of non-compliant work, but this is subject to the usual **12-month limit** from completion of the works for taking section 36 proceedings.

Because the loft was done in **2019**, that 12-month window is almost certainly long gone.

### Practical meaning
- **No routine Building Control enforcement** if more than 12 months has passed
- But the lack of certificate still matters for sale, mortgage, and safety
- Also, if the works are dangerous, the council can still use other powers in some circumstances, and civil liability issues may still arise if something later fails

## 3) Planning permission may be separate

A loft conversion can be:
- permitted development, or
- require planning permission, depending on roof alterations, dormers, volume increase, roof height changes, materials, etc.

If there is no Building Regulations certificate, that does **not** automatically mean there was no planning permission, and vice versa.

### What to check
- copy of planning permission, if any
- whether the loft qualified under permitted development
- whether a **Lawful Development Certificate** exists

If planning permission was required and never obtained, the usual enforcement time limit is generally **4 years** for operational development under **section 171B of the Town and Country Planning Act 1990**.

Since the work was done in 2019, that may also now be time-barred if it was completed more than 4 years ago, but you should verify dates carefully.

## 4) No competent person scheme: what that means

If the work was **not done under a competent person scheme**, there will be no scheme installer certificate to rely on. That means:
- no self-certification route was used
- the buyer must rely on Building Control approval, indemnity, retrospective regularisation, or survey evidence

This is common for structural loft work: many elements are **not** eligible for competent person scheme certification anyway, especially the structural conversion itself.

Examples of competent person schemes mainly cover items like:
- electrical work
- gas work
- windows/doors
- some ventilation or heating installations

A loft conversion usually still needs Building Regulations approval for the overall works, even if some sub-elements could be self-certified separately.

## 5) Remedies available to the buyer/seller

### A. Retrospective Building Regulations regularisation
The seller can apply to the local authority for a **regularisation certificate** under **regulation 18 of the Building Regulations 2010**.

This is the most direct remedy if the council is willing to inspect.

#### What happens
The local authority may:
- inspect the works
- ask for opening up of concealed areas
- require calculations or reports
- require remedial works if standards are not met

#### Pros
- can give proper formal compliance evidence
- better than insurance alone
- often preferred by lenders

#### Cons
- may involve invasive works
- no guarantee the council will issue the certificate
- can take time and cost money

### B. Indemnity insurance
A common conveyancing solution is **lack of Building Regulations indemnity insurance**.

#### Important limits
This does **not** make the loft compliant.
It only covers financial risk if the local authority takes enforcement action or if a lender/resale issue arises in relation to the missing certificate.

#### Typical conditions
Insurers often require:
- no approach has been made to Building Control about the issue
- no regularisation application has been made
- no enforcement notices have been received
- the seller/buyer has not disclosed the defect to the council before completion

If you apply for regularisation, you usually **kill the chance of indemnity insurance**.

#### Practical advice
If you want insurance, **do not contact the council first** until your solicitor has checked the policy position.

### C. Structural survey / engineer’s report
A chartered structural engineer can inspect and report on:
- floor loading
- roof support
- stair structure
- adequacy of alterations

This does **not** replace Building Control approval, but it helps:
- reassure the buyer
- support mortgage negotiations
- identify hidden defects

### D. Price retention / completion holdback
If the buyer is concerned, the parties may agree:
- a retention from the purchase price
- released once regularisation or remedial work is done

This is more common where a lender is involved and the issue is material.

## 6) Lender implications

Mortgage lenders often require:
- a completion certificate, or
- regularisation certificate, or
- indemnity insurance

If the loft is a major part of the value or bedroom count, the lender may worry about:
- structural risk
- fire safety
- whether the room can really be treated as habitable space

If the room is marketed as a bedroom but lacks sign-off, the valuer may:
- downvalue the property
- exclude the loft room from bedroom count
- require a retention or evidence

## 7) Fire safety is often the biggest substantive issue

For loft conversions, the most common compliance problems are:
- lack of protected stair route
- insufficient fire doors to rooms opening onto the escape route
- inadequate smoke detection
- insufficient headroom at stair
- unsuitable window escape route

If the conversion created an extra storey or altered escape arrangements, the absence of inspection is more serious than in a simple cosmetic alteration.

## 8) Practical conveyancing approach

### If acting for buyer
Ask for:
1. copies of any planning permission / lawful development certificate
2. any Building Control correspondence
3. details of the contractor
4. any engineer’s calculations
5. current and pre-works photos if available
6. indemnity insurance quote
7. whether the seller is willing to seek regularisation

Then decide whether to:
- proceed with indemnity only
- insist on regularisation
- renegotiate price
- walk away if the risk is unacceptable

### If acting for seller
Best options are:
- locate any old Building Control documents or final inspection notes
- obtain a structural engineer’s report
- offer indemnity insurance
- if time allows and the works are likely compliant, consider regularisation

But remember:
- once you approach the council about regularisation, indemnity insurance may no longer be available
- full disclosure is essential if asked in the Property Information Form

## Bottom line

Because the loft conversion was done in **2019**:
- the usual **section 36 Building Act 1984** Building Regulations enforcement route is likely time-barred
- but the lack of completion certificate still matters for **mortgage, resale, and safety**
- the main remedies are:
  1. **regularisation certificate** under **regulation 18 of the Building Regulations 2010**
  2. **indemnity insurance**
  3. **structural engineer’s report**
  4. **price retention/renegotiation**

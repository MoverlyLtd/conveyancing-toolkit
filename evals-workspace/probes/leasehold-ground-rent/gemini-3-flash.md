This is a classic conveyancing issue involving the "AST Trap" and lender criteria. Because the lease was granted in 2015, it predates the recent legislative changes, meaning your client is potentially exposed to significant legal and financial risks.

Below is the specific breakdown of the issues, the impact of the 2022 Act, and practical steps.

---

### 1. The "AST Trap" (Housing Act 1988)
The most immediate issue is **Section 7 of the Housing Act 1988**. 

*   **The Threshold:** Under the Act, if the ground rent exceeds **£250 per year** (or **£1,000 per year in Greater London**), the lease is legally classified as an **Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST)** rather than a long leasehold.
*   **The Risk (Ground 8):** If the lease is an AST and the tenant (your client) falls into at least two months of rent arrears, the landlord has a mandatory right to possession under **Schedule 2, Ground 8** of the Act. 
*   **The Problem:** Unlike a standard lease forfeiture, the court has *no discretion* to grant relief against forfeiture for an AST under Ground 8. A lender’s security could be entirely wiped out by a simple rent debt.
*   **Specific Figures:** Your client’s rent is currently exactly **£250**. 
    *   If the property is **outside London**, it is currently on the brink. The moment it hits £500 (Year 25), it becomes an AST.
    *   If the property is **in London**, it is safe for now, but will hit the £1,000 threshold at the second doubling (Year 50).

### 2. Lender Requirements
Most lenders follow the **UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook**. 
*   **Section 5.14.9** states that ground rent must be "reasonable" and its increase "predictable." 
*   **0.1% Rule:** Many lenders (e.g., Santander, Barclays, Nationwide) refuse to lend if the ground rent exceeds **0.1% of the property’s value**. If the flat is worth £200,000, a £250 ground rent is 0.125%, which may already trigger a decline.
*   **Doubling Clauses:** While "doubling every 25 years" is less "toxic" than doubling every 10 or 15 years, many lenders still require a **Deed of Variation** to cap the rent or link increases to RPI (Retail Price Index) instead.

### 3. The Leasehold Reform (Ground Rent) Act 2022
The most important thing to advise your client is that **this Act does NOT apply retrospectively.**

*   **Effective Date:** 30 June 2022.
*   **Application:** It only applies to **new** long leases granted after this date. 
*   **The Rule:** For new leases, the ground rent must be a "peppercorn" (zero financial value).
*   **Effect on your client:** Since the lease is from 2015, the landlord can continue to charge the £250 and the doubling remains legally enforceable. 
*   **The "Two-Tier" Market:** This Act has created a market disadvantage for your client. Future buyers will prefer "post-2022" flats with zero ground rent, potentially making your client's "doubling" lease harder to sell or forcing a price reduction.

### 4. Practical Advice and Remedies

#### A. Deed of Variation (The Gold Standard)
You should ask the seller to approach the Freeholder to negotiate a **Deed of Variation**. 
*   **Objective:** To cap the ground rent at £250 for the remainder of the term (to prevent it exceeding the AST threshold) or to convert it to a peppercorn.
*   **Cost:** The Freeholder will likely demand a "premium" (a buyout fee) plus legal costs.

#### B. Indemnity Insurance
If the lender is only concerned about the **AST/Ground 8** risk (and not the 0.1% value risk), you can suggest **Forfeiture of Lease (Housing Act) Indemnity Insurance**.
*   **Function:** It protects the lender if the landlord attempts to repossess via Ground 8. 
*   **Limitation:** This does not "fix" the lease; it only provides a financial safety net for the lender. It does not stop the rent from being a burden to the client or a future buyer.

#### C. Statutory Lease Extension (Section 42)
Under the **Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993**, if the client has owned the property for 2 years (though this 2-year rule is being abolished by the 2024 Act, see below), they can force a lease extension.
*   **The Result:** A statutory extension adds 90 years to the lease and **reduces the ground rent to a peppercorn** automatically.
*   **Advice:** Your client could ask the seller to serve the Section 42 notice and assign the benefit to the client upon completion.

#### D. Note on the Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024
This Act received Royal Assent in May 2024 but is not yet fully in force. It aims to:
*   Make it cheaper/easier to extend leases.
*   Potentially cap existing ground rents (though the £250 cap for existing leases was a point of heavy debate and may be subject to further secondary legislation). 
*   **Advice:** Tell the client that while help is coming, they cannot rely on the 2024 Act to fix the issue *before* their purchase completes.

### Summary Recommendation
1.  Check the **property value**. If £250 is > 0.1%, report to the lender immediately.
2.  If **outside London**, insist on a **Deed of Variation** to prevent the rent from doubling to £500, or at the very least, an **Indemnity Policy** if the lender accepts it.
3.  Warn the client that they may face difficulty selling in the future because they are competing with "Peppercorn" leases created after 2022.

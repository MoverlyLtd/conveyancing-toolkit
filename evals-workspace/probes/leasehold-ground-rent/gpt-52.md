### 1) Is the 2015 lease with £250 p.a. doubling every 25 years “an issue”?
**Yes, potentially—mainly for mortgageability/resale and future extension cost/value**, even though it is not automatically unlawful.

Key points:

- **It is not a “peppercorn” rent** and will become **£500 p.a. in 2040**, **£1,000 p.a. in 2065**, etc. That trajectory can make the flat **less attractive to buyers and some lenders** as time passes.
- **Assured tenancy risk (historic concern):** The old concern was that if annual rent exceeded the Housing Act 1988 thresholds, the lease could become an **assured tenancy**, giving a landlord a mandatory possession route on rent arrears.  
  - The relevant figure for Greater London used to be **£1,000 p.a.** (and **£250 p.a. outside London**).  
  - This is why **£250** (especially **outside London**) has often been treated as a red flag by lenders.  
  - **However**, the **Renters’ Rights Bill** (not yet law at the time of writing) proposes abolition of assured tenancies in their current form; until changes are in force, lenders still commonly apply their own policies and the historic risk still drives underwriting.
- **Lender policy:** Many lenders also dislike **doubling ground rent** clauses as a matter of policy even where assured tenancy risk is not in play, because doubling looks “onerous” and can affect valuation/saleability.

**Practical conveyancing take:** treat it as a lending/resale risk issue rather than a “void/illegal” issue. You should check:
- **Where is the flat?** (London vs outside London affects the old assured tenancy threshold logic.)
- **The lender’s UK Finance Mortgage Lenders’ Handbook requirements** for that specific lender (Part 2 is critical—many have explicit ground rent limits/escalation restrictions).
- **Valuer’s comments** (some valuers down-value or refer where doubling terms exist).

### 2) What changed with the Leasehold Reform (Ground Rent) Act 2022?
The **Leasehold Reform (Ground Rent) Act 2022** (“LRGRA 2022”) **largely stopped ground rents on most new long residential leases** by requiring a **peppercorn rent**.

#### (a) Core rule
- For a **“regulated lease”**, the landlord cannot require a monetary ground rent: rent must be **“one peppercorn”** (i.e. effectively **£0**) for the ground rent element.

**Statutory hook:** the Act prohibits charging ground rent above a peppercorn on regulated leases (see **LRGRA 2022, ss. 3–7** (interpretation and effect), with the peppercorn requirement embedded in the regime; enforcement/prohibited rent via **ss. 11–16**).  

#### (b) What leases does it apply to?
It applies to **most new long residential leases** granted on/after commencement (with staged commencement dates):

- **30 June 2022:** main commencement for most new long residential leases.
- **1 April 2023:** commencement extended to **retirement homes**.

**Crucially:** it is **not retrospective**—it does **not** automatically change ground rent in existing leases.

#### (c) Does it affect a 2015 lease?
**No, not directly.** A lease **granted in 2015** is an **existing lease** and the Act does **not** rewrite its rent clause.

However, it can become relevant if there is a **“deemed grant”** of a new lease that falls within the Act, for example:
- **Voluntary surrender and regrant** after commencement (often used as a workaround to vary terms). If the transaction amounts to a **new lease**, it may trigger the peppercorn requirement for ground rent (depending on structure).

But:

- **Statutory lease extensions** under the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993 are generally at **peppercorn ground rent anyway** for the extended term (so the Act doesn’t change that position in practice; it mainly targets “new” leases).

#### (d) Enforcement/penalties (why landlords care)
Where a landlord demands or takes a prohibited ground rent under a regulated lease:
- Trading Standards/local weights and measures authorities can impose financial penalties.  
**LRGRA 2022, ss. 17–24** cover enforcement and penalties (including financial penalty regime and repayment).

### 3) What should you do on this purchase?
**A. Report clearly to the client and lender**
- Provide a **ground rent schedule** (2015–2115 etc.) showing doubling points and amounts.
- Flag lender policy risk and resale risk.

**B. Check if the client needs a mortgage**
- If mortgage-backed, check **that lender’s Part 2** handbook requirements. Some lenders refuse “doubling more frequently than every 20 years”, some require ground rent not to exceed a percentage of value, etc. The 25‑year doubling may or may not pass—depends on lender.

**C. Consider solutions**
1. **Deed of Variation** to replace doubling with:
   - **peppercorn** (best), or
   - **RPI-linked** with a cap, and/or
   - a fixed rent (often discouraged if still high).
   Note: the landlord may demand a premium and costs.

2. **Statutory lease extension** (if eligible—usually requires 2 years’ ownership unless the seller serves and assigns a s.42 notice):
   - Under the 1993 Act route, the extended lease typically has **peppercorn ground rent**.
   - If the buyer needs the benefit immediately, ask the seller to **serve a s.42 notice and assign it on completion** (common conveyancing fix).

3. **Price negotiation**: if variation/extension is needed to satisfy lender, negotiate a retention or price reduction.

**D. If outside London, treat £250 as especially sensitive**
- Many lenders historically treat **£250 p.a.** outside London as a “do not proceed without variation” category due to the assured tenancy concern (even if the real-world risk is debated).

### Bottom line
- **Yes, it can be an issue**: £250 doubling every 25 years is a known “onerous ground rent” pattern that can affect **mortgageability and future sale**, especially (historically) outside London and as it rises over time.
- **LRGRA 2022 did not fix 2015 leases**: it mainly ensures **new long residential leases granted from 30 June 2022 (and retirement from 1 April 2023) must have a peppercorn ground rent**. Existing ground rent clauses generally remain unless varied/extended.
